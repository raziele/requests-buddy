## Context & Background

Approximately 500,000 IDF service members took part in the Iron Swords War; their families—parents, siblings, partners, grandparents—represent about 30% of Israel's population and have been living since October 7th in ongoing worry, emotional overload, and economic and employment strain. While many support services target soldiers, their families have often been left outside support networks, and the security situation is expected to affect them for a long time. Inner Forces was established to fill this gap with a long-term support framework, advancing public policy and building infrastructure. They operate on five pillars: resilience, community support, rights advocacy, employment stability, and education.

In light of the current security situation and escalation of fighting, Inner Forces is experiencing a significant surge of requests from mothers of combat soldiers and wives of reservists. The home has become a frontline; emotional strain and burnout are accumulating. They adapted their annual resilience program into a condensed, rapid-response format: a series of four online resilience sessions per group, up to 20 participants each, led by professional facilitators. Within the first week of Operation "Roaring Lion," they had already initiated three of the planned six groups; participant feedback is strongly positive (e.g., "I feel like someone finally gave me oxygen," "For the first time, I don't feel crazy").

Research conducted with Ruppin Academic Center shows that mothers of combat soldiers experience ambiguous loss, prolonged worry and anxiety, emotional overload, decline in functioning at home and at work, loneliness, and diminished meaning; a supportive peer group was found to be a significant resilience factor. The organization's 2025 Community Resilience Ambassador Program ran four cohorts nationwide with significant improvement in well-being; in 2026 they plan to expand with 10 additional cohorts and 100+ workshops and retreats. Funding for the immediate six groups would allow them to meet the current surge in demand while maintaining their longer-term programs.

### Immediate Resilience Support in Response to the Emergency Situation – March 2026.pdf

**Immediate Resilience Support in Response to the Emergency Situation – March 2026**

Approximately 500,000 IDF service members took part in the Iron Swords War, and their families—parents, siblings, partners, and grandparents—about 30% of Israel's population—have been living since October 7th in an ongoing reality of worry, emotional overload, and economic and employment strain. While many support services have been developed for the soldiers themselves, their families have often been left outside the conversation and support networks, even though the security situation is expected to affect them for a long time to come.

Inner Forces works to change this reality by building a long-term support framework for the families of service members, alongside advancing public policy and developing long-term infrastructure. Based on field work and research, we developed a multidisciplinary model for strengthening the resilience of service members' families, addressing emotional resilience, community, employment, and utilization of rights.

In light of the current security situation and the escalation of fighting in recent days, we are experiencing a significant surge of requests from mothers of combat soldiers and wives of reservists seeking immediate support. The home has become a frontline, and emotional strain and burnout are accumulating. Therefore, we adapted our annual resilience program into a condensed, rapid-response format consisting of a series of four online resilience sessions, each in small groups of up to 20 participants, led by professional facilitators specializing in therapy and group work.

**Funding ask:** We are seeking to open at least six groups: three for mothers of active-duty and reserve combat soldiers, and three for wives of reservists. **Cost per group: 9,150 NIS**, including facilitation, participant coordination, content adaptation, and ongoing support between sessions.

Within the first week of Operation "Roaring Lion," we have already initiated 3 groups out of the planned 6. Participating mothers and spouses shared:
- "I feel like someone finally gave me oxygen. I was suffocating."
- "For the first time, I don't feel crazy."
- "Only other mothers can truly understand me."
- "Thank you for giving me solid ground to stand on - something I haven't experienced in a long time."

**Resilience Sessions – Mothers of Combat Soldiers**
Target Audience: Mothers of soldiers serving in mandatory service, reserves, and career service. Guided, safe group space for processing ongoing worry and uncertainty, with professional tools for emotional regulation and coping with anxiety, burnout, and accumulated stress. Combines psychoeducation (ambiguous loss and resilience) with open group dialogue and exercises; builds supportive peer community.

**Resilience Sessions – Wives of Reservists**
Target Audience: Wives of reservists dealing with active and prolonged reserve duty. Facilitated space for processing emotional strain and burnout from running a household and raising children during a partner's absence, plus ongoing worry and shifts between emergency and routine. Psychoeducation, tools for family resilience and coping with uncertainty, practical exercises; tools for emotional regulation, daily functioning, and couple/family communication. Strong peer community.

**Community Resilience Ambassadors – face-to-face program**
Year-long program with local municipalities for mothers of soldiers (mandatory, reserves, career). Includes 10-session weekly training (3 hours each, up to 20 mothers): emotional processing, tools for personal and family resilience. Trains participants as Community Resilience Ambassadors; after the course, monthly support, professional guidance, case consultation, links to therapeutic and rights-related services. Active community network of trained volunteers. During 2025: four cohorts nationwide, including mothers of injured soldiers and soldiers with PTSD; significant improvement in well-being. 2026 plan: 10 additional cohorts, adapt model for fathers and wives, 100+ workshops and retreats.

**Research (Ruppin Academic Center)**
Study of mothers of combat soldiers and combat-support soldiers: ambiguous loss, extreme and prolonged worry and anxiety, psychological impact including emotional overload, decline in functioning at home and work, loneliness, diminished meaning. Supportive peer group found to be a significant resilience factor.

### Inner Forces ENG..pdf

**Inner Forces (HaNefashot HaPoalot) – Serving the Families Who Serve**

Approximately 500,000 service members took part in the October 7th war, meaning nearly 30% of Israel's population (their families—parents, brothers/sisters, spouses, children, grandparents) have been living for over two years in a state of constant anxiety. While efforts and resources are allocated to support soldiers, their families remain largely overlooked. Inner Forces was established to fill this gap. With public mental health systems and state services overwhelmed, targeted community-based support for families of soldiers is an urgent national necessity.

Inner Forces provides a holistic support framework based on empirical knowledge and academic research. Core belief: when families are resilient, soldiers are resilient. Five core pillars: **Resilience, Rights Advocacy, Community, Economic Stability, and Education.**

Leadership: mothers of combat soldiers with professional backgrounds in healthcare, education, and social innovation.

**1. Resilience Programs – From Concern to Community Action**

Community Resilience Ambassador Program: structured, research-based annual model with local municipalities. For mothers of combat soldiers; year-long professional support, strengthens personal and family resilience, prepares participants as Resilience Ambassadors. Graduates volunteer locally with municipal authorities. Research with Ruppin Academic Center; measurable outcomes. Participants report significant improvement in well-being and resilience.

- 2025: Four cohorts for soldiers' mothers, including mothers of injured soldiers; significant improvement in overall well-being.
- 2026: Launch 10 additional cohorts; adapt model for fathers and wives; 100+ workshops and retreats.

Research with Ruppin: mothers of soldiers in combat and combat-support roles experience ambiguous loss trauma affecting multiple areas including employment; peer support emerged as key resilience factor. Strengthening mothers' and families' resilience contributes to soldiers' resilience.

Participant quotes: "The program helped me release constant tension, gain practical tools, and reconnect. It gave me hope." "We created a supportive community of strong mothers who are always there for each other."

**2. Occupational Rehabilitation & Economic Stability**

Approximately 20% of mothers of combat soldiers report reduced employment or job loss; about 45% of reservists' spouses report changes in employment status during the war. Occupational resilience program: trauma-informed career counseling, employment navigation, guidance on retention, helping participants return to work. 2026: Launch five occupational resilience groups for mothers and reservists' spouses with municipalities and business sector; work with employers on trauma awareness and supporting affected employees.

**3. Rights Advocacy**

Guidance for families navigating bureaucratic systems; comprehensive national resource database. 2026: Implement CRM for secure case management; funding needed for case manager to oversee expanding volunteer network.

**4. Community Building & Local Resilience**

Dynamic nationwide community, online and in-person. 2026: Launch three local communities with municipalities, each led by a trained Community Manager; develop digital platform for rapid access to information, redirection to organizations, mutual support.

**5. Education, Adolescents & Family Cohesion**

Addressing erosion of communication between soldiers and families. 2026: Family communication workshops for 11th–12th graders and their parents before enlistment.
