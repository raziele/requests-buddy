## The Ask

Project Kesher Israel (PKI) is requesting $60,000 to provide emergency support to Russian-speaking Jewish immigrant women and children. The ongoing attacks have placed these women at acute risk due to financial instability, lack of safe spaces, and inability to access vital information. PKI plans to provide emergency-responsive programming and basic needs (food, water, clothing), expanded mental health access (trauma-informed therapy, counseling), and sustained staff response capacity.

**Funding Requested:** $60,000

**Funding Breakdown:**
- Emergency Basic Needs (food, water, clothing/blankets, emergency stipends for 200 women and families): $20,000
- Mental Health Services (individual and group therapy for approx. 300 high-risk women): $30,000
- Emergency Programming & Coordination (Daily Zoom sessions, outreach, safety coordination for 5,000+ women): $10,000

## Context & Background

Project Kesher Israel (PKI) has been supporting Russian-speaking Jewish immigrant women in Israel for over 17 years, providing them with financial literacy, Jewish education, leadership development, language access, and trauma-informed support. Due to the war in Ukraine, PKI's network has expanded significantly with the arrival of over 200,000 immigrants and refugees from Ukraine, Russia, and Belarus.

The current emergency has placed immigrant women in PKI's network at acute risk, particularly due to financial instability. Many live in older buildings without safe rooms, have experienced damage to their homes, and cannot access local news or safety instructions due to language barriers. Public mental health systems are overloaded, and transportation disruptions further isolate these women. Many are also stuck in shelters without essential supplies.

PKI is requesting $60,000 for emergency interventions, including resources for basic needs, expanded mental health access, and sustained staff response capacity. The total program budget is $60,000, with $10,000 raised to date, leaving a remaining need of $50,000.

PROJECT
KESHER

Project Kesher Israel: Supporting Immigrant Women and Children During Crisis

Organizational Background: Founded in 1989, Project Kesher (PK) develops Jewish
women leaders, strengthens pluralist Jewish communities, and advances civil society. In
2008, Project Kesher Israel (PKI) was established to support Russian-speaking Jewish
immigrant women from the post-Soviet states.

For more than 17 years, PKI has helped immigrant women build stable lives in Israel
through financial literacy, Jewish education, leadership development, language access,
and trauma-informed support. Following the arrival of over 200,000 immigrants and
refugees from Ukraine, Russia, and Belarus due to the war in Ukraine, PKI's network
expanded significantly.

Because of its trusted relationships, extensive experience, and established
infrastructure, PKI is able to respond immediately and effectively in times of crisis.

Current Emergency Need: The ongoing attacks across Israel have placed immigrant
women in our network at acute risk, particularly due to financial instability.

• Many live in older buildings without safe rooms and must run repeatedly to public
	shelters or remain in parking garages for hours.
•
11 participants reported shattered windows and structural damage following
nearby rocket explosions; several experienced temporary hearing impairment.
• Immigrant women need Russian and Ukrainian-language briefings, as many do
	not speak Hebrew and cannot follow local news or safety instructions.
•
Women are reporting panic attacks, insomnia, and inability to function under

continued sirens.
• Public mental health systems are currently overloaded and unable to accept new

patients.
•
Women displaced from damaged buildings have been relocated to hotels without

belongings.
• Many are caring for children at home due to school closures.
• Public transportation disruptions further isolate low-income immigrant women.
• Many are stuck in shelters without food, water, warm clothing, chargers, or

reliable cell signal.

It is estimated that 37% of Israeli residents lack safe spaces in their homes; within the
Russian-speaking immigrant community, that percentage is significantly higher due to
economic constraints. Many of the women we serve already live close to the poverty
line. One participant, age 24, wrote: “This is my fourth war in four years.” Without
immediate intervention, acute distress risks becoming long-term trauma.

Program Interventions
Emergency-Responsive Programming & Basic Needs: Resources for women stuck
in shelters, including food, water, and warm clothing/blankets; emergency support for
displaced families; ongoing safety coordination and information sharing; instruction on
accessing available medical care.

Expanded Mental Health Access: Individual trauma-informed therapy, crisis
counseling, and group emotional-support sessions delivered by licensed professionals
serving the Russian- and Ukrainian-speaking community

Sustained Staff Response Capacity: Ensuring our coordinators can continue real-time
communication, Zoom programming (three sessions daily), expert briefings, and
outreach to isolated participants as the situation evolves.

Beneficiaries & Budget
• Emergency Basic Needs – $20,000 ($100 per package, serves 200 women and
families with food, water, clothing/blankets, or small emergency stipends)

• Mental Health Services – $30,000 (Individual and group therapy, serves
approximately 300 high-risk women, some receiving multiple sessions)

• Emergency Programming & Coordination – $10,000 (Daily Zoom sessions,
outreach, and safety coordination, reaching our network of 5,000+ women
virtually)

Total Program Budget: $60,000
