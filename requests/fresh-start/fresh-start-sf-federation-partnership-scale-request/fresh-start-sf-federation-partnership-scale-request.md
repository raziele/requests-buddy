## The Ask

Fresh Start (Daf Chadash) is an Israeli nonprofit that runs short-term economic recovery and resilience programs for middle-class families and small businesses in financial crisis. Since the start of the war it has assisted hundreds of families and businesses (mainly reservists) through a nationwide network of lawyers, family-economy consultants, and business consultants offering holistic support: debt management, rights realization, responsible credit, employment guidance, and complementary rehabilitation services. The program is funded by the Ministry of Economy, US Jewish Federations (New York, Los Angeles, Chicago, Philadelphia), Schusterman Foundation, and other major funders. The organization is now working to scale the program and embed it as a mainstream public service and is requesting the San Francisco Federation to join as a partner in this strategic move.

**Funding Requested:** Not specified

**Non-Financial Ask:** Request to coordinate a short call to explore partnership and collaboration; no specific funding amount requested in the materials.

## Context & Background

Since the outbreak of the war, a large share of Israeli middle-class households—roughly a third—have faced accelerated economic crisis: prolonged reserve duty, loss of income for employees and self-employed, and the end of temporary relief mechanisms have left working families with sustained debt and instability. These families often do not qualify for welfare safety nets or municipal discounts but cannot absorb prolonged economic shocks on their own. Without adequate systemic solutions they repeatedly fall into the trap of consumer credit. Fresh Start frames the problem as systemic: a system that allows rapid entry into debt and even dependence on credit for daily living but does not provide a structured path out or toward economic recovery and stability.

Fresh Start operates a holistic, multidisciplinary model combining financial and household coaching, business and employment counseling, legal advice, debt settlement support, rights realization, responsible credit access, and stronger financial management for both the business and the household. The intervention is short and intensive (about 10 sessions over roughly 3 months), with in-depth assessment, a tailored action plan, cash-flow stabilization, and removal of barriers to recovery. The model has already helped hundreds of families and businesses exit crisis; evaluation reports cite a 108% median increase in business income and an 18% increase in household income for those in insolvency proceedings, alongside debt reduction and improved financial self-efficacy.

The current moment is described as a unique window to embed this economic recovery model in public systems—banks, government ministries, and local authorities—so that anyone in financial difficulty can receive professional, disinterested advice in "real time" and avoid mistakes that deepen their situation. Pilots have been launched with the Ministry of Economy, the Jerusalem municipality, and others, and the organization is working to integrate the program into the banking sector and additional municipalities and ministries. Scaling requires both expanding internal infrastructure and policy/communications work to raise awareness and advance the issue on the public agenda as part of the broader struggle against cost of living in Israel.

The program has successfully assisted hundreds of participants since the start of the war and now aims to embed it as an integral service in public systems (local authorities, government ministries, banking system).

Short, intensive process (10 sessions over ~3 months): assessment, action plan, cash-flow stabilization, decisions, barrier removal. Target: working populations with potential for rapid recovery where timely intervention prevents deterioration.

**Current need:** To make the model systemic, systematic integration into public systems (banking, government, local authorities) is required so anyone in difficulty can get professional, disinterested advice in "real time." Scaling requires expanding infrastructure and policy/communications to raise awareness and advance the issue on the public agenda.

**Results cited:** Since the war, support to nearly 500 families and small businesses (mainly reservists): (a) significant income increase (108% for businesses, 18% for households in insolvency); (b) debt reduction and settlement (15% without collection proceedings, full payment compliance in insolvency); (c) sustained household balance; (d) improved financial self-efficacy. ### סיכום מחקר ראיונות עומק - מענקי מילואים - יולי 2025.pdf

**Title:** Evaluation research for the business accompaniment initiative of 'Fresh Start' for reservists with businesses mobilized for "Iron Swords" and their families. In-depth interview findings. Authors: Moshe Cohen, Dr. Yuval Paldi. July 2025.

**Summary:** High satisfaction with business accompaniment and with the grant. The emotional aspect of the consultant’s work was seen as important, often central to effective accompaniment, especially given the complex period and the fact that clients were reservists moving between war and civilian life or partners of reservists. Willingness to recommend Fresh Start was high, as was willingness to donate in the future when possible. Willingness to take a conditional loan instead of a grant was low, mainly due to fear of additional debt. Two business owners reported a significant positive turnaround; others reported that despite good accompaniment, meaningful business results were not yet evident, sometimes citing full responsibility on their side (e.g., not yet able to engage emotionally or to implement the consultant’s advice).

**Recommendations:** Strengthen the interface between the organization and clients (designated contact in addition to the consultant); reduce uncertainty regarding the process and grant; shorten time from grant application to response; align expectations before starting to avoid underuse of the accompaniment; consider revisiting charging for accompaniment (detailed in recommendations chapter).

**Program:** Up to 10 meetings with a business consultant at no cost; grants of up to 40,000 ₪. 8 in-depth interviews with reservists or their partners who received accompaniment and grant in 2024 or early 2025.

**Findings (abbreviated):** Character of businesses and entry into difficulty (small businesses, diverse sectors; difficulty often due to mobilization and business halt). Trigger for seeking help (combination of business and emotional difficulty). How they found Fresh Start (mainly WhatsApp groups, social networks, including spouses of reservists; personal referral also mentioned). Business accompaniment (topics: income/expense, separation of business/household, budgeting, credit, marketing, pricing; emotional support highlighted as very important; average rating for accompaniment 9.1; for grant impact 8.8). Grant amounts (20,000 ₪ or 40,000 ₪); most invested in the business per plan; some said the amount was insufficient. Suggestions for improvement: expectation alignment, follow-up after accompaniment, physical presence at grant committee, reduced uncertainty and shorter approval timelines, possibility of specialist meetings, fewer forms, direct contact with the organization, discussion of charging for accompaniment.

### פרויקט הליווי העסקי של דף חדש - דוח הערכה מסכם 2.1.2025.pdf

**Title:** Business accompaniment project of Fresh Start for businesses affected by the 'Iron Swords' war. Summary evaluation report – December 2024. Dr. Yuval Paldi, Anuva Shabtai, Moshe Cohen – Makhon Tvunot.

**Summary:** The project aimed to help business owners whose income was affected by the war through meetings with a business consultant to strengthen them personally and find solutions to return the business to growth. The project (ending at time of report) began January 2024; 130 business owners participated to date. Evaluation examined effectiveness along three axes: personal (feelings, stress, optimism, proactivity), business (solutions to main challenges), and financial (income, debt payments, total debt). 27 business owners who completed accompaniment between March–September 2024 were interviewed (in-depth interview ~1 hour) and provided financial data.

**Participant status:** 60% completed, 19% in process, 21% frozen after 0–2 meetings. 13 consultants; average ~8.5 sessions for those who completed.

**Personal axis:** Pre-accompaniment, the state of the business strongly affected participants (anxiety, stress, fear, confusion). Post-accompaniment: reported improvement in self-confidence, self-efficacy, well-being, reduced stress and anxiety, greater economic certainty and sense of ability to cope. Strong expressed desire to advance the business.

**Business axis:** Many did not know how to manage their business before accompaniment; consultants tailored support to each business. After accompaniment: more planning and order, better self-confidence in the service/product, less avoidance of financial aspects, adoption of tools (Excel, targets, pricing, marketing). Minority reported only moderate improvement, often linked to emotional state.

**Financial axis (23 businesses):**
- **Change in income:** Median monthly income before accompaniment 42,980 ₪; after 89,393 ₪ — **increase of 108%**. 20 of 23 had increased income, 3 had decrease.
- **Monthly debt repayment:** Of 19 with data: 8 had no repayment before; 5 no change; 4 decrease; 2 in settlement (frozen). No increase in monthly repayment.
- **Total debt:** Of 23: 10 had decrease in total debt, 8 had no debt, 3 no change, 2 increase. Median debt before 180,000 ₪, after 152,450 ₪ — **decrease of 15.3%**.

**Most significant change (qualitative):** Four main areas: (1) Acquisition and use of management tools (dominant); (2) Optimism about the future of the business; (3) Proactivity and initiative to change the situation; (4) Improvement in emotional state. Most interviewees reported significant change in at least one area; over half in two or three areas.

**Conclusions:** High satisfaction with accompaniment; consultants perceived as professional and committed; accompaniment promoted recovery and adoption of healthier management patterns (order in income/expense, cash-flow management, planning, marketing, pricing). Free provision of accompaniment may have lowered initial expectations and increased satisfaction once quality was evident. Financial results: 108% median income increase; moderate improvement in debt indicators (15% median debt reduction).
