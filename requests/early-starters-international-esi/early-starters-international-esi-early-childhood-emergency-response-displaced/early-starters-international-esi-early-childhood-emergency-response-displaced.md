## The Ask

Early Starters International (ESI) is requesting partnership and funding to sustain and expand its emergency response for young children and families displaced by Operation Lion's Roar. Nearly 3,000 people have been displaced after homes were damaged by missile strikes; roughly 400 are young children now living in temporary hotels without safety and routine. ESI is delivering mobile trauma-informed safe-space programs, caregiver training, and system-level coordination with municipalities and partners. They have already deployed two mobile units in Jerusalem serving 40 children and are preparing to scale to additional locations. They are seeking support to continue and expand this early-childhood response as the situation evolves.

## Context & Background

As Israel faces daily missile strikes during Operation Lion's Roar, nearly 3,000 people—mostly from Tel Aviv, Beit Shemesh, and Be'er Sheva—have been evacuated after their homes were damaged, with additional families likely to be displaced. Approximately 400 young children are among those temporarily relocated to hotels. For these children and families, displacement means loss of safety, routine, and community support, and a heightened risk of stress responses that can become long-term trauma if not addressed early.

ESI brings global expertise in protecting young children in emergency settings by combining direct services that stabilize families, capacity-building for caregivers and professionals, and system-level coordination. In Israel, ESI rapidly deployed this model after October 7, mobilizing to 17 sites. Since the outbreak of Operation Lion's Roar, ESI has been conducting continuous needs assessments with municipal departments, emergency authorities, and local service providers. They have found significant but fragmented support: multiple actors provide pieces of support, and families with young children often move between agencies without a coherent, family-oriented pathway. Mental-health services exist but are not always tailored to early childhood, and there is limited integration between immediate relief, parent-child support, and day-to-day routines that prevent post-trauma escalation in young children.

To address this gap, ESI is coordinating with municipalities, authorities, and civil-society partners to align services and create a single, coherent early-childhood response. On March 8 they began deploying mobile safe-space programming in Jerusalem (largest concentration of young children), including daily 3-hour child-focused sessions, parent-child support, and trauma-informed guidance for educators. ESI currently deploys two mobile units staffed by three trained professionals delivering 6–7 hours of daily programming for 40 young children (birth to age 6). They are also starting activities in northern shelters and preparing to deploy to additional locations. "Early on Wheels" mobile units allow quick scaling and can continue into the recovery phase. ESI has redirected existing resources for the immediate response but needs additional funds to continue and scale as the situation evolves.

# Early Starters International (ESI) – Emergency Deployment in Israel | March 2026

As Israel faces daily missile strikes, nearly 3,000 people - mostly from Tel Aviv, Beit Shemesh, and Be'er Sheva - have been evacuated after their homes were damaged, with additional families likely to be displaced as the situation evolves. The families that have been temporarily relocated to hotels across the country include approximately **400 young children**.

For these children and their families, displacement means the loss of safety, routine, and community support, and a heightened risk of stress responses that can harden into long-term trauma if not addressed early.

Early Starters International (ESI) brings global expertise in protecting young children in emergency and crisis settings by combining direct services that stabilize families, capacity-building for caregivers and professionals, and system-level coordination and knowledge transfer that strengthen long-term resilience and enable recovery to be sustained through local institutions. In Israel, ESI rapidly deployed this model after October 7, mobilizing to 17 sites within days and establishing safe spaces, educator training, and tailored programming that continues into recovery.

Since the outbreak of Operation Lion's Roar, ESI has been conducting continuous needs assessments with municipal departments, emergency authorities, and local service providers. Findings include significant support on the ground from municipal and national authorities alongside civil-society partners, but also **fragmentation**: multiple actors providing pieces of support, families with young children often moving between agencies without a coherent, family-oriented pathway. Mental-health services exist but are not always tailored to early childhood, and there is limited integration between immediate relief, parent-child support, and the day-to-day routines that prevent post-trauma escalation in young children. In short, the system is active but not yet aligned around the specific needs of children ages 0–6 and their caregivers.

To address this gap, ESI is coordinating closely with municipalities, authorities, and civil-society partners to align services and create a single, coherent early-childhood response. Direct support: on March 8 ESI began deploying mobile safe-space programming in **Jerusalem**, where the largest concentration of young children is located. This includes daily 3-hour child-focused sessions, parent-child support (beginning next week), and trauma-informed guidance for educators and other support staff. ESI currently deploys **two mobile units**, staffed by **three trained professionals** who deliver **6–7 hours of daily programming** for a total of **40 young children (birth to age 6)**.

In parallel, ESI is starting activities in shelters in the northern part of the country and preparing to deploy across additional locations as needs evolve. The specialized "Early on Wheels" mobile units will enable quick scaling throughout the country and can continue to be deployed in the recovery phase once families are able to return home. Each unit carries rapid-assembly, sensory-rich equipment for a child-centered environment.

Additional funds are needed to continue activities as the situation evolves. ESI is inviting partners to support the early-childhood emergency response and join efforts to prevent post-trauma, stabilize families, and sustain recovery.
