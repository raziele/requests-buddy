## The Ask

The Israeli Association of Community Centers (Matnasim) is requesting emergency program funding to scale its response to community needs during Operation Roar of the Lion. The request covers neighborhood resilience ("Together in the Building"), early childhood day care for reservists' and essential workers' families, child and family resilience activities, youth engagement and "Special Friends" programming, elderly outreach to reduce loneliness, and support for families of people with special needs. Total estimated budget is $900,000 per month for the national program, with the majority directed to direct delivery, kits, and community response.

**Funding Requested:** $900,000 per month

**Funding Breakdown:**
- All populations — neighborhood/community strengthening, "Together in the Building" (kits, equipment, local coordinators, building competitions and prizes): $300,000/month
- Early childhood (kits, respite activities): $75,000/month
- Children and family (activity kits, Zoom and protected-space activities): $200,000/month
- Youth ("Special Friends" coordinators, training, equipment, materials): $125,000/month
- Elderly (hotline, activity kits, visits, basic supplies as needed): $100,000/month
- Special needs (kits, facilitators, materials development, individual parent support): $100,000/month

## Context & Background

Operation Roar of the Lion has placed Israel in a prolonged, multi-front security reality characterized by ongoing fire toward civilian areas, extended stays in protected spaces, and disruption of community life. Effects are felt nationwide: families spend long periods in shelters, parents are called to reserves, education systems are currently closed or operating remotely, and many citizens face stress, uncertainty, and sustained emotional strain.

The Matnasim network, through community centers across the country, acts as a critical community anchor in routine and emergency. Since the operation began, it has received many requests from communities, local authorities, and partners for an immediate, tailored response to needs on the ground. The document details central needs by population: neighborhood resilience and "Together in the Building"; early childhood frameworks for reservists and essential workers; child and family wraparound (online and in-protected-space activities, resilience workshops); youth engagement and "Special Friends" (youth with and without disabilities); elderly (outreach calls, activity kits, protected-space activities, home visits where possible); and special-needs populations (adapted Zoom sessions, parent guidance, kits, ongoing phone support).

The estimated budget is spread across roughly 200 community centers nationwide with a potential reach of about 3 million residents. Most of the budget is for direct delivery, kits, and community response, with a portion for professional staff. The association states it is ready to act immediately, at scale, and is open to deepening, refining, and adapting the program scope in partnership.
