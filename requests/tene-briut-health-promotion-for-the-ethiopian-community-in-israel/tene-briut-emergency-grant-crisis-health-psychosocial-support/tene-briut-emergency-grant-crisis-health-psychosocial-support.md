## The Ask

Tene Briut requests an emergency grant of $50,000 to maintain and scale crisis health and psychosocial support for Israel's Ethiopian community during wartime. The organization is expanding 24/7 Amharic and Tigrinya hotlines, conducting proactive welfare calls to isolated seniors, and distributing culturally tailored emergency and mental health guidance through trusted community professionals to close language and trust gaps that limit access to life-saving services.

**Funding Requested:** $50,000

**Funding Breakdown:**
- Staffing and experts (Amharic-speaking therapists, social workers, medical consultants): $25,000
- Hotline operations (technology, infrastructure, 24/7 staffing): $5,000
- Digital content (emergency videos and radio spots production/distribution): $10,000
- Outreach logistics (proactive calling campaign for 1,700+ seniors): $10,000

**Non-Financial Ask:** Partnership support for dissemination of emergency information and coordination with health and municipal actors.

## Context & Background

Tene Briut, a registered nonprofit focused on health promotion for Ethiopian Israelis, describes a severe wartime access gap affecting Amharic-speaking community members. According to the proposal, many emergency broadcasts and mental health resources are primarily in Hebrew, which heightens anxiety and reduces timely use of services among non-fluent speakers.

The organization highlights compounded risk among elderly residents and recent immigrants. It states that thousands of seniors are isolated and may not know how to access telemedicine, emergency rights, or remote psychiatric support. The proposal also emphasizes a trust dynamic: community members are more likely to adopt safety guidance and seek care when information is delivered by culturally familiar Ethiopian-Israeli health professionals.

In response, Tene Briut has activated a 24/7 multilingual hotline for psychosocial first aid and practical case support, including labor-rights navigation, medication access, and psychiatric referrals. It is also running a proactive tele-check initiative for over 1,700 adults aged 80+ to identify unmet needs such as food, medicine, and shelter and to escalate cases to municipal authorities.

The requested funding is positioned as emergency operating support to sustain these interventions and produce culturally tailored risk-communication content (videos, radio, webinars) in partnership with the Ministry of Health. The stated objective is to prevent exclusion of Ethiopian Israelis from critical health and resilience services during an active national emergency.

## GRANT PROPOSAL: Emergency Health and Resilience for Israel’s Ethiopian Community

**Submitted by:** Tene Briut (Health Promotion for the Ethiopian Community in Israel)  
**Project Lead:** Avi Masfin, CEO  
**Grant Request:** $50,000  
**Focus Area:** Emergency Relief, Mental Health, and Healthcare Access

### 1. Executive Summary
Tene Briut, a leader in culturally competent healthcare for over 20 years, seeks an emergency grant of $50,000 to sustain and expand crisis intervention services for the Ethiopian-Israeli community. During the current state of war, Amharic-speaking populations, particularly elderly people and new immigrants, face barriers to life-saving information and psychological support. The proposal states the mission is to ensure no Ethiopian-Israeli is left behind because of language or cultural gaps.

### 2. Organization Background
Tene Briut presents itself as a bridge between the Israeli healthcare system and the Ethiopian community. Staff include Amharic-speaking medical professionals, social workers, and health educators. Core approach: health equity through advocacy, linguistic accessibility, and direct intervention, with attention to cultural nuances and historical trauma.

### 3. Statement of Need
The proposal lists wartime vulnerabilities:
- **Information gap:** emergency broadcasts and mental-health resources mostly in Hebrew, increasing anxiety among non-fluent speakers.
- **Isolation of elderly:** thousands of seniors are isolated and may not know how to access remote medical services or emergency rights.
- **Trust deficit:** safety and mental-health engagement improve when guidance comes from trusted, culturally familiar figures (example named: Dr. Seffefe Aycheh).

### 4. Program Components & Implementation
#### A. 24/7 Multi-Channel Hotline (Emotional & Functional Support)
- Expanded Amharic and Tigrinya helplines to 24/7 operation.
- **Psychosocial support:** immediate emotional first aid to reduce trauma and stress.
- **Rights access:** support for families on emergency labor laws, online ordering of chronic medications, and access to psychiatric care from home.

#### B. Proactive Outreach to At-Risk Seniors
- "Tele-Check" campaign targeting **1,700 individuals aged 80+**.
- Goals: reduce loneliness, verify safety, identify unmet needs (food, medicine, shelter), and report needs to municipal authorities.

#### C. Culturally Tailored Emergency Communications
- Partnership with Israeli Ministry of Health to produce professional information videos.
- Content: safety instructions and mental-health resilience messages featuring Ethiopian-Israeli nurses and social workers.
- Distribution channels: social media, Amharic radio, and community WhatsApp groups.

#### D. Strategic Resilience Webinars
- Conducted with Ministry of Health.
- Led by CEO Avi Masfin and Dr. Seffefe Aycheh.
- Purpose: provide real-time updates and strengthen community-wide resilience.
