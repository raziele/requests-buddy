## The Ask

The Haifa Rape Crisis Center (HRCC) is requesting **$200,000** in emergency capacity funding to prepare for an expected surge in demand for support when Operation **Shaagat HaAri** de-escalates. Based on research and decades of field experience, requests typically rise by **up to 30%** after de-escalation: during acute operations many survivors withdraw, minimize their own pain, and seek less help, while relative calm later provides space for what they endured—prompting many to reach out simultaneously.

**Funding Requested:** $200,000

**Funding Breakdown:**
- **New Volunteer Training Course** (recruitment, training, supervision): **$95,000**
- **WhatsApp Expansion** (extended hours, additional responders, supervision): **$60,000**
- **Surge Capacity Reinforcement** (intake, coordination, flexible staffing): **$45,000**

## Proposed Activities (What the Funding Will Support)

### 1) New Volunteer Training Course (Emergency Cohort)
Funding would expand the volunteer backbone supporting the **24/7 hotline** so the center can absorb increased call volume **without wait times**. The program includes:
- Recruitment and screening
- **80-hour professional training** course
- Clinical supervision and ongoing mentorship
- Training materials and operational costs

### 2) Expansion of WhatsApp Support Hours
The funding would extend and strengthen WhatsApp support, which is critical when survivors cannot speak aloud (e.g., in shelters/safe rooms or limited privacy environments) and sometimes feel safer initiating contact in writing. The plan includes:
- Extending operating hours (including growth in late-night contacts when many survivors write during or after alerts)
- Increasing trained responders per shift
- Strengthening professional supervision and coordination for digital responders

HRCC notes that the nature of late-night messages can reflect extremely difficult circumstances, including sheltering with the person who harmed them and escalating suicidal thoughts.

### 3) Expanding Immediate Response Capacity
Funding would reinforce immediate response so survivors reaching out in the coming weeks are met by trained, available, and prepared responders. This includes:
- Expanded peak-hour coverage
- Maintaining and strengthening accompaniment (including to police stations and through criminal proceedings)
- Support at **National Insurance committee hearings**
- Use of the **Safe Social Space** as resilience-building emergency response
- Stronger coordination between hotline, accompaniment teams, and follow-up services

## Context & Background

Research and field experience indicate that survivors of sexual violence often experience collective crises as a **double trauma**. During war, many survivors instinctively withdraw, minimize their pain, and hesitate to seek help, feeling that their suffering is less legitimate in the face of a national emergency. For many, this inward shutdown—driven by shame and self-silencing—worsens outcomes: it can correlate with increased flashbacks, avoidance of emotional support, neglect of physical and emotional needs, declining functioning, and heightened despair with increased suicide risk.

One woman recently wrote to HRCC:
> “I'm angry at myself for feeling so broken right now when other people have shrapnel falling on their homes.”

Based on patterns observed in previous conflicts, when the immediate emergency subsides and people finally have space to process what they endured, many survivors reach out simultaneously. HRCC therefore anticipates a concentrated and intense post-operation surge.

Additional context includes:
- By the end of **2025**, HRCC helplines received **over 13,000** requests/contacts for assistance.
- Operation **Shaagat HaAri** follows nearly two years of sustained war, displacement, uncertainty, and cumulative national stress.
- Many survivors had only just begun to stabilize—returning to therapy, rebuilding routines, and cautiously reclaiming work and social life—and this new operation risks interrupting that fragile progress.
- Repeated exposure to national emergencies compounds trauma; sirens, confinement, and renewed insecurity can reactivate hypervigilance, panic responses, and loss of control, and repeated disruption can undo months of therapeutic progress.

HRCC serves **41 local authorities** across Northern Israel and responds to thousands of requests each year. The **24/7 hotline and digital platforms** are often the first—and sometimes the only—place survivors feel able to turn.
