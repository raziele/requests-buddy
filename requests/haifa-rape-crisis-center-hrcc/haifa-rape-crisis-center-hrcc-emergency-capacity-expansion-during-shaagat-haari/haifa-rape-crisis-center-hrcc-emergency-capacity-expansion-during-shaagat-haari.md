## The Ask

HRCC requests **$200,000** in emergency/program capacity funding to prepare for an expected post-operation surge in support requests after de-escalation of Operation "Shaagat HaAri."

Across prior escalations, HRCC consistently observed that during active conflict survivors often withdraw, silence distress, and defer help-seeking; when relative calm returns, requests rise rapidly (typically by up to **30%**). HRCC reports over **13,000** helpline contacts in 2025 (up from approximately 10,000), with intensified late-year distress and urgent needs.

### Funding Requested: $200,000

### Funding Breakdown

- **New Volunteer Training Course (Emergency Cohort)** — $95,000  
  (recruitment, screening, 80-hour professional training, supervision, mentorship, materials, operational costs)
- **WhatsApp Expansion** — $60,000  
  (extended hours, additional trained responders per shift, supervision and coordination for digital response)
- **Surge Capacity Reinforcement / Immediate Response Expansion** — $45,000  
  (peak-hour coverage, intake/coordination/flexible staffing, accompaniment and follow-up support)

## Context & Background

Operation "Shaagat HaAri" has returned communities to sirens, alerts, confinement, and prolonged uncertainty. HRCC emphasizes that survivors of sexual violence experience collective emergencies as a "double trauma": many minimize their pain during wartime and delay help-seeking out of shame, self-silencing, and perceived illegitimacy of personal suffering during national crisis.

One survivor message cited by HRCC:

> "I'm angry at myself for feeling so broken right now when other people have shrapnel falling on their homes."

HRCC reports that delayed help-seeking can correlate with worsening flashbacks, avoidance of support, declining day-to-day functioning, worsening emotional and economic hardship, and in some cases heightened suicide risk.

This period is assessed as especially high-risk because it follows nearly two years of cumulative war stress and displacement. Many survivors had only recently begun rebuilding routines and therapeutic stability; renewed disruption may reactivate hypervigilance, panic, and loss of control, and can undo months of progress.

HRCC has operated through major national crises for four decades and is proactively preparing to ensure no-delay response when demand spikes in the coming weeks.

## Planned Response Components

1. **New Volunteer Training Course (Emergency Cohort)**
   - Recruitment and screening
   - 80-hour professional training
   - Clinical supervision and ongoing mentorship
   - Training materials and operational support
   - Goal: maintain rapid, no-wait response capacity for the 24/7 hotline

2. **Expansion of WhatsApp Support Hours**
   - Critical for survivors unable to speak safely (e.g., shelters/safe rooms, limited privacy, or preference for written communication)
   - Includes late-night response when distress disclosures often increase
   - Addresses high-risk situations, including contact from survivors in unsafe living conditions and severe emotional crisis
   - Plan: longer operating hours, more responders per shift, stronger professional supervision

3. **Expanding Immediate Response Capacity**
   - Increased coverage during peak demand windows
   - Accompaniment to police stations and through criminal/legal processes
   - Support for National Insurance (Bituach Leumi) committee/hearing processes
   - Use of protected Safe Social Space as resilience-building emergency support
   - Stronger coordination between hotline, accompaniment teams, and follow-up services
