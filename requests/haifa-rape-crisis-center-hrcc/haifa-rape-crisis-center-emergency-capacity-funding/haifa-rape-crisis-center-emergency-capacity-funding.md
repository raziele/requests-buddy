## The Ask

The Haifa Rape Crisis Center (HRCC) is requesting $200,000 in emergency capacity funding to prepare for an expected surge in demand for support when Operation Shaagat HaAri de-escalates. Research and decades of experience show that during active military operations many survivors withdraw and seek less help, but when relative calm returns, requests typically rise by up to 30%. In 2025 the center received over 13,000 contacts to its helplines. The funding would support an additional volunteer training course for the 24/7 hotline, expansion of WhatsApp support hours (critical for women in shelters or with limited privacy), and strengthened immediate response capacity including accompaniment, Safe Social Space, and coordination so that when survivors reach out in the coming weeks they are met without delay.

**Funding Requested:** $200,000

**Funding Breakdown:**
- New Volunteer Training Course (recruitment, training, supervision): $95,000
- WhatsApp Expansion (extended hours, additional responders, supervision): $60,000
- Surge Capacity Reinforcement (intake, coordination, flexible staffing): $45,000

## Context & Background

Operation Shaagat HaAri has returned Northern Israel to a reality of sirens, tension, and uncertainty. The Haifa Rape Crisis Center has responded to every major escalation and national crisis over the past four decades. During acute operations, survivors often minimize their own pain and hesitate to seek help; when calm returns, many reach out simultaneously. By the end of 2025, HRCC helplines had received over 13,000 requests—a significant increase from prior years—with most contacts coming late in the year. This operation follows nearly two years of sustained war, displacement, and cumulative stress; many survivors had only begun to stabilize. Repeated exposure to national emergencies compounds trauma and can undo months of therapeutic progress.

The center is preparing now for the post-operation surge. The request targets three areas: (1) a new volunteer training cohort (recruitment, 80-hour professional training, clinical supervision) so the 24/7 hotline can absorb increased volume; (2) expansion of the WhatsApp helpline—essential during emergencies when women cannot speak aloud in shelters or lack privacy—including extended hours and more trained responders; (3) surge capacity reinforcement: expanded coverage at peak hours, accompaniment to police and court, support at National Insurance hearings, Safe Social Space for resilience, and stronger coordination between hotline, accompaniment, and follow-up.

HRCC serves 41 local authorities across the North. The outreach was relayed by Yael Yabes, strategic consultant at HaTasheet (התשתית), who supports the center on resource development and sees a natural fit with San Francisco Federation’s focus on trauma and supporting people in need.

**March 5th, 2026**

**Preparing for the Post-Operation Surge: $200,000 Emergency Capacity Expansion During Operation "Shaagat HaAri"**

Dear Friends and Partners,

As Operation "Shaagat HaAri" continues and communities across Israel once again live under sirens, security alerts, and prolonged uncertainty, we are preparing for a dynamic we know well from previous periods of war.

Research and decades of field experience show that survivors of sexual violence often experience collective crises as a double trauma. During times of war, many survivors instinctively withdraw, minimize their own pain, and hesitate to seek help, feeling that their suffering is less legitimate in the face of a national emergency.

One woman recently wrote to us:

> "I'm angry at myself for feeling so broken right now when other people have shrapnel falling on their homes."

This tendency to shrink inward, fueled by shame and self-silencing, often leads to a worsening of symptoms: increased flashbacks, avoidance of emotional support, neglect of physical and emotional needs, and declining functioning. In many cases, it also correlates with heightened despair and increased suicide risk.

Based on what we have seen in previous conflicts, we know that when the immediate emergency subsides, and people finally have space to process what they have endured, many survivors reach out simultaneously for support. We are preparing now for that moment.

At the Haifa Rape Crisis Center (HRCC), we have responded to every major escalation, war, and national crisis over the past four decades. One pattern remains consistent: During acute military operations, communities function in survival mode. Emotional processing is postponed. Trauma responses are contained. But when relative calm returns, when there is finally a quiet moment, survivors begin reaching out.

Following previous periods of conflict, requests for support typically rise by up to 30% in the weeks following de-escalation. By the end of 2025, our helplines received over 13,000 requests for assistance. These calls reflected intensified emotional distress—including urgent requests for mental health support, referrals for hospitalization, worsening health and economic hardship, and a profound need to reduce isolation and rebuild anchors for daily life.

This time, the risk is even greater. Operation "Shaagat HaAri" comes after nearly two years of sustained war, displacement, uncertainty, and cumulative national stress. Over the past year, many survivors of sexual violence had only just begun to stabilize: returning to therapy, rebuilding routines, cautiously reclaiming work and social life. This new operation interrupts that fragile progress. Repeated exposure to national emergencies compounds trauma. Sirens, confinement, and renewed insecurity reactivate hypervigilance, panic responses, and loss of control. Recovery is not linear. Repeated disruption can undo months of therapeutic progress.

We believe the post-operation surge may be both concentrated and intense, and we are preparing now to ensure we can respond without delay.
