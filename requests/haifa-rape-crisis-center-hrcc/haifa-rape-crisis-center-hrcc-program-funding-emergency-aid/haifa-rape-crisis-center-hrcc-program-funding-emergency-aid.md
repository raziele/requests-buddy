## The Ask

The Haifa Rape Crisis Center (HRCC) is requesting **$200,000** to expand emergency capacity ahead of an expected post–Operation Shaagat HaAri surge in demand. Research and decades of field experience show that during active military operations many survivors withdraw, minimize their own pain, and seek less help; when relative calm returns, requests typically rise by up to **30%** in the weeks after de-escalation. By the end of 2025, HRCC helplines received **over 13,000** requests for assistance (with **contacts rising from approximately 10,000 to more than 13,000** over the course of the year, and **most contacts coming late in the year**).

Funding would support: a **new volunteer training cohort** for the **24/7 hotline**; **extended WhatsApp support hours** and more trained responders (critical when survivors are in shelters, lack privacy, or cannot speak aloud—**including at night** when many write during or after alerts); and **reinforced immediate response capacity**—intake, coordination, flexible staffing, **accompaniment to police and through criminal proceedings**, support at **Bituach Leumi / National Insurance** hearings, use of the **Safe Social Space**, and **stronger coordination between hotline, accompaniment teams, and follow-up**—so that when survivors reach out in the coming weeks they are met **without delay** by trained, available responders.

**Funding Requested:** $200,000

**Funding Breakdown:**
- New Volunteer Training Course (recruitment, training, supervision): $95,000
- WhatsApp Expansion (extended hours, additional responders, supervision): $60,000
- Surge Capacity Reinforcement (intake, coordination, flexible staffing): $45,000

## Context & Background

Operation **Shaagat HaAri** (also written **“Shaagat HaAri”**) has returned Northern Israel to sirens, tension, confinement, and uncertainty. HRCC has responded to **every major escalation, war, and national crisis over the past four decades**.

Survivors of sexual violence often experience collective crises as a **double trauma**. During war many hesitate to seek help, feeling their suffering is less legitimate amid a national emergency. **Repeated exposure** to national emergencies compounds trauma—sirens, confinement, and renewed insecurity can reactivate **hypervigilance and panic**, especially among people whose nervous systems are already under prolonged strain. This operation follows nearly **two years** of sustained war, **displacement**, and cumulative stress; many survivors had **only just begun to stabilize** (therapy, routines, work, social life)—this new operation **interrupts fragile progress** and **can undo months of therapeutic progress**; recovery is **not linear**.

The center is **preparing now** for a surge that may be **concentrated and intense**. The three funding pillars align with the proposal: (1) **New Volunteer Training Course (Emergency Cohort)**—recruitment, screening, **80-hour** professional training, clinical supervision, mentorship, materials—volunteers as the backbone of the 24/7 hotline so increased volume is absorbed **without wait times**; (2) **WhatsApp expansion**—written contact when speaking aloud is unsafe; **growing contacts late at night**; some messages reflect **extremely difficult circumstances** (e.g., sheltering with the person who harmed them, **escalating suicidal thoughts**)—extend hours, more responders per shift, stronger **supervision and coordination for digital responders**; (3) **Immediate response capacity**—peak-hour coverage, police/court accompaniment, National Insurance hearings, Safe Social Space as **resilience-building** emergency response, coordination across services.

HRCC serves **41 local authorities** across Northern Israel. The **24/7 hotline and digital platforms** are often the **first and sometimes the only** place survivors feel able to turn.

**Date:** March 5th, 2026

**Title:** Preparing for the Post-Operation Surge: $200,000 Emergency Capacity Expansion During Operation "Shaagat HaAri"

**Letter (substantive excerpts):**

Dear Friends and Partners,

As Operation "Shaagat HaAri" continues and communities across Israel once again live under sirens, security alerts, and prolonged uncertainty, we are preparing for a dynamic we know well from previous periods of war.

Research and decades of field experience show that survivors of sexual violence often experience collective crises as a double trauma. During times of war, many survivors instinctively withdraw, minimize their own pain, and hesitate to seek help, feeling that their suffering is less legitimate in the face of a national emergency.

One woman recently wrote to us:

> "I'm angry at myself for feeling so broken right now when other people have shrapnel falling on their homes."

This tendency to shrink inward, fueled by shame and self-silencing, often leads to a worsening of symptoms: increased flashbacks, avoidance of emotional support, neglect of physical and emotional needs, and declining functioning. In many cases, it also correlates with heightened despair and increased suicide risk.

Based on what we have seen in previous conflicts, we know that when the immediate emergency subsides, and people finally have space to process what they have endured, many survivors reach out simultaneously for support. We are preparing now for that moment.

At the Haifa Rape Crisis Center (HRCC), we have responded to every major escalation, war, and national crisis over the past four decades. One pattern remains consistent: During acute military operations, communities function in survival mode. Emotional processing is postponed. Trauma responses are contained. But when relative calm returns, when there is finally a quiet moment, survivors begin reaching out.

Following previous periods of conflict, requests for support typically rise by up to 30% in the weeks following de-escalation. By the end of 2025, our helplines received over 13,000 requests for assistance. These calls reflected intensified emotional distress—including urgent requests for mental health support, referrals for hospitalization, worsening health and economic hardship, and a profound need to reduce isolation and rebuild anchors for daily life.

This time, the risk is even greater. Operation "Shaagat HaAri" comes after nearly two years of sustained war, displacement, uncertainty, and cumulative national stress. Over the past year, many survivors of sexual violence had only just begun to stabilize: returning to therapy, rebuilding routines, cautiously reclaiming work and social life. This new operation interrupts that fragile progress. Repeated exposure to national emergencies compounds trauma. Sirens, confinement, and renewed insecurity reactivate hypervigilance, panic responses, and loss of control. Recovery is not linear. Repeated disruption can undo months of therapeutic progress.

We believe the post-operation surge may be both concentrated and intense, and we are preparing now to ensure we can respond without delay.

**Targeted Emergency Capacity Expansion — Funding Request: $200,000**

1. **New Volunteer Training Course (Emergency Cohort)**
   - Recruitment and screening
   - 80-hour professional training course
   - Clinical supervision and ongoing mentorship
   - Training materials and operational costs
   - Volunteers are the backbone of the 24/7 hotline; expanding the cohort ensures increased call volume can be absorbed without wait times.

2. **Expansion of WhatsApp Support Hours**
   - Many women cannot speak aloud while in shelters or safe rooms; live in limited-privacy environments; or feel safer initiating contact via written messages.
   - Growing contacts late at night; some messages reflect extremely difficult circumstances (e.g., sheltering with the person who harmed them, escalating suicidal thoughts).
   - Plan: Extend WhatsApp operating hours; increase trained responders per shift; strengthen professional supervision and coordination for digital responders.

3. **Expanding Immediate Response Capacity**
   - Expanding response coverage during peak hours
   - Maintaining and strengthening accompaniment (e.g., to police stations, through criminal proceedings, National Insurance committee hearings)
   - Utilizing protected Safe Social Space as resilience-building emergency response
   - Strengthening coordination between hotline, accompaniment teams, and follow-up services
