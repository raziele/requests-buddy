## The Ask

HRCC is requesting **$200,000** to expand capacity ahead of an expected post–Operation Shaagat HaAri surge in demand. Research and decades of experience show that during active military operations many survivors withdraw and seek less help; when relative calm returns, requests typically rise by up to 30%. In 2025 the center received over 13,000 contacts to its helplines. Funding would support:

- A **new volunteer training cohort** (recruitment, 80-hour training, supervision) so the 24/7 hotline can absorb increased volume.
- **Extended WhatsApp support hours** (critical for survivors in shelters or with limited privacy), including additional trained responders and supervision.
- **Reinforced immediate response capacity** — intake, coordination, flexible staffing, accompaniment to police and through legal proceedings, support at Bituach Leumi/National Insurance hearings, and use of the Safe Social Space — so that when survivors reach out in the coming weeks they are met by trained, available responders without delay.

## Context & Background

Operation Shaagat HaAri has returned Northern Israel to sirens, tension, and uncertainty. Survivors of sexual violence often experience collective crises as a double trauma: during war many withdraw, minimize their own pain, and hesitate to seek help. One woman recently wrote to HRCC:

> "I'm angry at myself for feeling so broken right now when other people have shrapnel falling on their homes."

This self-silencing can worsen symptoms (flashbacks, avoidance, neglect of needs, declining functioning) and in some cases correlates with heightened despair and suicide risk. When the immediate emergency subsides and people have space to process what they endured, many reach out simultaneously. Following previous conflicts, HRCC has seen requests rise by up to 30% in the weeks after de-escalation. By end of 2025 the center received over 13,000 requests for assistance (contacts rose from ~10,000 to more than 13,000).

Operation Shaagat HaAri comes after nearly two years of sustained war, displacement, and cumulative national stress. Many survivors had only just begun to stabilize; this new operation interrupts that progress. Repeated exposure to national emergencies compounds trauma — sirens, confinement, and renewed insecurity reactivate hypervigilance and panic, especially among women whose nervous systems are already under prolonged strain. Recovery is not linear; repeated disruption can undo months of therapeutic progress.

HRCC has responded to every major escalation and national crisis over the past four decades. The outreach was relayed by Yael Yabes, strategic consultant at HaTasheet (התשתית), who supports the center on resource development and sees a natural fit with San Francisco Federation’s focus on trauma and supporting people in need. HRCC serves 41 local authorities across Northern Israel.
