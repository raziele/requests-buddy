## The Ask

HRCC requests **$200,000** in emergency/program capacity funding to prepare for an expected post-operation surge in demand after de-escalation of Operation "Shaagat HaAri." Based on research and four decades of field response, the center reports that during active conflict many survivors withdraw, minimize their pain, and delay seeking help; when relative calm returns, requests can rise by up to **30%** in a concentrated period.

The request is intended to ensure survivors are met immediately by trained responders, without delays, especially in the coming weeks after de-escalation.

**Funding Requested:** $200,000

**Funding Breakdown:**
- New Volunteer Training Course (recruitment, screening, 80-hour training, supervision, mentorship, materials): **$95,000**
- WhatsApp Expansion (extended hours, additional responders per shift, strengthened professional supervision): **$60,000**
- Surge Capacity Reinforcement (intake/coordination/flexible staffing, peak-hour coverage, accompaniment and follow-up): **$45,000**

## Context & Background

Operation "Shaagat HaAri" has reintroduced sirens, security alerts, confinement, and uncertainty across Northern Israel, following nearly two years of sustained war, displacement, and cumulative stress. HRCC notes that many survivors had only recently begun to stabilize (returning to therapy, rebuilding routines, and cautiously resuming work/social life), and that repeated national emergencies can reactivate hypervigilance, panic, shame, and loss of control.

HRCC has responded to every major escalation and national crisis over the past four decades. The center reports a consistent post-crisis dynamic: emotional processing is delayed during acute survival periods, then demand rises sharply once calm returns. Following prior conflicts, support requests rose by up to 30% in post-de-escalation weeks.

By end of 2025, HRCC helplines received over **13,000** requests/contacts (up from approximately **10,000**), including urgent mental health needs, referrals for hospitalization, worsening health/economic hardship, and requests to reduce isolation and rebuild daily functioning.

One cited survivor message:
> "I'm angry at myself for feeling so broken right now when other people have shrapnel falling on their homes."

HRCC emphasizes elevated risk in this cycle, including worsening symptoms, reduced help-seeking during conflict, and potential increases in despair and suicide risk without rapid, scalable response infrastructure.

## Program Components

1. **New Volunteer Training Course (Emergency Cohort)**
   - Recruitment and screening
   - 80-hour professional training
   - Clinical supervision and mentorship
   - Training materials and operating costs
   - Purpose: expand hotline workforce to absorb increased volume without wait times

2. **Expansion of WhatsApp Support Hours**
   - Critical for women in shelters/safe rooms or low-privacy settings
   - Supports those unable to speak aloud or preferring written contact
   - Includes late-night responsiveness for high-risk messages
   - Plan: extended operating hours, increased trained responders, stronger digital supervision/coordination

3. **Expanding Immediate Response Capacity**
   - Expanded peak-hour coverage
   - Accompaniment to police stations and through criminal proceedings
   - Support at National Insurance (Bituach Leumi) hearings/committees
   - Use of protected Safe Social Space as resilience-building emergency response
   - Stronger coordination across hotline, accompaniment teams, and follow-up services
