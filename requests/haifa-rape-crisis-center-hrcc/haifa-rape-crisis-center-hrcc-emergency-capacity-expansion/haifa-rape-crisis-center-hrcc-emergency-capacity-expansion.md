## The Ask

HRCC requests **$200,000** in emergency/program capacity funding to prepare for an anticipated surge in demand following de-escalation of Operation “Shaagat HaAri.” Based on research and four decades of field experience, the center reports that during active conflict survivors often withdraw, minimize their own pain, and delay help-seeking; once relative calm returns, requests for support rise sharply (historically up to **30%** in the following weeks).

The center is preparing to ensure survivors can access immediate, trained support without delay when post-operation demand increases.

**Funding Requested:** $200,000

**Funding Breakdown:**
- **New Volunteer Training Course (Emergency Cohort):** $95,000  
  (recruitment, screening, 80-hour training, supervision, mentorship, materials, operational costs)
- **WhatsApp Expansion:** $60,000  
  (extended hours, additional trained responders per shift, strengthened supervision/coordination for digital responders)
- **Surge Capacity Reinforcement:** $45,000  
  (intake and coordination, flexible/peak-hour staffing, accompaniment, follow-up linkage, Safe Social Space response)

## Context & Background

HRCC describes a recurring “double trauma” dynamic for survivors of sexual violence during national emergencies: personal trauma is reactivated while survivors feel pressure to deprioritize their own suffering. The center notes that this can intensify flashbacks, isolation, emotional suppression, and functional decline, and may correlate with increased despair and suicide risk.

One survivor message cited in the attached letter:
> “I'm angry at myself for feeling so broken right now when other people have shrapnel falling on their homes.”

Key contextual points:
- Operation “Shaagat HaAri” occurs after nearly two years of sustained war, displacement, uncertainty, and cumulative stress.
- Survivors who had only recently regained stability (therapy, routine, work, social functioning) face renewed disruption.
- Sirens, confinement, and insecurity can reactivate hypervigilance, panic, and loss of control.
- HRCC expects the post-operation surge to be especially concentrated and intense.
- HRCC has responded to every major escalation and national crisis over the past four decades.
- In 2025, contacts reportedly increased from approximately **10,000** to **over 13,000** calls/messages, with significant late-year growth.

Programmatic response focus:
1. Protect 24/7 hotline responsiveness and reduce wait times via expanded trained volunteer capacity.
2. Expand WhatsApp access for survivors unable to speak aloud (shelters/safe rooms, low privacy environments), especially late-night and high-risk situations.
3. Strengthen immediate response pathways: accompaniment to police stations and criminal proceedings, support at National Insurance (Bituach Leumi) hearings/committees, and use of Safe Social Space as a resilience-building emergency intervention.

HRCC emphasizes that hotline and digital channels are often the first—and sometimes only—accessible point of support.
