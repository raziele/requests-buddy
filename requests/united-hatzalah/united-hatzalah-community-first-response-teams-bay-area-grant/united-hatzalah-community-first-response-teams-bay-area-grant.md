## The Ask

United Hatzalah, Israel's only nationwide fully volunteer emergency medical service, is requesting a $1,000,000 grant from the Jewish Federation Bay Area to establish four new community-based volunteer medic teams ("Medical Kitot Konenut" / Community First Response teams). Each team will consist of 25 volunteer medics (100 total), trained and equipped to provide free, on-scene emergency care within minutes. The grant will fund 200-hour EMT training, complete medic bags and communication devices, and one year of volunteer support including internship, refresher courses, insurance, and post-incident counseling. The proposal emphasizes heightened need due to war and terror, improved disaster preparedness, and the goal of achieving 90-second response times in more areas.

**Funding Requested:** $1,000,000

**Funding Breakdown:**
- Recruitment & Training (200-hour EMT training, recruitment, learning materials & equipment, tests, certification): $120,000
- Medical & Dispatch Equipment (complete medic bag and communication device, including apps and licenses): $680,000
- One Year Volunteer Support (internship & 1-year support: continued education, medical supply refill, insurance, recognition events, post-incident support): $200,000

## Context & Background

United Hatzalah is Israel's only nationwide, fully volunteer emergency medical service. It trains and equips ordinary citizens to provide free on-scene medical treatment in the critical window before traditional ambulance arrival. With unique dispatch technology and rapid response vehicles, volunteers often arrive within minutes (as fast as 90 seconds in metropolitan areas). In 2025 the National Dispatch Center received over 800,000 emergency calls. On October 7, 2023, volunteers established field hospitals, entered terrorist-controlled kibbutzim, treated over 4,700 civilians and soldiers, provided psychological first aid to 2,700 Israelis, and evacuated 1,250 injured patients. Post-incident analysis highlighted the need to strengthen community-based teams for mass-casualty incidents.

Israeli ambulance response times average eleven minutes in cities and longer in rural areas; the brain survives only four to six minutes without oxygen. United Hatzalah's volunteer network arrives within three minutes or less to perform lifesaving treatment. Given heightened threat of war and two and a half years of rocket fire, the organization must respond to both routine emergencies (household injuries, car accidents, cardiac arrest, drownings, anaphylaxis, emergency births) and war- and terror-related injuries. During large-scale emergencies, trained community-based teams are essential when communication is disrupted or external backup cannot arrive in time.

The grant would fund four new "Jewish Federation Bay Area Medical Kitot Konenut" teams: 100 volunteers trained, equipped, and deployed nationwide. Each volunteer responds to an average of 20 emergencies monthly, so the new teams would assist 24,000+ citizens annually. The sponsorship includes 200-hour EMT course (all emergency scenarios including violence), complete medic bags (tourniquets, IVs, oxygen, EpiPen, defibrillators, etc.), communication devices for dispatch coordination, and 100 supervised emergency calls during internship.

United Hatzalah is Israel's only nationwide, fully volunteer emergency medical service (EMS), and our mission is to save lives. We train and equip ordinary citizens to provide free on-scene medical treatment to anyone in need during the critical window between the onset of crisis and arrival of traditional ambulance assistance. While ambulances are often delayed, United Hatzalah volunteers are always nearby. With unique dispatch technology, innovative rapid response vehicles, and community-based volunteer network, volunteers arrive within minutes (as fast as 90 seconds in metropolitan areas). In 2025, the National Dispatch Center received over 800,000 emergency calls. The organization is working to expand the volunteer network to 16,000 medics with a 90-second response goal for every emergency in Israel.

On October 7, 2023, during the biggest catastrophe in Israel's history, the EMS model proved indispensable. Volunteers entered the inferno on Shabbat morning, establishing field hospitals and entering terrorist-controlled kibbutzim, treating thousands and saving hundreds of lives. Treated over 4,700 civilians and soldiers; provided psychological first aid to 2,700 Israelis; evacuated 1,250 injured patients to hospitals via ambulance and helicopter. Post-incident analysis (hundreds of hours of calls, video, reports) highlighted the need to strengthen EMS capabilities and preparedness for mass-casualty incidents (MCIs) and community-based volunteer teams.

A grant of **$1,000,000** will enable recruitment, training, and equipping of **four new community-based volunteer teams** (medical "kitot konenut"). Each team: 25 volunteer medics; total **100 volunteers**.

**The challenge:** The human brain can survive only four to six minutes without oxygen; average ambulance response in Israel is eleven minutes in cities and longer in rural areas. United Hatzalah volunteers arrive within three minutes or less. Volunteers are dubbed "Angels in Orange." Given heightened threat of war, training and equipping additional medics for everyday emergencies and MCIs has never been more urgent. During the past two and a half years, Israeli citizens have endured heavy rocket fire. United Hatzalah must be ready for routine calls (household injuries, car accidents, cardiac arrests, drownings, anaphylaxis, emergency births) and war/terror injuries. During large-scale emergencies, community-based teams must coordinate, triage, and provide lifesaving care when communication is disrupted or external backup cannot arrive in time. New teams ensure disaster preparedness, support rebuilding of communities, and enhance local resilience.

**Sponsorship components:**
- 200-hour EMT course (emergency scenarios and medical treatment for every type of illness, accident, and act of violence; written and practical tests)
- Complete medic bags (bandages, tourniquets, IVs, oxygen tanks, EpiPen, glucometers, defibrillators, etc.)
- Communication devices (dispatch identification, alerting, coordination)
- Internship: 100 supervised emergency calls
- One year volunteer support: refresher courses, emergency drills, team meetings, volunteer insurance, medical supply refills, retention activities, post-incident psychological counseling

**Anticipated impact:** Each volunteer responds to ~20 emergencies monthly; new teams will assist **24,000+ citizens annually**. With the $1,000,000 grant, four new teams will be launched as "Jewish Federation Bay Area Medical Kitot Kenenut" (Community First Response) teams — 100 volunteers trained, equipped, and deployed nationwide.
