## The Ask

The Jewish Agency has issued an emergency needs document (draft as of March 1, 2026) in response to Operation Roaring Lion—the war that began on Saturday February 28 when Iran retaliated with hundreds of long-range ballistic missiles, causing massive damage to Israel's civilian population and infrastructure, ten lives lost, and more than 130 injured. The Agency, in coordination with national emergency authorities, local municipalities, beneficiaries, and partners, has identified three funding elements: (1) An Emergency Fund for Roaring Lion to provide financial assistance to bereaved families, injured parties, and those who suffered property destruction (immediate and rehabilitation grants); special assistance and group interventions for those who lost homes/evacuees including resilience strengthening; and assistance for populations requiring extra support (elderly, children). Total estimated need ranges from $3.7M (300 immediate grants) to $10.1M (2,000 immediate grants). (2) Support for Jewish Agency beneficiaries: Amigour residents (food packages) and Olim in absorption centers (shelter activities for children, mental health assistance). Total estimated need: $600,000. (3) Financial assistance for Israel Experience participants (temporary housing, travel costs to Athens or Cyprus to return home). Total need: $2,000,000.

**Funding Requested:** Combined estimated need: $6,300,000 (based on 300 immediate grants) to $12,700,000 (based on 2,000 immediate grants); breakdown: Emergency Fund $3.7M–$10.1M depending on grant scale, Jewish Agency beneficiaries $600,000, Israel Experience $2,000,000

## Context & Background

On Saturday February 28, 2026, Israel, the United States, and Iran entered into a new war entitled Operation Roaring Lion. Iran retaliated with hundreds of long-range ballistic missiles that have already caused massive damage to Israel's civilian population and infrastructure, resulting in ten lives lost and more than 130 injured. The Jewish Agency has supported the people of Israel during times of crisis for decades and, in coordination with national emergency authorities, local municipalities, beneficiaries, and partners, has identified emergency needs. The Emergency Fund consists of three elements: (a) financial assistance to bereaved families, injured parties, and property destruction—immediate and rehabilitation grants; (b) special assistance and group interventions, in partnership with municipalities, for those who lost homes/evacuees, including resilience strengthening; (c) assistance for populations requiring extra support (elderly, children). The Jewish Agency also has two beneficiary populations under its care: new immigrants in Absorption Centers and elderly residents of Amigour Housing. Amigour residents have been unable to leave their homes to buy food or essentials and need food packages; Olim in absorption centers are spending long hours in shelters and need activities for children and mental health assistance. Separately, there are approximately 6,000 young people in Israel on Israel Experience programs (Masa, Onward, Birthright); many face challenges as they are stuck for an unknown length of time, some need temporary housing, and many will require financial assistance to fly to Athens or Cyprus to return home as airlines resume routes.
