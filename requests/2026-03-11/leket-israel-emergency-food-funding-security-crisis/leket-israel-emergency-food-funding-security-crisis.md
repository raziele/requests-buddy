## The Ask

Leket Israel, Israel's national food bank, is requesting emergency funding to sustain food distribution during the current security crisis. The war with Iran has severely disrupted food rescue operations: rocket fire has paused volunteer activity and limited collection and distribution; farmers have halted harvests; corporate offices are closed; and IDF bases—Leket's largest suppliers of cooked surplus food—are not permitting entry. As a result, surplus food has declined sharply. Leket has seen daily meal distribution drop from 8,000 to 3,000 and must temporarily purchase an additional 5,000 meals per day plus significant produce to maintain supply for hundreds of nonprofit partners. The organization is also providing supermarket vouchers for families who cannot reach distribution points. They are asking the Jewish Federation of Greater Philadelphia for emergency support to help meet an additional $508,210 per week needed to maintain these operations.

**Funding Requested:** $508,210 per week

## Context & Background

Since the outbreak of war with Iran, daily life across Israel has been marked by deep uncertainty: sirens interrupt routines, families move in and out of shelters, and the instability touches nearly every part of daily life. Leket frames food security as essential to resilience—when parents know nourishing food will be on the table, they are better able to care for their children and face the challenges of this period. As Israel's national food bank, Leket is committed to ensuring that vulnerable families continue to receive a reliable supply of nutritious food even during national crises, and views consistent access to food during war as essential to protecting health, reducing trauma, and maintaining the social fabric that helps communities endure and recover.

However, the current security situation has significantly disrupted the networks that enable food rescue. Rocket fire has forced a pause in volunteer activity and limited collection and distribution operations. Farmers have halted harvests, corporate offices are closed, and IDF bases—Leket's largest suppliers of cooked food—are not permitting entry. The supply of surplus food available for rescue has declined sharply. Despite this, Leket's team is working to maintain regular distribution levels and, where possible, increase them. To do so, Leket is temporarily supplementing rescued food with purchased food and providing supermarket vouchers to support families who require assistance but cannot access distribution points.

The funding gap is driven by two main areas: meals (the sharp drop in surplus meals reduced daily distribution from 8,000 to 3,000; to maintain supply, Leket must purchase an additional 5,000 meals daily) and produce (farmers' inability to harvest since the escalation has drastically reduced produce available for rescue, requiring significant temporary purchases). The organization notes that this outreach addresses additional emergency needs created by the current situation, separate from several grant requests already under review with the Federation. They are asking for any emergency support the Federation can provide to sustain these efforts so that vulnerable Israelis continue to receive the food they depend on during this critical period.
