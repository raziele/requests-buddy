The organization has launched a national Emergency Hotline for families and professionals, virtual support sessions in Hebrew and Arabic, staff wellness and practical support, and educational/therapeutic continuity for students. They estimate $150,000–$200,000 (USD) per 10–14 days of conflict to maintain these services, and are also absorbing significant lost income and full salary retention for staff unable to work.

**Funding Requested:** $150,000–$200,000 USD per 10–14 days for emergency response; additional context: ~$100,000/week lost income, ~$230,000/week for unreduced staff salaries

## Context & Background

Israel is under attack again, and for people with disabilities and their families the crisis is especially destabilizing. Many cannot move quickly, interpret urgency, or process what is happening during sirens; after alarms, routines collapse and anxiety and trauma persist. Beit Issie Shapiro has responded with a national Emergency Hotline (WhatsApp then phone/video) for real-time emotional support and professional consultation; twice-weekly virtual support sessions for Beit Issie families in Hebrew and Arabic; partnership with the Shalem Foundation and the Ministry of Welfare for open online guidance sessions for parents and caregivers nationwide; emotional and practical support for staff (including at Ra'anana and Qalanswa); and virtual classes, home visits, and take-home kits for educational and therapeutic continuity.

Needs are growing by the hour. The organization estimates that sustaining this emergency response requires $150,000–$200,000 USD for every 10–14 days the conflict continues. In addition, Beit Issie is facing at least $100,000 per week in lost income from closed services and cancelled training, and has chosen to maintain full salaries for employees who cannot work under current circumstances at a cost of approximately $230,000 per week. They are asking for support so that people with disabilities, their families, and staff are not left to face the crisis alone.

# Emergency Update: Supporting People with Disabilities During the Current Security Crisis – March 2026

Dear Friends,

As Israel once again comes under attack, fear and uncertainty grip the entire country. For the children and adults with disabilities we serve and for their families, these moments are not only terrifying; they are deeply destabilizing.

When sirens wail, most people can instinctively process what is happening, decide how to respond, and seek safety. For many people with disabilities, this is far more complex. Some cannot move quickly or independently. Others struggle to understand what is happening, to interpret the urgency of the situation, or to read and respond to the reactions of those around them. Even when the sirens fall silent, the impact does not end. Routines collapse, familiar anchors disappear, and anxiety, confusion, and trauma often intensify long after the immediate danger has passed.

## What we are doing right now

### 1. Emergency Hotline
We have launched a national Emergency Hotline for professionals, caregivers and families of people with disabilities. Initial contact is made via WhatsApp, with follow-up by phone or video, providing free, real-time emotional support and professional consultation. Since its launch, the hotline has supported parents, adults with disabilities and staff in residential and community frameworks. Responses have included immediate emotional guidance, practical coping strategies, referrals for continued therapy and, where needed, extended consultations and group Zoom guidance sessions. The hotline serves as a central national support channel during this period of heightened stress and uncertainty.

### 2. Virtual Support Sessions for Families
Twice weekly, for as long as the conflict continues, we are holding virtual support sessions for Beit Issie families at two different times of day to maximize participation. Each session includes guidance from a leading mental health professional, together with staff from our school, early intervention center, or other relevant departments. Sessions are offered in both Hebrew and Arabic, providing families with practical tools, emotional support, and a sense of connection during an extremely isolating time.

On a national level, Beit Issie Shapiro is also partnering with the Shalem Foundation, together with the Ministry of Welfare and Social Affairs and local municipalities, to deliver a series of four open online guidance sessions for parents and caregivers of children and adults with disabilities. Offered in both Hebrew and Arabic, these sessions focus on practical strategies for coping with stress, supporting children and adults with disabilities during emergency situations, and strengthening family resilience. The webinars are led by experienced professionals from welfare and community services across the country and are open to families nationwide seeking support during this challenging time.

### 3. Emotional and Practical Support for Staff
While supporting thousands of families, our own staff are facing the same dangers and uncertainties: spouses called up for reserve duty, damaged homes, loved ones stranded abroad, and children at home for extended periods. Our social workers at our campuses in Ra'anana and Qalanswa are providing emotional support and professional guidance to staff coping with trauma, injury, or loss. In parallel, we are offering practical assistance, including meals, children's activity and game packages, virtual entertainment for children, wellness sessions such as yoga, and small but meaningful care packages for all staff, as an expression of solidarity, appreciation, and care.

### 4. Educational and Therapeutic Support for Children
Whenever possible, we are providing virtual classes and activities for students in our special education school, alongside home visits by teachers and therapists in accordance with safety regulations. Staff are also preparing emergency virtual activities and take-home kits to help families maintain therapeutic and educational continuity during prolonged disruptions.

---

The situation is evolving daily, and needs are growing by the hour. We currently estimate that Beit Issie Shapiro's emergency response will require **$US150,000–$US200,000** for every 10–14 days the conflict continues, in order to sustain these critical services. Your support enables us to respond immediately, flexibly, and with compassion, so that people with disabilities, their families, and our dedicated staff are not left to face this crisis alone.

In addition to these emergency efforts, Beit Issie Shapiro is experiencing a significant loss of income due to the closure of services, outpatient clinics, and the postponement or cancellation of professional courses and training programs, amounting to **at least $US100,000 per week** in planned income that cannot currently be realized. At the same time, as part of our commitment to our staff and their families, Beit Issie Shapiro has chosen not to reduce salaries, even for employees who are unable to work under the current circumstances, at a cost of **approximately $US230,000 per week**. From past crises, we know that this decision provides immeasurable strength and a measure of calm in the storm for our colleagues and their families.

Thank you for standing with us when it matters most, and for your consideration of the Beit Issie Shapiro community's needs at this critical time.

## Impact Stories: Emotional Support Through the Emergency Hotline

### Easing Severe Anxiety
A 43-year-old man with borderline intellectual disability who uses a wheelchair has been experiencing severe anxiety since the outbreak of the war. His mother reached out to Beit Issie Shapiro's support hotline seeking guidance. Shafi Mashiach, Director of our Dual Diagnosis Unit, spoke with him directly. Although he has verbal communication abilities, his language is limited, making it difficult for him to express the intensity of his fears. During the conversation, Shafi gently guided him through calming breathing techniques designed to help regulate anxiety. By the end of the call, he had learned practical tools to help manage his stress, and a follow-up conversation was scheduled to ensure continued support.

### Helping a Child Feel Safe Again
A single mother of three children reached out for guidance after a frightening experience during a recent siren. One of her children, who lives with autism and cerebral palsy, had fallen while rushing to the shelter. Since then, he has refused to return there. Dafna, an Occupational Therapist from our Dual Diagnosis Unit, provided the mother with practical strategies to help rebuild the child's sense of safety—e.g. gradually reintroducing the shelter at calm times with enjoyable activities like soap bubbles—so the space becomes associated with positive experiences rather than fear. For families already coping with the daily realities of disability, timely professional guidance and compassionate support can make a profound difference.
