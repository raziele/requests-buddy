## The Ask

United Hatzalah requests a $1,000,000 grant from the Jewish Federation Bay Area to establish four new community-based volunteer emergency medical teams ("Medical Kitot Konenut" / Community First Response teams). Each team will consist of 25 volunteer medics (100 total), trained and equipped to provide free, on-scene emergency care within minutes. The funding covers 200-hour EMT training, complete medic bags and communication devices, and one year of volunteer support. The proposal emphasizes strengthened disaster preparedness and mass-casualty response capability in the wake of October 7 and ongoing security threats, with an anticipated impact of 24,000+ citizens assisted annually by the new teams.

**Funding Requested:** $1,000,000

**Funding Breakdown:**
- Recruitment & Training (200-hour EMT training, recruitment, learning materials & equipment, tests, certification): $120,000
- Medical & Dispatch Equipment (complete medic bag and communication device, including apps and licenses): $680,000
- One Year Volunteer Support (internship & 1-year support: continued education, medical supply refill, insurance, recognition events, post-incident support): $200,000

## Context & Background

United Hatzalah is Israel's only nationwide, fully volunteer emergency medical service (EMS). Volunteers are trained and equipped to provide free on-scene treatment in the critical window before traditional ambulance arrival; with dispatch technology and rapid-response vehicles, they often arrive within three minutes (as fast as 90 seconds in metropolitan areas). The National Dispatch Center received over 800,000 emergency calls in 2025, and the organization aims to expand to 16,000 medics with a 90-second response goal nationwide.

On October 7, 2023, United Hatzalah volunteers established field hospitals, entered terrorist-controlled kibbutzim, treated over 4,700 civilians and soldiers, provided psychological first aid to 2,700 Israelis, and evacuated 1,250 injured patients. Post-incident analysis highlighted the need to strengthen EMS capabilities and preparedness for mass-casualty incidents (MCIs) through community-based volunteer teams that can coordinate effectively when communication is disrupted or external backup is delayed.

Israeli citizens have faced heavy rocket fire for over two years, and the average ambulance response time in Israel is eleven minutes in cities (longer in rural areas), while the human brain can survive only four to six minutes without oxygen. Community first response teams ensure that volunteer medics who live and work in an area can triage and provide lifesaving care in the first moments of a major emergency, supporting disaster preparedness and local resilience.

## Medical "Kitot Konenut"
## Establish Community First Response Teams
### Presented to the Jewish Federation Bay Area
### February 2026

United Hatzalah is Israel's only nationwide, fully volunteer emergency medical service (EMS), and our mission is to save lives. We train and equip ordinary citizens, so they can provide free on-scene medical treatment to anyone in need during the critical window of time between the onset of the crisis and arrival of traditional ambulance assistance. While ambulances are often delayed, United Hatzalah volunteers are always nearby, giving choking, drowning, cardiac arrest, and other victims the best chance of survival. With our unique dispatch technology, innovative rapid response vehicles, and community-based volunteer network, our volunteers arrive to emergencies within minutes, and at times as fast as 90 seconds in metropolitan areas. In 2025, our National Dispatch Center received over 800,000 emergency calls. As the number of calls continues to rise, we are working to expand our volunteer network to 16,000 medics, with the ambitious goal of achieving a 90-second response for every emergency, anywhere in Israel.

On October 7, 2023, during the biggest catastrophe in Israel's history, our unique EMS model proved to be an indispensable resource; our brave volunteers entered the inferno in the early hours of Shabbat morning, establishing field hospitals and entering terrorist-controlled kibbutzim, treating thousands of people and saving hundreds of lives that day. We treated over 4,700 civilians and soldiers, provided psychological first aid to 2,700 Israelis suffering from emotional trauma, and evacuated 1,250 injured patients to hospitals via ambulance and helicopter. United Hatzalah's response was quick and coordinated, despite the many security, medical, and logistical challenges.

Since then, the organization has analyzed and debriefed hundreds of hours of phone calls, video footage, and reports from our emergency response that day, all of which point to the highest level of professionalism and unsurpassed volunteer dedication. This thorough assessment has also highlighted the need to further strengthen our EMS capabilities and preparedness for mass-casualty incidents (MCIs), ensuring that community-based teams of volunteer medics can come together effectively to save lives.

A grant of $1,000,000 will enable us to recruit, train, and equip four new community-based volunteer teams to serve as medical "kitot konenut". Each new team will consist of 25 volunteer medics, a total of 100 volunteers, ensuring that more of Israel's people receive fast, free, professional emergency medical care — on time, every time.

**The challenge: response times**

Medical emergencies strike at any time and can result in long-term disability or fatality if help does not arrive on time. While the human brain can only survive for four to six minutes without oxygen, the average ambulance response time in Israel is eleven minutes in cities and longer in rural areas. Dispatched from a fixed base, often far from the site of the emergency, and further delayed by traffic, traditional ambulances often arrive too late. United Hatzalah's national network of volunteer medics — equipped with cutting-edge communication devices and innovative rapid response vehicles — arrive within three minutes or less to perform lifesaving treatment until the arrival of an ambulance. Our volunteers have been dubbed "Angels in Orange" after their trademark orange jackets.

Given the heightened threat of war, the need to train and equip additional medics across Israel to respond to both everyday emergencies and mass casualty incidents has never been more urgent. During large-scale emergencies, rapid response often depends on trained community-based teams that can immediately come together to manage mass casualty incidents where communication is disrupted or external backup cannot arrive in time. Establishing and strengthening local community response teams ensures that volunteer medics who live and work in the area are prepared to coordinate, triage, and provide lifesaving care during the critical first moments of a major emergency.

**New volunteer team sponsorship components**

- 200-hour EMT course, covering emergency scenarios and medical treatment for every type of illness, accident, and act of violence, including written and practical tests.
- Complete medic bags (bandages, tourniquets, IVs, oxygen tanks, EpiPen injections, glucometers, defibrillators, and more).
- Communication devices for dispatch to identify and alert volunteers to nearby emergencies and coordinate response.
- Internship period: 100 supervised emergency calls.
- One year volunteer support: refresher courses, emergency drills, team meetings, volunteer insurance, medical supply refills, volunteer retention activities, and post-incident psychological counseling.

**Anticipated impact**

- As each volunteer responds to an average of 20 emergencies monthly, the new medic teams will assist 24,000+ citizens annually for years to come.
- Enhanced disaster preparedness and community resilience.
