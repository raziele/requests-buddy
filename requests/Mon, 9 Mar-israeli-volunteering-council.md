## The Ask

The Israeli Volunteering Council is seeking $179,000 to equip Volunteer Leaders in 10 Arab municipalities with the tools and training needed to provide emergency support. This includes leadership training, distribution of group shelter kits and individual creative kits, and a knitting project where older adults create octopus dolls for children. The initiative aims to strengthen community resilience and provide psychological relief.

**Funding Requested:** $179,000

**Funding Breakdown:**
- Leadership Training Workshops: $25,000
- Group Shelter Kits: $50,000
- Individual Creative Kits: $80,000
- Octopus Knitting Kits: $9,000
- Logistics & Volunteer Coordination: $15,000

## Context & Background

Arab municipalities in Israel face a critical shortage of private bomb shelters and have a lower rate of public shelters compared to the national average. These communities are already carrying heavy burdens from prolonged internal violence and organized crime, making them more vulnerable to the current escalation. Logistical challenges, language barriers, and the difficulty of navigating formal systems under pressure often hinder aid delivery.

The Israeli Volunteering Council proposes a solution by activating and strengthening its existing infrastructure of trusted Volunteer Leaders within these 10 Arab municipalities. The initiative will operate through three complementary pillars:

1.  **Emergency Leadership Training:** Providing intensive training to Volunteer Leaders on managing volunteerism in emergencies, including personal and community resilience, working with traumatized populations, emergency protocols, community mapping, and strengthening community cohesion.
2.  **Distribution of Activity Kits:** Volunteer Leaders will manage the distribution of group shelter kits (for public shelters) and individual creative kits (for homebound/disabled children).
3.  **Knitting for the Children, Meaningful Activity for Older Adults:** Older adults in shelters will create hand-knitted octopus dolls for emotionally struggling children, providing purposeful activity for themselves and comfort for the children.

The total project cost is $179,000, which will equip leaders with tools and training to deliver aid effectively. The initiative aims to build lasting community resilience beyond the crisis.

When the Siren Sounds:
Emergency Kits for Children in Arab Municipalities
Ensuring no child is left behind - in shelter or at home.

Imagine a public parking lot in an Arab town in northern Israel. It is 2 AM. The sirens have
just sounded, yet again. Three hundred people are huddled between cars. There is no
private shelter, no activities, no plan. Among them, forty children.

This is not a hypothetical. It is the nightly reality for thousands of Arab families across Israel.
Unlike many communities, Arab localities have a critical shortage of private bomb shelters
(Mamads), or building-based shelters, forcing families to seek refuge in crowded public spaces:
schools, community centers, kindergartens, and parking lots, that are wholly unprepared for the
long hours of waiting, anxiety, and idleness that follow each alarm.

The Israeli Volunteering Council is uniquely positioned to respond. We already operate active
Volunteer Leader programs in 10 Arab municipalities. The trusted relationships, the local
knowledge, and the community infrastructure are already in place. Your investment activates
them.

The Context: A Community Facing Layered Vulnerability
To understand the urgency of this intervention, it is important to recognize the cumulative
pressures that Arab society in Israel was already carrying before the current escalation.

A Community Already Carrying Heavy Burdens
Before the war, Arab communities across Israel were living through a prolonged and deeply
damaging wave of internal violence. Years of gang-related shootings, organized crime, and
widespread intimidation left families in a state of chronic fear. For children in these towns, the
arrival of war sirens was not a disruption of normal life, it was one more layer added to lives
already marked by insecurity.

KEY
FACT
Arab municipalities in Israel have a significantly lower rate of private bomb
shelters compared to the national average. Approximately 46% of Arab
residents in Israel live in buildings without standard protective spaces,
compared to 26% of the general population. Of Israel's 11,775 public shelters,
only 37 are located in Arab municipalities, and 8 of those are unfit for use.
(Israel Democracy Institute, 2024)

The Resource Gap: Why Aid Does Not Always Arrive
In emergencies, communities with fewer private shelters and greater language barriers are often
slower to receive organized support, not due to intent, but due to logistics: distance from central
distribution hubs, language gaps, and the difficulty of navigating formal systems under pressure.

Pillar I: Emergency Leadership Training
Before and during the distribution, the Israeli Volunteering Council's professional staff will
provide intensive training sessions for Volunteer Leaders on managing volunteerism in
emergencies. Training topics include:
•
Personal and community resilience: tools for leaders to manage their own stress while
supporting others
•
Sense of capability: Do's and Don'ts when working with traumatized populations and
children under stress
•
Emergency protocols: case-study workshops: real shelter scenarios with structured
response frameworks
•
Community mapping: identifying 'invisible' residents: the elderly, people with disabilities,
isolated families
•
Strengthening community cohesion: practical tools for creating stability and normalcy
during prolonged emergencies

Pillar III: Knitting for the Children, Meaningful Activity for Older Adults in Shelters
Long hours in public shelters are difficult for everyone, but especially for older adults, who often
experience prolonged waiting with no structured activity, no sense of contribution, and little to
occupy their hands or minds.

This pillar addresses that reality directly. Older adults in public shelters will receive knitting kits
and simple, illustrated instructions to knit octopus dolls, small, soft figures with documented
therapeutic benefits for young children. The octopuses they create will be distributed to children
in the community who are struggling emotionally with the ongoing emergency situation.

There is no complex model here. The logic is straightforward: older adults benefit from having
something purposeful to do with their time in the shelter, and children benefit from receiving a
hand-made gift made by someone in their own community.

THE
POWER
OF THIS
PILLAR
Long hours in a shelter are hard for everyone, but especially for older adults,
who may be less mobile and more sensitive to the surrounding anxiety. Giving
them something to make with their hands, for a child nearby, turns idle waiting
into something purposeful. A small gift, made by a neighbor. That is enough.

Each knitting kit contains yarn, knitting needles, embroidery thread and needle, marker, filling
material, and illustrated knitting instructions.
