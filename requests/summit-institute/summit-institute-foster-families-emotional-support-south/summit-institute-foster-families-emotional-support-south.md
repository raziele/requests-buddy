## The Ask

Summit Institute (Makhon Summit), an Israeli nonprofit operating for over five decades in trauma, mental health, and family support, is requesting funding to provide enhanced emotional support (ליווי רגשי מוגבר) for foster children and foster families in the South. Recent security escalation and the outbreak of the campaign with Iran have sharply intensified emotional, functional, and family challenges for foster families in the region, including those caring for children with special needs. The project would deliver intensive professional wraparound support—ongoing accompaniment by certified foster-care social workers, parent guidance, crisis intervention, and a dedicated coordinator for special needs—to strengthen families, prevent placement breakdown, and ensure stable care for vulnerable children. Estimated additional cost is approximately 1,700 ILS per child for the coming months.

**Funding Requested:** Approximately 1,700 ₪ per child for the coming months (total project amount not stated in document)

## Context & Background

Summit Institute has operated for over five decades at the intersection of trauma, mental health, and family in Israel. It accompanies children, women, youth, and adults at their most fragile moments and helps them rebuild stability, functioning, and meaning. In Israel's reality of security tension, uncertainty, and persistent social and economic gaps, some populations' distress does not fade and sometimes intensifies.

Foster children often have histories of neglect, violence, abandonment, and ongoing trauma; placement in foster care itself is a major disruption. Since October 7 and the recent tense weeks—including the outbreak of the campaign with Iran—an additional layer of trauma has been added. For children already accustomed to emotional "earthquakes," every disruption returns them backward. Some foster children in the South remain in regression: anxiety, sleep difficulties, intense fears, outbursts, learning difficulties, and functional decline. When sirens and uncertainty returned, children who had begun to rebuild stability were destabilized again. Placement breakdown means further harm, possible re-placement or institutional care, and deepened trauma; the public system does not provide sufficiently intensive, tailored support.

The proposed response is enhanced emotional support for approximately 100 foster families and children in the South (including families caring for children with special needs) through deeper professional wraparound: ongoing accompaniment by certified foster-care social workers trained in special needs, parent guidance, crisis intervention, strengthened daily coping tools, and a dedicated coordinator for special needs who supports staff and leads learning. The project also includes enhanced professional development for staff. Summit operates foster care services in Jerusalem and the South under the Ministry of Welfare, currently responsible for about 1,400 children in roughly 900 foster and emergency-care families and serves about 2,000 beneficiaries annually.
