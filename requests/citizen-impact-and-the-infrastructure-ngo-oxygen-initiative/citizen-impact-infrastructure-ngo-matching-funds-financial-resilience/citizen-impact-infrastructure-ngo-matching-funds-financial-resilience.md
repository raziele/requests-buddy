## The Ask

Citizen Impact and The Infrastructure NGO are seeking matching funds to scale two year-long financial resilience programs in Israel: one for active-duty soldiers and one for war-affected households. The proposal emphasizes continuous personalized support, coordinated service delivery, and measurable recovery outcomes, with a remaining combined funding gap needed to sustain and expand coverage.

**Funding Requested:** $411K

**Funding Breakdown:**
- IDF Soldiers Program matching requirement: $260K
- War-Affected Households Program remaining gap: $151K

**Non-Financial Ask:** Partnership and stakeholder participation to help close matching gaps and enable implementation at scale

## Context & Background

The attached proposal presents two complementary, long-term interventions under the "Oxygen" initiative to address financial vulnerability at different life stages in Israel. One track focuses on active-duty soldiers who are already in financial distress and need structured support to reach stable, independent financial functioning. The second track addresses households affected by the war through a broad recovery framework that combines financial, employment, rights-access, and emotional dimensions.

For soldiers, the program is designed as a national model delivering 12 months of continuous, personalized support that combines human guidance with AI tools. It includes financial assessment, an individualized action plan, and implementation support across income growth, debt management, expense optimization, and rights realization. The scale target is 500 soldiers per year over a three-year national framework. Funding information in the document lists $650K approved and a required $260K match (40%).

For war-affected households, the program positions itself as a one-stop, year-long recovery system with adaptive support, holistic assessment, and hands-on implementation rather than referral-only service. It reports 2,500+ households already supported and seeks expansion to an additional 650 households. The budget indicates a cost of $650 per household, $325 secured through Ogen, $211K matching required, $60K already committed by the Detroit Federation, and a remaining $151K gap.

Overall, the request is time-sensitive because both programs are already operating or positioned for immediate scale, and the organizations frame the remaining match as the key blocker to delivering sustained resilience outcomes for soldiers and war-affected families.
