NATIONAL RESILIENCE BEGINS AT HOME
Inner Forces (HaNefesh HaAhat) – Serving the Families Who Serve
Approximately 500,000 service members took part in the October 7th war, meaning nearly 30% of Israel's population (parents, siblings, spouses, children, grandparents) have been living for over two years in a state of constant anxiety. While efforts and resources are allocated to support soldiers, their families remain largely overlooked. Inner Forces was established to fill this gap.
As the initial intensity of the war gives way to a new reality, the real challenges for many families are only beginning. With public mental health systems and state services already overwhelmed and insufficient support available, targeted community-based support for families of soldiers is an urgent national necessity.
Inner Forces provides a holistic support framework for families of soldiers and reservists based on empirical knowledge (including vast fieldwork and input from our national mothers' community) and academic research. We operate under a core belief: when families are resilient, soldiers are resilient.
We operate along five core pillars: Resilience, Rights Advocacy, Community, Economic Stability, and Education.
Our leadership team consists of mothers of combat soldiers with professional backgrounds in healthcare, education, and social innovation. This combination fosters community trust and enables effective program delivery. We would appreciate the opportunity to meet and discuss our initiatives further.
1. Resilience Programs – From Concern to Community Action
Our Community Resilience Ambassador Program is a structured, research-based annual model implemented in partnership with local municipalities. Designed for mothers of combat soldiers, it provides year-long professional support that strengthens personal and family resilience while preparing participants to serve as Resilience Ambassadors within their communities. Through this model, graduates go on to volunteer locally in partnership with municipal authorities, supporting service members and their families.
The program is accompanied by ongoing research conducted with Ruppin Academic Center, ensuring measurable outcomes and continuous evidence-based refinement.
Participants report significant improvement in well-being and resilience.
2025 Achievements: Four cohorts for soldiers' mothers, including many mothers of injured soldiers, resulting in significant improvement in overall well-being.
2026 Plan: To launch 10 additional cohorts to expand our program by adapting the model for fathers and wives, and to deliver 100+ workshops and retreats.
In collaboration with Ruppin Academic Center, we conducted a study on mothers of soldiers in combat and combat-support roles. Findings show they experience ambiguous loss trauma affecting multiple areas of life, including employment, while peer support emerged as a key resilience factor. The research highlights that strengthening mothers' and families' resilience directly contributes to the resilience of the soldiers themselves.
What participants say:
- "The program helped me release constant tension, gain practical tools, and reconnect. It gave me hope."
- "We created a supportive community of strong mothers who are always there for each other."

2. Occupational Rehabilitation & Economic Stability
Approximately 20% of mothers of combat soldiers report reduced employment or job loss, and about 45% of reservists' spouses report changes in employment status during the war, reflecting the significant employment impact of prolonged emotional strain on soldiers' families.
In response, we developed an occupational resilience program that provides trauma-informed career counseling, employment navigation support, and practical guidance on employment retention, helping participants return to work while adapting to shifting employment conditions and new family responsibilities.
2026 Plan: Launch five occupational resilience groups for mothers and reservists' spouses in collaboration with local municipalities and the business sector. In parallel, we work with employers, equipping them with trauma awareness and practical tools to strengthen their ability to support affected employees.
3. Rights Advocacy
We provide guidance to families navigating bureaucratic systems, backed by a comprehensive national resource database.
2026 Plan: Expansion and Scaling – We intend to implement a CRM for secure case management to enhance family support and enable data-driven advocacy. To support this, we need funding for a case manager to oversee our expanding volunteer network.
4. Community Building & Local Resilience
We have built a dynamic nationwide community that is active both online and through in-person meetings. This infrastructure creates a vital mutual support system that allows us to identify and respond to real-time needs emerging directly from the field.
2026 Plan: We aim to launch three local communities in partnership with municipalities, each led by a trained Community Manager. To scale up our impact, we plan to develop a digital platform that would ensure rapid access to information, active redirection to the appropriate organizations, and mutual support.
5. Education, Adolescents & Family Cohesion
Israeli adolescents are navigating a continuous crisis, marching from the COVID-19 pandemic directly into war. Addressing the erosion of communication between soldiers and their families is vital for maintaining resilience.
2026 Plan: To launch family communication workshops for 11th–12th graders and their parents to strengthen familial bonds and improve communication before enlistment.
