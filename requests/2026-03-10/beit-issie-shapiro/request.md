Dear Friends

,

As Israel once again comes under attack, fear and uncertainty grip the entire country. For the
children
and adults with disabilities we serve
and for their families, these moments are not only terrifying; they
are deeply destabilizing.

When sirens wail, most people can instinctively process what is happening, decide how to respond, and
seek safety.

For many people with disabilities, this is far more complex. Some cannot move quickly or
independently. Others struggle to understand what is happening, to interpret the urgency of the
situation, or to read and respond to the reactions of those around them
. Even when the sirens fall silent,
the impact does not end. Routines collapse, familiar anchors disappear, and anxiety, confusion, and
trauma often intensify long after the immediate danger has passed.

What we are doing right now:

Emergency Hotline

with
disabilities. Initial contact is made via WhatsApp, with follow

time emotional support and professional consultatio
n.

Since its launch, the hotline has supported
parents, adults with disabilities and staff in residential and community frameworks. Responses have
included immediate emotional guidance, practical coping strategies, referrals for continued therapy and,
wher
e needed, extended consultations and group Zoom guidance sessions.
The hotline serves as a
central national support channel during this period of heightened stress and uncertainty.

2.

Virtual Support Sessions for Families

Twice weekly, for as long as the

Impact Stories:

Emotional Support

T
hrough
the
Emergency Hotline

To illustrate how these efforts are making a difference in real time, we would like to share a few
examples
from the many calls and consultations our teams are currently handling through the Emergency Hotline.

Easing Severe Anxiety

A 43
year
old man with borderline intellectual disability who uses a wheelchair has been experiencing
severe
anxiety since the outbreak of the war. His mother reached out to Beit Issie Shapiro's support
hotline seeking guidance.

Shafi Mashiach, Director of our Dual Diagnosis Unit, spoke with him directly. Although he has verbal
communication abilities, his langu
age is limited, making it difficult for him to express the intensity of his
fears.

During the conversation, Shafi gently guided him through calming breathing techniques designed to help
regulate anxiety. When meeting clients in person, she often uses
simple imagery to make the technique
accessible, for example, encouraging them to "breathe in as if smelling a flower." While explaining this
concept over the phone was more challenging, she carefully adapted the guidance to ensure he could
understand and
practice the technique. By the end of the call, he had learned practical tools to help
manage his stress, and a follow
up conversation was scheduled to ensure continued support.

Helping a Child Feel Safe Again

A single mother of three children reached out
for guidance after a frightening experience during a recent
siren. One of her children, who lives with autism and cerebral palsy, had fallen while rushing to the
shelter. Since then, he has refused to return there, leaving his mother deeply worried about
how to keep
him safe during future alarms.

Dafna, an Occupational Therapist from our Dual Diagnosis Unit, provided the mother with practical
strategies to help rebuild the child's sense of safety. She suggested gradually reintroducing the shelter
environm
ent at calm times, when there is no siren, so the space can become associated with positive
experiences rather than fear.

For example, she recommended bringing activities the child enjoys, such as blowing soap bubbles, to
create a relaxed and comforting a
tmosphere. These simple but concrete tools gave the mother a way to
gently support her child in overcoming the trauma of the fall.

For families already coping with the daily realities of disability, the additional pressures of war, especially
for those wi
thout protected rooms or shelters at home, can feel overwhelming. In these moments,
timely professional guidance and compassionate support can make a profound difference.
