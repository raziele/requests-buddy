## The Ask

Summit Institute is requesting urgent funding to provide enhanced emotional support to 100 foster families and children in Southern Israel, particularly those with special needs, who have been severely impacted by the ongoing conflict. The funding will cover a comprehensive program of parental guidance, professional interventions, and specialized training for foster parents.

**Funding Requested:** Not specified

## Context & Background

The Summit Institute, an organization with over five decades of experience in trauma and mental health, is providing critical support to foster children and families in Israel. The current conflict has exacerbated the existing vulnerabilities of these children, many of whom have already experienced neglect, abuse, and family separation. The recent escalation of hostilities, including missile attacks, has led to a regression in the children's emotional and functional well-being. Summit's 'enhanced emotional support' project aims to provide a stable, supportive environment for these children, mitigating further trauma and promoting their recovery. This initiative is particularly crucial for children with special needs who require specialized care and a consistent routine, which has been disrupted by the ongoing security situation.

### עמותת מכון סאמיט - מרץ 26 -ליווי רגשי מוגבר לילדי האומנה בדרום.pdf

**About Summit Institute:**
The Summit Institute (an NPO) was established in 1973 and has been active for over five decades in the fields of mental rehabilitation and foster care in Israel. It currently accompanies approximately 600 individuals with mental health challenges. Since 2003, the Institute has operated foster care services in the Jerusalem and Southern districts, under the supervision of the Ministry of Welfare, with a focus on children with special needs. Currently, the Institute is responsible for approximately 1,400 children being raised in about 900 foster families and emergency shelters. Annually, the Institute provides services to approximately 2,000 beneficiaries through a broad professional network of social workers, foster care mentors, professional coordinators, and complementary treatment personnel. The Chairman of the Institute's Friends Association is Mr. Alon Haimovitz, CEO of Microsoft Israel.

**The Need:**
Children placed in foster families have often experienced neglect, violence, abandonment, and ongoing trauma from a young age. The transition from their biological homes, sometimes through emergency centers and then to foster families, constitutes a profound upheaval in their lives. They have already experienced loss, separation, and a basic breach of security. Placement in foster families entails daily complex challenges. Since October 7th, an additional layer of trauma has been added to these children. For children already accustomed to emotional "earthquakes," any disruption to their routine brings them back to square one. Some foster children in the South are currently exhibiting regression, manifested in anxiety, sleep difficulties, intense fears, outbursts of anger, learning difficulties, and functional decline.

**The Current Situation:**
Just as a sense of stability was beginning to be rebuilt, with some children returning to educational frameworks and cautiously resuming their routine, the alarms, stress, and uncertainty have returned. For children who have already experienced loss of home and family, any further upheaval undermines their fundamental sense of security, which is built with great effort. For example, Osher, a 9-year-old boy from Sderot, who came to a foster family after difficult experiences in his biological home, only recently began to show improvement in his nighttime fears and difficulty adjusting to returning to Sderot. After months of consistent emotional support, significant progress was made. However, with the renewal of tensions and security incidents, regression is evident again – a return to nighttime bedwetting, fear of falling asleep, and fear of being alone.

The collapse of a foster family means further harm to the child, the need for re-placement or transfer to an institutional setting, and deepening of the trauma, emotional damage, and developmental harm. The existing public system does not provide adequate intensive and tailored support for these needs, and specialized reinforcement of family and professional support is required. Creating stability and routine is not a luxury for these children – it is a fundamental condition for healthy emotional development. Children who have already experienced a breakdown need a consistent, predictable, and supportive environment to rebuild trust in the world and those around them. Therefore, especially during this difficult time, it is crucial to continue providing ongoing emotional support, early professional intervention, and enhanced continuous support.

**The Proposed Solution - Enhanced Emotional Support:**
This project offers enhanced support to 100 foster families and children from the South, who have experienced significant setbacks since the outbreak of the current conflict with Iran. Some of these are families caring for foster children with special needs. This will be provided through a comprehensive professional framework, including ongoing guidance from foster care mentors who are certified social workers with specialized expertise in working with children with special needs in foster care. The support includes parental training, professional interventions during crises, strengthening daily coping skills, and providing tailored responses to the changing needs of children and families. Additionally, a dedicated coordinator for special needs will lead the work, providing professional backing to the mentors and facilitating learning and enrichment processes.

The program also includes enhanced professional development for the team, aimed at deepening knowledge and skills in working with children with special needs and with foster children in ongoing emergency situations.

The additional cost required for enhanced support per child during the coming months is estimated at NIS 1,700 in total.

Any support will significantly strengthen foster families in the Southern region, including families caring for children with special needs, and will help prevent the collapse of family frameworks and ensure a stable, safe, and supportive continuity of life for children who need it most.
