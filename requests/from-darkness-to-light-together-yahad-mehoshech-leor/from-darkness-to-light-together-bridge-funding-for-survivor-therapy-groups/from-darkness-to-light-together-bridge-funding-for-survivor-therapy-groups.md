## The Ask

From Darkness To Light Together requests immediate small-scale financial support to sustain its long-term group therapy programs for adult survivors of childhood sexual abuse during a difficult operating year. The organization currently runs 18 groups nationwide and seeks both near-term bridge support and longer-term partners to adopt therapy groups or participants so it can maintain existing services, expand to underserved regions, and advance academic validation of its model.

**Funding Requested:** Not specified

**Non-Financial Ask:** Long-term partners to adopt a therapy group or individual participants, plus relationship-building conversations and network support

## Context & Background

The request describes a specialized NGO founded 13 years ago that provides long-term group psychotherapy for men and women who experienced childhood sexual abuse. The founder states that tailored treatment options in Israel remain insufficient despite a high prevalence of childhood sexual abuse (at least 20% of the population, and about 1.4 million survivors cited in the attached materials). The organization reports 18 active groups across Israel, including activity in Yokneam and Karmiel where specialized options are limited.

Their model emphasizes long-duration treatment without arbitrary time limits, gender-inclusive frameworks (mixed groups and women-only options), and professional facilitation by trained clinicians with ongoing supervision. They report recognition by Israel's Ministry of Health and Ministry of Welfare-related frameworks, plus external effectiveness measurement and annual evaluation. The attachments cite outcome indicators from participant questionnaires showing improvements in emotional coping (92%), self-confidence (89%), and overall mood (88.6%).

The organization says it is facing a challenging year financially and is therefore seeking multiple small funding sources in the short term. In parallel, it is launching academic research to validate and institutionalize the therapeutic model in public services and care organizations. The stated rationale is both direct survivor recovery and prevention of intergenerational harm, including reduced secondary traumatization among survivors' children and families.
