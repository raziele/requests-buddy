## The Ask

NATAL is requesting support to scale its emergency trauma response following Operation Lion's Roar (late February 2026), a joint Israel–US preemptive operation against Iran that has caused severe psychological harm and continuous threat across Israel. In the first two days of the crisis, NATAL's Helpline saw a 620% increase in calls, with acute anxiety, panic, and distress—especially at night and among vulnerable populations. NATAL is running both its main Helpline and the Ministry of Defense 'Nefesh Achat' Helpline in emergency mode, has implemented a dedicated clinical protocol, provides interventions in hotels for civilians whose homes were hit, and is delivering training and support to the public and first responders. The organization is directing all resources to emergency response and preparing for long-term trauma support, and is asking partners for support to act swiftly and scale capacity.

## Context & Background

On Saturday, February 28, 2026, a joint preemptive operation by Israel and the United States began against Iran. The resulting psychological needs and damage are described as profoundly severe, with the entire population of Israel exposed to an unprecedented level of continued threat, intense anxiety, and traumatic incidents, compounded by the ongoing impact of events since October 7th.

In the two days since the inception of the latest war with Iran, NATAL's Helpline experienced a 620% increase in calls. The overwhelming majority were related to anxiety and fear due to the situation, particularly at night. Key issues include concern for family members and reservists, difficulty mediating the situation to children, lack of adequate protective shelter for many callers, panic attacks and acute fears of direct physical harm, severe nighttime distress, and a high volume of calls from affected areas including eyewitnesses to direct strikes. The 'Nefesh Achat' Helpline, operated by NATAL on behalf of the Ministry of Defense Rehabilitation Department for disabled IDF veterans, former security personnel, and their families, is also in emergency mode.

The Clinical Unit has implemented a dedicated protocol for new callers and ongoing patients; therapists are observing regression among some patients and maintaining therapy online with proactive contact. A dedicated internal emergency consultation line supports hundreds of NATAL therapists. NATAL is also providing therapeutic intervention in Tel Aviv and Jerusalem hotels for civilians whose homes were directly hit. Community work includes webinars and training on emergency coping and mediation for the public, parents, therapists, and volunteers; situation-relevant training for field therapists; and support for first responders. Media outreach distributes coping tools and raises awareness. NATAL states that in order to continue providing life-saving mental health assistance at an ever-increasing scale, it is directing all organizational resources to emergency response and preparations for long-term support, and that partner support enables swift action and scaling of capacity.

**Dear partners and friends,**

Following two difficult days of relentless sirens and missile fire, lack of sleep, widespread destruction, psychological harm, and continuing uncertainty, we would like to provide our partners and supporters with an update about NATAL's activities.

On Saturday, February 28, 2026, a joint preemptive operation by Israel and the United States began against Iran. It is already becoming clear that the resulting psychological needs and damage are profoundly severe. The entire population of Israel is being exposed to an unprecedented level of continued threat, intense anxiety and traumatic incidents, the effects of which are compounded by the ongoing impact of events since October 7th.

In the 2 days since the inception of the latest war with Iran, **NATAL's Helpline has experienced a 620% increase in calls.** The overwhelming majority of calls were related to anxiety and fear due to the situation, particularly at night. This time, unlike during Operation "Am KeLavi" ("Rising Lion"), the intensity of the sirens has not permitted any respite, placing a particularly heavy burden on children, older adults, people with disabilities, and those coping with mental health conditions.

**Key issues include:**
- Concern for family members and reservists
- Difficulty in mediating and explaining the situation to children
- Lack of adequate protective shelter for many callers
- Panic attacks, acute fears of direct physical harm, and severe nighttime distress
- A high volume of calls from affected areas, including eyewitnesses to direct strikes

Simultaneously, the "Nefesh Achat" ("One Soul") Helpline, operated by NATAL on behalf of the Rehabilitation Department of the Ministry of Defense, is also working in emergency mode. This Helpline provides support to disabled IDF veterans, former security service personnel, and their family members dealing with post-trauma, as well as veterans and reservists experiencing psychological distress and anxiety including due to having to report for reserve duty.

The Clinical Unit has implemented a dedicated protocol for both new callers and ongoing patients. The Unit's therapists are observing regression among some patients, requiring augmented psychiatric support and therapeutic adjustments. All therapy is currently being conducted online, and the clinical teams maintain proactive and continuous contact with patients. Due to the increased caseload and this clinical deterioration, a dedicated internal emergency consultation line has been introduced to provide professional consultations for hundreds of NATAL therapists throughout the war. In addition, NATAL is providing therapeutic intervention in the Tel Aviv and Jerusalem hotels hosting civilians whose homes were directly hit by missile strikes, to offer them ongoing support.

**Community Work and Training**
- We are conducting webinars and training sessions on emergency coping skills and on how to mediate the current situation for the general public, parents, therapists, and volunteers.
- The Multidisciplinary Trauma Studies Center is delivering situation-relevant training to NATAL staff and to therapists in the field.
- We are providing support and emergency interventions for first responders on the front lines.

**Media Outreach**  
We are distributing coping tools for emergency situations across various media platforms and emphasizing the importance of preserving resiliency. At the same time, we are engaging in extensive media outreach to raise public awareness and are organizing specialized training and support for rescue personnel involved in life-saving operations in the field.

In times of national trauma, silence can sometimes be more deafening than any roar. People withdraw, communities contract, and the heart seeks protection. From our many years of professional experience, resilience is not the absence of suffering, it is the ability to remain together in the tremor, and to create meaning even from within a fracture.

In the words of Boaz Shalgi, Chief Psychologist of NATAL: "Uncertainty can weigh heavily upon us, especially when added to accumulated fatigue. Yet the knowledge that our reaction is human and understandable, and that anchors of stability can be created even now, this is part of resilience. Resilience is not the absence of fear, but rather the ability to continue even amidst the turmoil."

In order to continue providing life-saving mental health assistance on an ever-increasing scale, we are now directing all organizational resources to our emergency response, along with preparations for long-term support, as the effects of trauma will continue to reverberate well beyond the restoration of calm. Your support enables us to act swiftly and to scale capacity, with uncompromising professionalism. Thanks to you, we are able to reach thousands of people at their most vulnerable moments.

We will continue to provide ongoing updates and are happy to answer any questions that may arise.

With hope for more peaceful days,  
Jude, Ruben and Efrat
