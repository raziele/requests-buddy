## The Ask

Bizchut, Israel’s leading human rights center for people with disabilities, is requesting an introduction meeting to present its current projects and to explore **emergency funding** for its war-related work. The organization was previously in contact (about a year earlier) with Jewish Federation of Greater Philadelphia regarding possible support and is one of **18 nonprofits recommended** in the North American Jewish Federations (Israel & Overseas Committee Designated Allocations Task Force) report on supporting Israelis with disabilities (attached). Bizchut has pivoted part of its work to wartime disability issues and asks whether the Federation has emergency funds designated for war-time support; any support, including small amounts, would help advance the rights and welfare of people with disabilities.

**Funding Requested:** Not specified

**Non-Financial Ask:** Meeting to present current projects; inquiry whether emergency war-related funds are available for their disability-rights work.

## Context & Background

Bizchut (בזכות) is Israel’s human rights center for people with disabilities, advancing dignity, equality, autonomy, and full inclusion. The email follows up on contact from about a year ago regarding possible support and attaches the Jewish Federations of North America Designated Allocations Task Force report (February 2026), which recommends Bizchut among 18 vetted organizations. The report notes that Bizchut **receives no government funding** to preserve independence in advocacy, has **eight staff** and an **annual budget of around $600,000**, and faces the challenge of directing attention to people with disabilities during national crisis and war—when their risks increase.

Since the war ("Roar of the Lion" / "Lion's Roar"), Bizchut has focused on urgent disability issues:

1. **Accessible Home Front Command alerts for deaf and hard-of-hearing people** — Alerts are currently inaccessible. Bizchut had petitioned the Supreme Court (e.g. on behalf of Tamar, 63, deaf) ~2 years ago; the state began working on a solution and committed to act, and a pilot of accessible emergency alert devices has been launched. Bizchut recently filed an **urgent motion** and promoted media coverage (Ynet).

2. **Priority return of foreign home-care workers** — A letter to the Transport Minister requesting priority for foreign care workers stranded abroad to return to Israel. Their absence leaves tens of thousands of people with disabilities and elderly at risk and severely burdens families (Israel Hayom coverage cited).

3. **Accessible protected spaces** — Regulations under the Equal Rights for Persons with Disabilities Law have not been enacted for over 20 years. Bizchut had petitioned with others, pressed the PM’s office and Defense Ministry, and requested an urgent Knesset discussion; a discussion was scheduled for the following week (Ynet link provided). Advocacy includes demands to the Prime Minister's Office and Ministry of Defense.

The attached Task Force report states that up to **20% of Israel’s population** (about two million people) live with disabilities; the war has increased physical and psychosocial disabilities and demand for services. Philanthropy is urged to fund areas government does not, including advocacy, accessibility, and accessing rights—all central to Bizchut’s work.

# Supporting Israelis with Disabilities: A Strategic Guide for Jewish Federations  
February 2026

**Table of Contents:** Executive Summary, Introduction, Background Information, Current Funding Landscape, Jewish Federations' Historic Partners, Task Force Methodology, Recommendations, Appendix (Task Force Members & Interviewees).

### Executive Summary

Report presents the Israel & Overseas Committee Designated Allocations Task Force's guide and recommendations for supporting people in Israel with disabilities. Up to 20% of Israel's population—around two million people—live with disabilities (psychosocial, physical, sensory, developmental, learning, intellectual, chronic illness). Israel has a strong legislative framework; implementation is uneven, with regional disparities, bureaucratic complexity, and persistent gaps. Urgency has intensified since October 7, 2023: tens of thousands with permanent physical and psychosocial disabilities; opportunity for philanthropy to complement government, strengthen nonprofits, and accelerate change. Government is the primary funder (billions/year); philanthropy fills gaps, supports innovation and pilots, capital development, and advocacy. **Guiding principles:** prioritize programs not funded by government; support innovation with pathways to government adoption; invest in capital and human resources to maximize public funding; increase attention to underserved populations. **18 vetted nonprofits recommended**, plus continued support for JDC Israel Unlimited (JDC-IU).

### Introduction

The Task Force seeks to support Jewish Federations' overseas work with a more data-informed approach. It chose to examine support for Israelis with disabilities after first exploring Ethiopian Israeli integration (2023). Goal: develop a comprehensive resource introducing leading nonprofits, outlining strategic funding priorities, and providing allocation recommendations.

### Background Information

- Approximately 20% of Israel's 10 million residents live with disabilities (diverse types and experiences).
- **Legislative foundations:** Equal Rights for Persons with Disabilities Law (1998), Rehabilitation in the Community of Persons with Mental Disabilities Law (2000), Inclusion Amendment to Special Education Law (2002), ratification of UN CRPD, Welfare Services for People with Disabilities Law (2012), employment integration and accessibility legislation (2022).
- Nearly 20,000 Israelis with disabilities still live in closed institutions; government committed to advancing community-based frameworks.
- Implementation uneven across regions and subpopulations; disparities exacerbated for Arab citizens, people in poverty, and those struggling with bureaucracy.
- **Authorities involved:** National Insurance Institute, Ministry of Welfare and Social Affairs, Ministry of Health, Kupot Holim, Ministry of Education, Ministry of Labor, Ministry of Defense, Ministry of Justice (Commission for Equal Rights), Ministry of Transportation, Local Authorities.

### October 7th: Impact on Israelis with Disabilities

- As of December 2025: 20,000+ soldiers wounded since Oct. 7; 80,000 total wounded veterans from all conflicts; 58% of wounded soldiers experienced emotional harm; 38% dealing exclusively with PTSD; 1,000 new disability claims filed monthly; $2.5B annual government budget to treat wounded soldiers.

### Current Funding Landscape

- Ministry of Welfare and Social Affairs Disabilities Administration: $671M over five years plus $135M annually (2022 Welfare Services for People with Disabilities Law); person-centered approach. Philanthropy fills gaps, supports innovation and pilots, capital development, advocacy.

### Jewish Federations Allocations (2021–2024)

- Total designated allocations for disability programs: $1.27M (2021), $1.49M (2022), $1.87M (2023), $1.65M (2024). Grantees: 28–31 nonprofits per year. Philadelphia listed among Federations that allocated funds to organizations serving Israelis with disabilities in 2023–2024. **Core allocations to JDC:** 2023 — 2.4% of $29.5M supported JDC-IU ($713,414); 2024 — 3.4% of $32.5M ($1,098,277). List of Federations that allocated to disability orgs in 2023–2024 includes Ann Arbor, Atlanta, Boston, Chicago, Detroit, etc.

### Recommendations

- Support disability programming **not funded by government:** accessibility, advocacy, public awareness, technology, accessing rights, research.
- For government-funded nonprofits: focus on new/innovative approaches with pathway to government adoption; physical infrastructure or human resources to maximize government funding; additional services only where government does not fund and clear need exists; consider prioritizing underserved populations (adults, minorities).

### Recommended Organizations (18 + JDC-IU)

**Bizchut (this request):** Mission—ensure people with disabilities can fully exercise rights to dignity, equality, autonomy, and freedom. Advances rights for all types of disabilities in every aspect of life. **Five focus areas:** independent living in the community, fair employment, participation in policymaking ("nothing about us without us"), elimination of use of restraints in institutional settings, access to justice. Over 30 years of experience; credibility with civil servants, officials, academics, nonprofits. Receives **no government funding** to maintain independence. **Main challenges:** directing public attention to people with disabilities during national crisis and war when attention is elsewhere—precisely when risks are heightened; raising philanthropic funds when donors prioritize direct aid over systemic change. **Eight staff; annual budget around $600,000.** Example in report: petition to Supreme Court on behalf of Tamar (deaf) demanding accessible means for missile alerts; government committed to act and launched pilot of accessible emergency alert devices.

**Other recommended organizations (summary):** Access Israel (~$10M), Kesher (~$750K), AlManarah (~$1.5M), Beit Issie Shapiro (~$13M), Chimes Israel (>$25M), Israel Elwyn (>$70M), Kivunim (>$7M), Shalva (>$25M), SHEKEL (>$35M), OTI (>$95M), AKIM Israel (>$60M), ILAN (>$30M), Enosh (~$60M), Etgarim (>$6M), Nitzan Association (~$15M), Krembo Wings (~$15M), Simcha Layeled (~$4.5M), plus JDC-Israel Unlimited.
