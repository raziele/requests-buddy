## The Ask

Bizchut, an Israeli disability rights organization that receives no government funding, is requesting emergency support and an introduction meeting. They ask whether the Federation has emergency funds for war-time support and offer to meet to present current projects. They previously engaged with Jewish Federation of Greater Philadelphia about support and are following up in line with the North American Federations Task Force report on supporting Israelis with disabilities, in which Bizchut is one of 18 recommended organizations.

**Funding Requested:** Not specified

**Non-Financial Ask:** Request to set a meeting to present the projects Bizchut is currently working on; inquiry whether emergency war-related funds are available for their disability-rights work.

## Context & Background

Tali Marcus, Executive Director of Bizchut, wrote to the Jewish Federation of Greater Philadelphia (Talia Lidar) on March 5, 2026. They had been in contact about a year earlier regarding possible support for Bizchut. In the interim, the Israel & Overseas Committee Designated Allocations Task Force of Jewish Federations of North America produced a report on supporting people with disabilities in Israel; the report writers consulted Bizchut on the situation and needs of people with disabilities in Israel, and Bizchut is one of the organizations recommended for support in that report.

Bizchut (בזכות) is Israel’s human rights center for people with disabilities, advancing dignity, equality, autonomy, and full inclusion. The organization runs only on donations and does not receive government funding, in order to maintain independence in advocacy. They are asking whether the Federation has emergency funds designated for war-time support and whether it can support Bizchut's activity; any support, including small amounts, would help them continue to advance the rights and welfare of people with disabilities. The attached Task Force report states that up to 20% of Israel’s population (about two million people) live with disabilities; the war has increased physical and psychosocial disabilities and demand for services. Philanthropy is urged to fund areas government does not, including advocacy, accessibility, and accessing rights—all central to Bizchut’s work.

Since the war ("Lion's Roar" / "Roar of the Lion"), Bizchut has temporarily redirected part of its activities toward urgent disability issues:

1. **Accessible Home Front Command alerts for deaf and hard-of-hearing people** — Alerts are currently inaccessible. Bizchut filed a petition to the Supreme Court (with a recent urgent motion), and the state has begun working on a solution; they promoted media coverage (Ynet). The report cites the case of Tamar (63, deaf): Bizchut petitioned the Supreme Court for accessible missile alerts; the government committed to act and launched a pilot of accessible emergency alert devices.

2. **Priority return for foreign care workers** — A letter to the Transport Minister requesting priority for foreign care workers stranded abroad to return to Israel, given the essential role of care workers for tens of thousands of people with disabilities and elderly; their absence leaves tens of thousands at risk and severely burdens families. They cited Israel Hayom coverage.

3. **Accessible protected spaces** — Advocacy on the lack of accessible protected spaces for people with disabilities. Regulations under the Equal Rights for Persons with Disabilities Law have not been enacted for over 20 years. Bizchut had petitioned with others, pressed the Prime Minister's Office and Ministry of Defense, and requested an urgent Knesset discussion; a discussion was scheduled for the following week (Ynet link provided).

### 2025-RESOURCES-FOR-SUPPORTING-ISRAELIS-WITH-DISABILITIES-REPORT (1).pdf

# Supporting Israelis with Disabilities: A Strategic Guide for Jewish Federations
February 2026

**Table of Contents:** Executive Summary, Introduction, Background Information, Current Funding Landscape, Jewish Federations' Historic Partners, Task Force Methodology, Recommendations, Appendix (Task Force Members & Interviewees).

### Executive Summary

This report presents the Jewish Federations of North America's Israel & Overseas Committee Designated Allocations Task Force's guide and recommendations for supporting people in Israel with disabilities. Up to 20% of Israel's population—around two million people—live with disabilities (psychosocial, physical, sensory, developmental, learning, intellectual, chronic illnesses). Israel has a strong legislative framework, but uneven implementation, regional disparities, bureaucratic complexity, and persistent gaps limit realization of rights. The urgency has intensified since October 7, 2023: tens of thousands of Israelis have sustained permanent physical and psychosocial disabilities. The report offers guiding principles: prioritize programs not funded by government; support innovation with pathways to government adoption; invest in capital and human resources to maximize public funding; increase attention to underserved populations. It recommends eighteen vetted nonprofits plus continued support for JDC's Israel Unlimited (JDC-IU).

### Introduction

The Task Force seeks to support Jewish Federations' overseas work with a more data-informed approach. It chose to examine support for Israelis with disabilities after first exploring Ethiopian Israeli integration (2023). Goal: develop a comprehensive resource introducing leading nonprofits, outlining strategic funding priorities, and providing allocation recommendations.

### Background Information

- Approximately 20% of Israel's 10 million residents live with disabilities; diverse types and experiences.
- Legislative foundations: Equal Rights for Persons with Disabilities Law (1998), Rehabilitation in the Community of Persons with Mental Disabilities Law (2000), Inclusion Amendment to Special Education Law (2002), ratification of UN CRPD, Welfare Services for People with Disabilities Law (2012), employment integration and accessibility legislation (2022).
- Nearly 20,000 Israelis with disabilities still live in closed institutions; government committed to advancing community-based frameworks.
- Implementation uneven across regions and subpopulations; disparities exacerbated for Arab citizens, people in poverty, and those struggling with bureaucracy.
- Many government authorities involved: National Insurance Institute, Ministry of Welfare and Social Affairs, Ministry of Health, Kupot Holim, Ministry of Education, Ministry of Labor, Ministry of Defense, Ministry of Justice (Commission for Equal Rights), Ministry of Transportation, Local Authorities.

### October 7th: Impact on Israelis with Disabilities

- As of December 2025: 20,000+ soldiers wounded since Oct. 7; 80,000 total wounded veterans from all conflicts; 58% of wounded soldiers experienced emotional harm; 38% dealing exclusively with PTSD; 1,000 new disability claims filed monthly; $2.5B annual government budget to treat wounded soldiers.

### Current Funding Landscape

- Israeli government is the primary funder (billions per year). Ministry of Welfare and Social Affairs Disabilities Administration: $671M over five years plus $135M annually (2022 Welfare Services for People with Disabilities Law). Philanthropy fills gaps, supports innovation and pilots, capital development, advocacy.
- Jewish Federations designated allocations (2021–2024): 2021 $1.27M/67 grants/28 grantees; 2022 $1.49M/70/31; 2023 $1.87M/82/27; 2024 $1.65M/81/28. Philadelphia listed among Federations that allocated funds to organizations serving Israelis with disabilities in 2023–2024.
- Core allocations to JDC: in 2023, 2.4% of $29.5M supported JDC-IU ($713,414); in 2024, 3.4% of $32.5M supported JDC-IU ($1,098,277).

### Recommendations

- Support disability programming not funded by government (accessibility, advocacy, public awareness, technology, accessing rights, research).
- For government-funded nonprofits: focus on new/innovative approaches with pathway to government adoption; physical infrastructure or human resources to maximize government funding; additional services only where government does not fund and clear need exists; consider prioritizing underserved populations (adults, minorities).

### Recommended Organizations (18 + JDC-IU)

**Bizchut** (relevant to this request): Mission—ensure people with disabilities can fully exercise rights to dignity, equality, autonomy, and freedom. Advances rights for all types of disabilities in every aspect of life. Five focus areas: independent living in the community, fair employment, participation in policymaking ("nothing about us without us"), elimination of use of restraints in institutional settings, access to justice. Over 30 years of experience; major successes; credibility with civil servants, officials, academics, nonprofits. Receives **no government funding** to maintain independence. Main challenges: directing public attention to people with disabilities during national crisis and war when attention is elsewhere—precisely when risks are heightened; raising philanthropic funds when donors prioritize direct aid over systemic change. Eight staff; annual budget around **$600,000**. Example in report: petition to Supreme Court on behalf of Tamar (deaf) demanding accessible means for missile alerts; government committed to act and launched pilot of accessible emergency alert devices.

Other recommended organizations summarized in report: Access Israel (~$10M), Kesher (~$750K), AlManarah (~$1.5M), Beit Issie Shapiro (~$13M), Chimes Israel (>$25M), Israel Elwyn (>$70M), Kivunim (>$7M), Shalva (>$25M), SHEKEL (>$35M), OTI (>$95M), AKIM Israel (>$60M), ILAN (>$30M), Enosh (~$60M), Etgarim (>$6M), Nitzan Association (~$15M), Krembo Wings (~$15M), Simcha Layeled (~$4.5M), plus JDC-Israel Unlimited.
