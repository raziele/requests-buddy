## The Ask

Bizchut requests an introduction meeting to present its current projects and to explore emergency war-related funding support.

The organization reports it has redirected significant activity to urgent disability-rights issues during the current war (referred to as "Lion's Roar" / "Roar of the Lion"), and asks whether emergency funds are available for this work. Bizchut notes it receives no government funding and says even modest support would help sustain rights-based interventions.

**Funding Requested:** Not specified (inquiry about emergency war-related support; any amount could help)

**Non-Financial Ask:** Meeting to present current projects and discuss possible support.

## Context & Background

Bizchut is a leading Israeli disability rights organization focused on dignity, equality, autonomy, freedom, and full inclusion for people with disabilities. It was previously in contact with Federation stakeholders about a year earlier regarding potential support and is now following up.

Both source records reference the Jewish Federations of North America (JFNA) Israel & Overseas / Designated Allocations Task Force report (February 2026), in which Bizchut is one of 18 recommended organizations for support. The report and email context emphasize that Bizchut receives no government funding in order to preserve advocacy independence, has approximately 8 staff, and an annual budget around $600,000.

The request is framed in the context of heightened wartime risk to people with disabilities and the need for philanthropy to complement government systems, especially in areas like accessibility, advocacy, and rights realization.

## Current War-Related Activities Highlighted by Bizchut

1. **Accessible emergency alerts (Home Front Command)**
   - Advocacy for accessibility of missile alerts for deaf and hard-of-hearing people.
   - Bizchut filed a Supreme Court petition (including a recent urgent motion).
   - State reportedly began work on a solution and launched a pilot of accessible emergency alert devices.
   - A representative case referenced in the report is Tamar, a deaf woman affected by inaccessible alerts.

2. **Return of foreign care workers**
   - Letter to the Transportation Minister requesting priority return for foreign home-care workers stranded abroad.
   - Rationale: absence of workers leaves tens of thousands of people with disabilities and elderly people without essential support, with heavy burden on families.

3. **Accessible protected spaces**
   - Advocacy regarding insufficiently accessible protected spaces for people with disabilities.
   - Pressure directed to the Prime Minister’s Office and Ministry of Defense; request for urgent Knesset deliberation.
   - One source indicates a Knesset discussion was scheduled the following week.

# Supporting Israelis with Disabilities: A Strategic Guide for Jewish Federations (February 2026)

## Executive Summary

- Prepared by the Jewish Federations of North America’s Israel & Overseas Committee Designated Allocations Task Force.
- Up to 20% of Israel’s population (about two million people) lives with disabilities.
- Despite strong legal frameworks, implementation is uneven with regional disparities, bureaucratic barriers, and persistent service gaps.
- Since October 7, 2023, urgency has increased due to large-scale physical and psychosocial injury and growing demand for disability services.
- Philanthropy is positioned to fill gaps, support innovation, build infrastructure/capacity, and fund advocacy and rights-access work not covered by government.
- Recommends 18 vetted nonprofits plus continued support for JDC-Israel Unlimited.

## Introduction & Purpose

- Task Force developed a more data-informed approach for overseas disability allocations.
- Aims to provide federations with a strategic resource on organizations, priorities, and funding recommendations.

## Background Information

- Approx. 20% of Israel’s ~10 million residents live with disabilities.
- Key legal/policy anchors include:
  - Equal Rights for Persons with Disabilities Law (1998)
  - Rehabilitation in the Community of Persons with Mental Disabilities Law (2000)
  - Inclusion amendment to Special Education Law (2002)
  - UN CRPD ratification
  - Welfare Services for People with Disabilities Law (2012; plus major 2022 budget framework)
- Around 20,000 people with disabilities still live in closed institutions; policy direction favors community-based alternatives.
- Disparities are more acute for Arab citizens, people in poverty, and those facing administrative complexity.
- Multiple relevant authorities include National Insurance Institute; Welfare, Health, Education, Labor, Defense, Justice, Transportation ministries; Kupot Holim; and local authorities.

## October 7 Impact (as cited in report)

- 20,000+ soldiers wounded since Oct 7 (as of Dec 2025).
- 80,000 total wounded veterans across conflicts.
- 58% of wounded soldiers reported emotional harm; 38% PTSD-only.
- ~1,000 new disability claims filed monthly.
- Approx. $2.5B annual government budget for treatment of wounded soldiers.

## Current Funding Landscape

- Government remains primary funder (billions annually).
- Ministry of Welfare Disabilities Administration funding cited at $671M over five years plus $135M annually.
- Jewish Federations designated allocations for disability programs:
  - 2021: $1.27M
  - 2022: $1.49M
  - 2023: $1.87M
  - 2024: $1.65M
- JDC-Israel Unlimited core allocation examples:
  - 2023: 2.4% of $29.5M ($713,414)
  - 2024: 3.4% of $32.5M ($1,098,277)

## Recommendations

- Prioritize disability programming not funded by government (accessibility, advocacy, public awareness, technology, rights access, research).
- For government-funded nonprofits, prioritize:
  - Innovation with clear path to government adoption
  - Capital/human resources that unlock greater public funding impact
  - Additional services only where unmet need is clear
  - Underserved populations (including adults and minorities)

## Bizchut Profile in Report

- Mission: ensure full exercise of rights to dignity, equality, autonomy, and freedom for people with disabilities.
- Focus areas include independent living, fair employment, participation in policymaking ("nothing about us without us"), ending restraints in institutions, and access to justice.
- Over 30 years of experience and significant public-sector/civil-society credibility.
- Receives **no government funding** to preserve advocacy independence.
- Reported size: **8 staff; annual budget about $600,000**.
- Core challenge: keeping disability rights visible and fundable during national crisis/war when public and donor attention shifts.
