## The Ask

Bizchut is requesting a meeting/introduction to present its current projects and to explore emergency war-related funding support. The organization notes prior contact about support roughly a year earlier and highlights that it is one of 18 recommended organizations in the Jewish Federations of North America (JFNA) Designated Allocations Task Force report on Israelis with disabilities (Feb 2026).

Bizchut asks whether emergency funds are available for wartime disability-rights work and emphasizes that even small support would help sustain urgent advocacy and rights protection efforts.

**Funding Requested:** Not specified

**Non-Financial Ask:** Meeting to present current projects and discuss potential support.

## Context & Background

Bizchut is an Israeli disability rights organization focused on dignity, equality, autonomy, and freedom for people with disabilities. It receives **no government funding** to preserve independence in advocacy. In the attached Task Force report, Bizchut is described as having over 30 years of work, eight staff, and an annual budget around **$600,000**.

Both source versions state that Bizchut has redirected significant work to wartime disability issues during the current conflict (referenced as "Lion's Roar"/"Roar of the Lion"). The request follows earlier conversations with Federation representatives and references the Task Force process in which Bizchut was consulted and later recommended for support.

### Urgent wartime issues highlighted by Bizchut

1. **Accessible missile/emergency alerts for deaf and hard-of-hearing people**  
   Bizchut petitioned the Supreme Court (with recent urgent motion activity), pushing for Home Front Command alert accessibility. Reported progress includes state action and pilot efforts for accessible emergency alert devices.

2. **Return of foreign home-care workers**  
   Bizchut urged prioritization of re-entry for foreign care workers stranded abroad, citing severe impact on tens of thousands of people with disabilities and elderly people and major strain on families.

3. **Accessible protected spaces**  
   Bizchut advocated for accessible protected spaces and advanced regulatory/accountability demands with government entities, including calls for urgent Knesset discussion; one account notes a discussion scheduled for the following week.

### 2025-RESOURCES-FOR-SUPPORTING-ISRAELIS-WITH-DISABILITIES-REPORT (1).pdf

# Supporting Israelis with Disabilities: A Strategic Guide for Jewish Federations (February 2026)

## Executive Summary

- The report provides recommendations for Federation support to Israelis with disabilities.
- Up to **20% of Israel's population** (about two million people) lives with disabilities.
- Israel has strong legal frameworks, but implementation gaps, regional disparities, bureaucracy, and service inequities persist.
- Since Oct 7, 2023, urgency has intensified due to large increases in physical and psychosocial disability-related needs.
- Government is the primary funder; philanthropy is positioned to fill gaps, support innovation, advocacy, and systems change.
- Recommendations cover **18 vetted nonprofits** plus continued support for **JDC Israel Unlimited (JDC-IU)**.

## Background Information

- Legal and policy context includes: Equal Rights for Persons with Disabilities Law (1998), Rehabilitation in the Community Law (2000), Inclusion Amendment to Special Education Law (2002), UN CRPD ratification, Welfare Services for People with Disabilities Law (2012), and subsequent employment/accessibility measures.
- Around 20,000 people with disabilities are still described as living in closed institutions; policy direction favors community-based living.
- Disparities are especially acute for Arab citizens, people in poverty, and those facing bureaucratic barriers.
- Multiple ministries/agencies share responsibility (Welfare, Health, Education, Labor, Defense, Justice, Transportation, National Insurance Institute, local authorities, and health funds).

## October 7 Impact Indicators (as cited in report)

- 20,000+ soldiers wounded since Oct 7 (as of Dec 2025).
- 80,000 total wounded veterans from all conflicts.
- 58% of wounded soldiers experienced emotional harm.
- 38% dealing exclusively with PTSD.
- About 1,000 new disability claims filed monthly.
- Approx. $2.5B annual government budget for wounded soldiers' treatment.

## Current Funding Landscape

- Ministry of Welfare Disabilities Administration funding cited as $671M over five years plus $135M annually.
- Jewish Federations designated allocations for disability programs:
  - 2021: $1.27M
  - 2022: $1.49M
  - 2023: $1.87M
  - 2024: $1.65M
- One version provides additional granularity:
  - 2021: 67 grants / 28 grantees
  - 2022: 70 / 31
  - 2023: 82 / 27
  - 2024: 81 / 28
- JDC-IU core allocation references:
  - 2023: 2.4% of $29.5M = $713,414
  - 2024: 3.4% of $32.5M = $1,098,277

## Strategic Recommendations

- Prioritize disability programming not funded by government (advocacy, accessibility, rights access, public awareness, technology, research).
- For government-funded nonprofits, prioritize:
  - innovation with a path to government adoption,
  - capital/human-resource investments that leverage public funding,
  - clearly unmet services,
  - underserved populations (including adults and minorities).

## Bizchut Profile in Report (relevant to request)

- Mission: ensure rights to dignity, equality, autonomy, and freedom for people with disabilities across all life domains.
- Focus areas include independent living, fair employment, participation in policymaking ("nothing about us without us"), ending restraints in institutions, and access to justice.
- **No government funding** (to maintain advocacy independence).
- Key wartime challenge: keeping disability rights visible during national crisis while donor attention shifts to direct relief.
- Capacity note: **8 staff; ~ $600,000 annual budget**.
- Example highlighted: Supreme Court petition (Tamar case) for accessible missile alerts; state committed to action and pilot launched.
