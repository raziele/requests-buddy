## The Ask

Bizchut (בזכות), an Israeli human rights organization focused on disability rights (and receiving no government funding to maintain independence in advocacy), is requesting an introduction/meeting to present its current projects and to explore whether emergency war-related funds are available for its disability-rights work during the current conflict (“Lion’s Roar” / “Roar of the Lion”).

- **Funding Requested:** Not specified
- **Non-Financial Ask:** Schedule a meeting to present current projects and ask about emergency funds for war-time support.

## Context & Background

- **Initial contact and follow-up:** Bizchut previously communicated about support about a year earlier, and on **Mar 5, 2026** Tali Marcus wrote to the Jewish Federation of Greater Philadelphia regarding potential support. The subsequent outreach uses the North American Jewish Federations Task Force report on supporting Israelis with disabilities (Feb 2026), which recommends Bizchut among the vetted organizations.
- **Why emergency support now:** During the war, Bizchut reports intensified disability needs, including greater physical and psychosocial impacts, and heightened risks for people with disabilities as systems and services are strained.
- **Independence and funding approach:** Bizchut runs only on donations and does not receive government funding, which they present as essential to maintaining independence and advocacy credibility.
- **Report alignment:** The attached Task Force resource frames philanthropy as a complement to government—especially for advocacy, accessibility, and other gaps that government does not fund directly—and highlights Bizchut as a recommended nonprofit.

## Current / Recent Advocacy & Wartime Projects

### Accessible Home Front Command alerts (deaf and hard-of-hearing)
- Bizchut reports that missile/alert messaging via **Home Front Command** has been inaccessible to **deaf and hard-of-hearing** individuals.
- Actions described include:
  - A **Supreme Court petition** (stated as ~2 years prior in one email; another source references filing a petition and an urgent motion).
  - The state reportedly **began working on a solution**.
  - Bizchut filed an **urgent motion** and promoted media coverage via **Ynet**.
- Report example: the Task Force guide references the Tamar case (Tamar described as **deaf**, and one email specifies **Tamar (63, deaf)**), where Bizchut’s Supreme Court petition concerned **accessible missile alerts**; the government reportedly committed to action and launched a **pilot** of **accessible emergency alert devices**.

### Priority return for foreign care workers
- Bizchut asks whether the recipient has **emergency war-related funds** and emphasizes immediate operational needs.
- One described priority is to request the **Transportation Minister** to enable **priority return** for **foreign home-care workers** who left for vacation and could not return.
- Bizchut states that the absence of these care workers leaves **tens of thousands** of people with disabilities and elderly at risk and **severely burdens families**.
- Media coverage referenced: **Israel Hayom** (as cited in one email).

### Accessible protected spaces
- Bizchut highlights gaps in accessible sheltering during emergencies:
  - It states that regulations under the **Equal Rights for Persons with Disabilities Law** have not been enacted for **over 20 years**.
- Actions described include:
  - Petitioning with others and advocacy directed to the **Prime Minister’s Office** and **Ministry of Defense**.
  - Requesting an urgent **Knesset** discussion.
  - One email notes that the discussion was scheduled for the **following week**, with **Ynet** reporting (link referenced as provided in that email).

# Supporting Israelis with Disabilities: A Strategic Guide for Jewish Federations
February 2026

## Executive Summary
- Presents the Jewish Federations of North America **Israel & Overseas Committee Designated Allocations Task Force** guide and recommendations for supporting people in Israel with disabilities.
- Estimates that **up to 20%** of Israel’s population (around **two million**) live with disabilities (psychosocial, physical, sensory, developmental, learning, intellectual, chronic illnesses).
- Notes: Israel has a legislative framework, but rights realization is limited by uneven implementation, regional disparities, bureaucratic complexity, and persistent gaps.
- Urgency intensified since **October 7, 2023**: tens of thousands of Israelis have sustained **permanent physical and psychosocial disabilities**.
- Guiding principles:
  - Prioritize programs **not funded by government**
  - Support innovation with pathways to government adoption
  - Invest in capital and human resources to maximize public funding
  - Increase attention to underserved populations
- Recommends **eighteen vetted nonprofits**, plus continued support for **JDC’s Israel Unlimited (JDC-IU)**.

## Introduction
- Task Force selected support for Israelis with disabilities after initially exploring Ethiopian Israeli integration (2023).
- Goal: create a comprehensive resource introducing leading nonprofits, outlining strategic funding priorities, and providing allocation recommendations.

## Background Information
- Approximately **20%** of Israel’s **10 million** residents live with disabilities.
- Legislative foundations (as listed in the report):
  - Equal Rights for Persons with Disabilities Law (1998)
  - Rehabilitation in the Community of Persons with Mental Disabilities Law (2000)
  - Inclusion Amendment to Special Education Law (2002)
  - Ratification of UN CRPD
  - Welfare Services for People with Disabilities Law (2012)
  - Employment integration and accessibility legislation (including 2022)
- Notes: nearly **20,000 Israelis** with disabilities still live in closed institutions; government committed to advancing community-based frameworks.
- Highlights disparities, especially for Arab citizens, people in poverty, and individuals facing bureaucracy.
- Multiple authorities involved: National Insurance Institute, Ministry of Welfare and Social Affairs, Ministry of Health, Kupot Holim, Ministry of Education, Ministry of Labor, Ministry of Defense, Ministry of Justice (Commission for Equal Rights), Ministry of Transportation, Local Authorities.

## October 7th: Impact on Israelis with Disabilities
- As of **December 2025** (as cited in the report):
  - **20,000+** soldiers wounded since Oct. 7
  - **80,000** total wounded veterans from all conflicts
  - **58%** of wounded soldiers experienced emotional harm
  - **38%** dealing exclusively with PTSD
  - **1,000** new disability claims filed monthly
  - **$2.5B** annual government budget to treat wounded soldiers

## Current Funding Landscape
- Israeli government is the primary funder (billions per year).
- Ministry of Welfare and Social Affairs Disabilities Administration:
  - **$671M** over five years plus **$135M** annually (as referenced via the 2022 Welfare Services for People with Disabilities Law).
- Philanthropy fills gaps, supports innovation and pilots, capital development, and advocacy.

## Jewish Federations Allocations (2021–2024)
- Total designated allocations for disability programs:
  - **$1.27M** (2021)
  - **$1.49M** (2022)
  - **$1.87M** (2023)
  - **$1.65M** (2024)
- Grantees: **28–31 nonprofits per year**
- Philadelphia noted as allocating funds to organizations serving Israelis with disabilities in **2023–2024**.
- Core allocations to **JDC**:
  - 2023: **2.4%** of **$29.5M** core = **$713,414** to JDC-IU
  - 2024: **3.4%** of **$32.5M** core = **$1,098,277** to JDC-IU

## Recommendations
- Support disability programming not funded by government (accessibility, advocacy, public awareness, technology, accessing rights, research).
- For government-funded nonprofits:
  - Prioritize new/innovative approaches with a pathway to government adoption
  - Use capital and human resources to maximize public funding
  - Provide additional services only where government does not fund and a clear need exists
  - Consider prioritizing underserved populations (adults, minorities)

## Bizchut (Recommended Organization)
- **Mission:** Ensure people with disabilities can fully exercise rights to dignity, equality, autonomy, and freedom.
- **Advancement scope:** Rights for all types of disabilities in every aspect of life.
- **Five focus areas:**
  - Independent living in the community
  - Fair employment
  - Participation in policymaking (“nothing about us without us”)
  - Elimination of use of restraints in institutional settings
  - Access to justice
- **Experience:** Over 30 years.
- **Independence model:** Receives **no government funding** to maintain independence.
- **Main challenges highlighted:**
  - Directing public attention to people with disabilities during national crisis and war—precisely when risks are heightened and attention elsewhere
  - Raising philanthropic funds when donors prioritize direct aid over systemic change
- **Operations (as cited):** Eight staff; annual budget around **$600,000**.
- **Supreme Court example:** References Tamar (deaf; one email specifies **Tamar (63, deaf)**) and the Bizchut petition regarding accessible missile alerts, including a government commitment and pilot of accessible emergency alert devices.
