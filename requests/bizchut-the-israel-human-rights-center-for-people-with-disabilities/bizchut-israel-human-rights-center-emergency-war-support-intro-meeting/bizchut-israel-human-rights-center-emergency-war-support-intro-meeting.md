## The Ask

Bizchut (בזכות), an Israeli disability rights organization that receives **no government funding** (to preserve independence in advocacy), is requesting support for its war-related work assisting people with disabilities during the current conflict (described as “Lion's Roar” / “Roar of the Lion”). They are seeking:

1. **Emergency war-related funding** for their disability-rights work *(amount not specified)*.
2. **An introduction meeting** to present the projects Bizchut is currently working on and to inquire whether the recipient has emergency war-related funds available for this work.

**Funding Requested:** Not specified

**Non-Financial Ask:** Meeting to present current projects; inquiry whether emergency war-related funds are available and whether the recipient can support Bizchut’s activity.

## Context & Background

- **Prior connection & timeline:** Bizchut says it was previously in contact about support roughly **a year earlier**. The first email’s described outreach is dated **Mar 5, 2026**, with a follow-up captured in the second request dated **Mar 10, 2026**.
- **Policy/strategy context:** Bizchut attached and referenced a Jewish Federations of North America Designated Allocations Task Force report (Feb 2026) on supporting Israelis with disabilities. Bizchut is one of **18 recommended nonprofits** in that report.
- **Why Bizchut is asking now:** During the war, attention and resources often shift away from people with disabilities even though their needs and risks increase. Bizchut describes its core challenge as directing public attention to people with disabilities during national crisis/war and raising philanthropic funds when donors prioritize direct aid over systemic change.
- **Organizational basics (as provided across the two emails and the attached report):**
  - Bizchut has **eight staff**.
  - Annual budget is **around $600,000**.
  - Receives **no government funding** to maintain independence.

## Wartime Disability Work Bizchut Highlights (Current Projects)

Bizchut describes three urgent focus areas they have pivoted toward during the war:

1. **Accessible missile alerts / Home Front Command alerts for deaf and hard-of-hearing people**
   - Alerts are described as **currently inaccessible**.
   - Bizchut previously **petitioned the Supreme Court about ~2 years ago**.
   - The state began working on a solution.
   - In the current phase, Bizchut **filed an urgent motion** and promoted **media coverage (Ynet)**.
   - The attached task force report also includes an example case involving **Tamar (63, deaf)**: Bizchut filed a Supreme Court petition demanding accessible means for missile alerts; the government committed to act and launched a **pilot of accessible emergency alert devices**.

2. **Priority return for foreign home-care workers**
   - Bizchut describes writing to the **Transport Minister** requesting priority for foreign care workers stranded abroad who cannot return (referenced as leaving for vacation and being unable to come back).
   - They state that the absence of these workers leaves **tens of thousands** of people with disabilities and elderly at risk, and severely burdens families.
   - They cite **Israel Hayom** coverage.

3. **Accessible protected spaces**
   - Bizchut states that regulations under the **Equal Rights for Persons with Disabilities Law** have **not been enacted for over 20 years**.
   - They describe advocacy including demands to the **Prime Minister’s Office** and the **Ministry of Defense**, plus requests for an urgent **Knesset** discussion.
   - In the intro email version, they also mention that a discussion is scheduled for the following week (with a **Ynet** reference/link provided).

### Executive Summary
- The report presents the Jewish Federations of North America Israel & Overseas Committee Designated Allocations Task Force guide and recommendations for supporting people in Israel with disabilities.
- **Scale of disability in Israel:** Up to **20% of Israel’s population** (around **two million people**) live with disabilities, including psychosocial, physical, sensory, developmental, learning, intellectual, and chronic illnesses.
- **Legislation vs. implementation:** Israel has a strong legislative framework, but uneven implementation, regional disparities, bureaucratic complexity, and persistent gaps limit realization of rights.
- **War-time urgency:** The urgency intensified since **October 7, 2023**: tens of thousands of Israelis have sustained permanent physical and psychosocial disabilities.
- **Guiding principles:**
  - Prioritize programs not funded by government.
  - Support innovation with pathways to government adoption.
  - Invest in capital and human resources to maximize public funding.
  - Increase attention to underserved populations.
- **Allocation recommendation:** Recommends **eighteen vetted nonprofits**, plus continued support for **JDC’s Israel Unlimited (JDC-IU)**.

### Introduction
- The Task Force seeks to support Jewish Federations’ overseas work with a more data-informed approach.
- It chose to examine support for Israelis with disabilities after first exploring Ethiopian Israeli integration (2023).
- Goal: develop a comprehensive resource introducing leading nonprofits, outlining strategic funding priorities, and providing allocation recommendations.

### Background Information
- Approximately **20% of Israel's 10 million residents** live with disabilities.
- Legislative foundations cited include:
  - Equal Rights for Persons with Disabilities Law (1998)
  - Rehabilitation in the Community of Persons with Mental Disabilities Law (2000)
  - Inclusion Amendment to Special Education Law (2002)
  - Ratification of UN CRPD
  - Welfare Services for People with Disabilities Law (2012)
  - Employment integration and accessibility legislation
- Nearly **20,000 Israelis with disabilities** still live in closed institutions; government commitment is described as advancing community-based frameworks.
- Implementation uneven across regions and subpopulations; disparities are noted as especially affecting Arab citizens, people in poverty, and those struggling with bureaucracy.
- Multiple government authorities involved (as cited): National Insurance Institute, Ministry of Welfare and Social Affairs, Ministry of Health, Kupot Holim, Ministry of Education, Ministry of Labor, Ministry of Defense, Ministry of Justice (Commission for Equal Rights), Ministry of Transportation, Local Authorities.

### October 7th: Impact on Israelis with Disabilities
- As of December 2025:
  - **20,000+** soldiers wounded since Oct. 7
  - **80,000** total wounded veterans from all conflicts
  - **58%** of wounded soldiers experienced emotional harm
  - **38%** dealing exclusively with PTSD
  - **1,000** new disability claims filed monthly
  - **$2.5B** annual government budget to treat wounded soldiers

### Current Funding Landscape
- Israeli government is described as the primary funder (billions per year).
- The Ministry of Welfare and Social Affairs Disabilities Administration funding is described as:
  - **$671M over five years** plus **$135M annually** (citing 2022 Welfare Services for People with Disabilities Law).
- Philanthropy fills gaps, supports innovation and pilots, and supports capital development and advocacy.

### Recommendations
- Support disability programming **not funded by government**, including accessibility, advocacy, public awareness, technology, accessing rights, and research.
- For government-funded nonprofits:
  - Fund innovation with pathways to government adoption.
  - Fund physical infrastructure or human resources to maximize government funding.
  - Add services only where government does not fund and clear need exists.
  - Consider prioritizing underserved populations (adults, minorities).

### Bizchut (as a recommended organization)
- **Mission:** Ensure people with disabilities can fully exercise rights to dignity, equality, autonomy, and freedom.
- **Scope:** Advances rights for all types of disabilities across every aspect of life.
- **Five focus areas:**
  - Independent living in the community
  - Fair employment
  - Participation in policymaking (“nothing about us without us”)
  - Elimination of use of restraints in institutional settings
  - Access to justice
- **Experience & credibility:** Over **30 years of experience**, with credibility with civil servants, officials, academics, and nonprofits.
- **Funding independence:** Receives **no government funding** to maintain independence.
- **Main challenges highlighted in the report and emails:**
  - Directing public attention to people with disabilities during national crisis and war when attention is elsewhere—when risks are heightened.
  - Raising philanthropic funds when donors prioritize direct aid over systemic change.
- **Staff & budget:** Eight staff; annual budget around **$600,000**.
- **Illustrative example included:** Supreme Court petition for **Tamar (63, deaf)** demanding accessible missile alerts; government committed and launched a pilot of accessible emergency alert devices.
