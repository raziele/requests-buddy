## The Ask

Bizchut, Israel’s leading human rights center for people with disabilities, is requesting an introduction meeting to present its current projects and to explore emergency funding. The organization was previously in contact about support and is one of 18 nonprofits recommended in the North American Jewish Federations Task Force report on supporting Israelis with disabilities (attached). Bizchut has pivoted part of its work to wartime disability issues: accessible Home Front Command alerts for deaf and hard-of-hearing people, priority return for foreign care workers, and accessible protected spaces. They ask whether the recipient has emergency war-related funds and state that any support would help advance the rights and welfare of people with disabilities.

**Funding Requested:** Not specified

**Non-Financial Ask:** Meeting to present current projects; inquiry whether emergency war-related funds are available for their disability-rights work.

## Context & Background

Bizchut (בזכות) is Israel’s human rights center for people with disabilities, advancing dignity, equality, autonomy, and full inclusion. The email follows up on contact from about a year ago regarding possible support and attaches the Jewish Federations of North America Designated Allocations Task Force report (February 2026), which recommends Bizchut among 18 vetted organizations. The report notes that Bizchut receives no government funding to preserve independence in advocacy, has eight staff and an annual budget of around $600,000, and faces the challenge of directing attention to people with disabilities during national crisis and war—when their risks increase.

Since the war ("Roar of the Lion"), Bizchut has focused on urgent disability issues: (1) Making Home Front Command alerts accessible to deaf and hard-of-hearing people—alerts are currently inaccessible; Bizchut had petitioned the Supreme Court ~2 years ago, the state began working on a solution, and they recently filed an urgent motion and promoted media coverage (Ynet). (2) Asking the Transportation Minister to prioritize return of foreign home-care workers who left for vacation and cannot return—their absence leaves tens of thousands of people with disabilities and elderly at risk and severely burdens families; they cited Israel Hayom coverage. (3) Accessible protected spaces—regulations under the Equal Rights for Persons with Disabilities Law have not been enacted for over 20 years; Bizchut had petitioned with others, pressed the PM’s office and Defense Ministry, and requested an urgent Knesset discussion; a discussion is now scheduled for the following week (Ynet link provided).

The attached Task Force report states that up to 20% of Israel’s population (about two million people) live with disabilities; the war has increased physical and psychosocial disabilities and demand for services. Philanthropy is urged to fund areas government does not, including advocacy, accessibility, and accessing rights—all central to Bizchut’s work.

# Supporting Israelis with Disabilities – A Strategic Guide for Jewish Federations (February 2026)

**Table of Contents:** Executive Summary, Introduction, Background Information, Current Funding Landscape, Jewish Federations' Historic Partners, Task Force Methodology, Recommendations, Appendix (Task Force Members & Interviewees).

**Executive Summary**
- Report presents the Designated Allocations Task Force's guide and recommendations for supporting people in Israel with disabilities.
- Up to 20% of Israel's population—around two million people—live with disabilities (psychosocial, physical, sensory, developmental, learning, intellectual, chronic illness).
- Strong legislative framework exists; implementation uneven, regional disparities, bureaucratic complexity, and service gaps remain.
- Urgency intensified since October 7, 2023: tens of thousands with permanent physical and psychosocial disabilities; opportunity for philanthropy to complement government, strengthen nonprofits, accelerate change.
- Government is primary funder (billions/year); philanthropy fills gaps, supports innovation, infrastructure, advocacy, capacity.
- Guiding principles: prioritize programs not funded by government; support innovation with pathways to government adoption; invest in capital/human resources to maximize public funding; increase attention to underserved populations.
- **18 vetted nonprofits recommended**, plus continued support for JDC Israel Unlimited.

**Background**
- ~20% of 10M residents live with disabilities; diverse types and experiences.
- Key laws: Equal Rights for Persons with Disabilities Law (1998), Rehabilitation in the Community (2000), Inclusion Amendment to Special Education (2002), ratification UN CRPD, Welfare Services for People with Disabilities Law (2012), employment and accessibility (2022).
- ~20,000 still in closed institutions; community-based alternatives prioritized.
- Disparities worse for Arab citizens, people in poverty, those struggling with bureaucracy.
- Multiple authorities: National Insurance Institute, local authorities, Welfare, Health, Kupot Holim, Education, Labor, Defense (veterans), Justice (Commission for Equal Rights), Transportation.

**October 7 impact**
- As of December 2025: 20,000+ soldiers wounded since Oct 7; 80,000 total wounded veterans; 58% of wounded soldiers experienced emotional harm; 38% dealing exclusively with PTSD; 1,000 new disability claims monthly; $2.5B annual government budget to treat wounded soldiers.

**Current Funding Landscape**
- Ministry of Welfare Disabilities Administration: $671M over five years + $135M annually; person-centered approach.
- Jewish Federations designated allocations (2021–2024): 2021 $1.27M/67 grants/28 grantees; 2022 $1.49M/70/31; 2023 $1.87M/82/27; 2024 $1.65M/81/28.
- JDC-IU: 2023 2.4% of $29.5M core ($713,414); 2024 3.4% of $32.5M ($1,098,277).
- List of Federations that allocated to disability orgs in 2023–2024 (Ann Arbor, Atlanta, Boston, Chicago, etc.).

**Recommendations**
- Support disability programming not funded by government: accessibility, advocacy, public awareness, technology, accessing rights, research.
- For government-funded nonprofits: fund innovation with pathway to government adoption; capital/human resources to maximize impact; additional services only where government does not fund and need is clear; consider underserved populations (adults, minorities).

**Bizchut (recommended organization)**
- Mission: ensure people with disabilities can fully exercise rights to dignity, equality, autonomy, freedom; advance rights for all disability types; five focus areas: independent living, fair employment, participation in policymaking ("nothing about us without us"), elimination of restraints in institutions, access to justice.
- Major successes over 30+ years; credibility with civil servants, officials, academics, nonprofits.
- **Receives no government funding** to maintain independence.
- Main challenges: directing public attention to people with disabilities during crisis/war when attention is elsewhere—and when risks are heightened; raising philanthropic funds when donors prioritize direct aid over systemic change.
- **8 staff; annual budget around $600,000.**
- Report includes Tamar (63, deaf) case: Bizchut filed Supreme Court petition for accessible missile alerts; government committed to act and launched pilot of accessible emergency alert devices.

**Other recommended organizations (summary)**
- Access Israel (~$10M budget); Kesher (~$750K); AlManarah (~$1.5M); Beit Issie Shapiro (~$13M); Chimes Israel (>$25M); Israel Elwyn (>$70M); Kivunim (>$7M); Shalva (>$25M); SHEKEL (>$35M); OTI (>$95M); AKIM (>$60M); ILAN (>$30M); Enosh (~$60M); Etgarim (>$6M); Nitzan (~$15M); Krembo Wings (~$15M); Simcha Layeled (~$4.5M).
