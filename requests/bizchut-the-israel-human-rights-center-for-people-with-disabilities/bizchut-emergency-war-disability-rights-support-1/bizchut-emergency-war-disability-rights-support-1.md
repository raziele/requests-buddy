## The Ask

Bizchut is requesting:
1. **An introduction/working meeting** to present its current projects.
2. **Emergency war-related financial support** (amount not specified), or confirmation whether emergency funding is available.

The organization references prior contact (about a year earlier) and notes it is one of **18 recommended nonprofits** in the Jewish Federations of North America (JFNA) Task Force report on supporting Israelis with disabilities (Feb 2026). Bizchut emphasizes that it receives **no government funding** to preserve advocacy independence, so philanthropic support—including small amounts—directly sustains its rights-based wartime work.

**Funding Requested:** Not specified  
**Non-Financial Ask:** Meeting/introduction to present projects and discuss support options.

## Context & Background

Bizchut is a long-standing disability rights organization in Israel focused on dignity, equality, autonomy, and full participation. During the current war (named as "Lion’s Roar" / "Roar of the Lion" in sources), Bizchut redirected significant effort to urgent rights and accessibility gaps.

The request situates Bizchut within the JFNA Designated Allocations Task Force process:
- Bizchut was consulted during report preparation.
- The report recommends Bizchut among 18 vetted organizations (+ JDC Israel Unlimited).
- Bizchut is described as having **8 staff** and an annual budget of about **$600,000**.
- Core challenge highlighted: keeping disability rights visible during crisis periods when public/donor attention shifts elsewhere.

## Current Wartime Advocacy Priorities Cited by Bizchut

1. **Accessible emergency/missile alerts for deaf and hard-of-hearing people**
   - Bizchut petitioned the Supreme Court (initially ~2 years earlier).
   - A recent urgent motion was filed.
   - State authorities began developing a solution; report mentions pilot emergency alert devices.
   - Media coverage referenced (including Ynet in one source).

2. **Return of foreign home-care workers**
   - Letter to Transportation Minister requesting priority return for workers stranded abroad.
   - Rationale: tens of thousands of people with disabilities and elderly depend on them; family burden rises when workers cannot return.
   - Media coverage referenced (including Israel Hayom in one source).

3. **Accessible protected spaces (shelters/protected rooms)**
   - Bizchut argues required accessibility regulations under disability law have remained unimplemented for over 20 years.
   - Advocacy directed to PMO and Ministry of Defense; request for urgent Knesset discussion.
   - One source notes a Knesset discussion was scheduled for the following week.

# Supporting Israelis with Disabilities: A Strategic Guide for Jewish Federations (February 2026)

## Scope and Purpose
- Produced by JFNA’s Israel & Overseas Committee Designated Allocations Task Force.
- Intended as a data-informed resource for Federations: sector context, funding principles, and recommended organizations.
- Includes executive summary, background, funding landscape, historical allocations/partners, methodology, recommendations, and appendix.

## Key Sector Context
- Up to **20% of Israel’s population** (~2 million people) lives with disabilities.
- Disabilities include psychosocial, physical, sensory, developmental, learning, intellectual, and chronic illness-related.
- Israel has strong legal foundations but inconsistent implementation, regional disparities, bureaucratic barriers, and service gaps.
- Around **20,000** people with disabilities remain in closed institutions; community-based alternatives are a policy priority.
- Underserved groups include Arab citizens, people in poverty, and people facing bureaucratic exclusion.

## Relevant Legislative/Policy References
- Equal Rights for Persons with Disabilities Law (1998)
- Rehabilitation in the Community of Persons with Mental Disabilities Law (2000)
- Inclusion amendment to Special Education Law (2002)
- UN CRPD ratification
- Welfare Services for People with Disabilities Law (2012; with noted later funding frameworks)
- Employment/accessibility legislation (including 2022 references in one source summary)

## October 7 War Impact Metrics (as cited)
- 20,000+ soldiers wounded since Oct 7 (as of Dec 2025)
- 80,000 total wounded veterans from all conflicts
- 58% of wounded soldiers with emotional harm
- 38% dealing exclusively with PTSD
- ~1,000 new disability claims monthly
- ~$2.5B annual government budget for treatment of wounded soldiers

## Funding Landscape and Federation Allocations
- Government remains primary funder (billions/year).
- Ministry of Welfare Disabilities Administration figures cited: **$671M over five years + $135M annually**.
- Philanthropy’s role: fund gaps, innovation, advocacy, infrastructure, and capacity.
- Jewish Federations designated disability allocations:
  - 2021: $1.27M
  - 2022: $1.49M
  - 2023: $1.87M
  - 2024: $1.65M
- Additional grant-count/grantee figures (from one source):  
  2021: 67 grants / 28 grantees; 2022: 70 / 31; 2023: 82 / 27; 2024: 81 / 28.
- JDC-Israel Unlimited (JDC-IU) core allocation references:
  - 2023: 2.4% of $29.5M = $713,414
  - 2024: 3.4% of $32.5M = $1,098,277
- Philadelphia is listed among Federations allocating to disability organizations in 2023–2024.

## Strategic Recommendations in the Report
- Prioritize disability programming **not funded by government** (advocacy, accessibility, rights access, technology, research, awareness).
- For government-funded nonprofits, support:
  - innovation with adoption pathways,
  - capital/human resources that leverage public funds,
  - additional services only where clear unmet need exists.
- Increase focus on underserved populations (including adults and minorities).

## Bizchut Profile in the Report
- Mission: ensure people with disabilities can exercise rights to dignity, equality, autonomy, and freedom.
- Focus areas: independent living, fair employment, participation in policymaking ("nothing about us without us"), eliminating restraints in institutions, access to justice.
- 30+ years of activity and strong policy credibility.
- **No government funding** (to preserve advocacy independence).
- Main wartime challenge: low attention and fundraising for systemic rights work versus direct relief.
- Approx. **8 staff**, **~$600,000 annual budget**.
- Case example: Supreme Court petition on behalf of Tamar (deaf) for accessible missile alerts; state committed to action and launched a pilot.
