## The Ask

Bizchut is requesting:
1. A meeting/introduction to present its current projects.
2. Consideration for emergency war-time funding for disability-rights work during the current conflict (described as "Lion's Roar" / "Roar of the Lion").

Bizchut notes prior contact with Federation partners roughly a year earlier and references the Jewish Federations of North America (JFNA) Task Force report (Feb 2026), where Bizchut is one of 18 recommended organizations for disability-related support in Israel.

**Funding Requested:** Not specified (any amount said to be helpful).  
**Non-Financial Ask:** Introductory meeting/presentation and discussion of available emergency funds.

## Context & Background

Bizchut is a long-standing Israeli disability-rights organization focused on dignity, equality, autonomy, and full participation. Its five focus areas are:
- Independent living in the community
- Fair employment
- Participation in policymaking ("nothing about us without us")
- Elimination of restraints in institutional settings
- Access to justice

Key organizational profile from the attached report:
- 30+ years of activity
- 8 staff
- Annual budget around $600,000
- Receives **no government funding** to preserve advocacy independence

During the war period, Bizchut reports shifting substantial effort to urgent issues:

1. **Accessible emergency alerts**
   - Home Front Command missile alerts were inaccessible to deaf and hard-of-hearing people.
   - Bizchut petitioned the Supreme Court (original petition around two years earlier; later urgent motion filed).
   - State began working on a solution; pilot for accessible emergency alert devices launched.
   - Media references included Ynet.

2. **Return of foreign care workers**
   - Bizchut appealed to the Transportation Minister for priority return of foreign/home-care workers stranded abroad.
   - Concern: tens of thousands of people with disabilities and elderly depend on these workers; prolonged absence creates severe risk and family burden.
   - Media reference included Israel Hayom.

3. **Accessible protected spaces**
   - Bizchut raised ongoing gaps in accessible shelters/protected spaces.
   - Cited that relevant regulations under the Equal Rights for Persons with Disabilities framework were not enacted for many years (described as over 20 years).
   - Advocacy included pressure on the Prime Minister’s Office and Ministry of Defense, plus request for urgent Knesset deliberation (a discussion reportedly scheduled the following week).

### 2025-RESOURCES-FOR-SUPPORTING-ISRAELIS-WITH-DISABILITIES-REPORT (1).pdf

# Supporting Israelis with Disabilities: A Strategic Guide for Jewish Federations (February 2026)

## Executive Summary

- Up to 20% of Israel’s population (about two million people) lives with disabilities (psychosocial, physical, sensory, developmental, learning, intellectual, chronic illness).
- Israel has robust disability-rights legislation, but implementation is uneven across regions and populations, with bureaucracy and service gaps.
- Since October 7, 2023, urgency has grown substantially due to increased physical and psychosocial disability needs.
- Government remains primary funder; philanthropy is positioned to fill gaps, support innovation, advocacy, and capacity-building.
- Recommends 18 vetted nonprofits plus continued support to JDC-Israel Unlimited.

## Background and System Context

- Legal framework cited includes: Equal Rights for Persons with Disabilities Law (1998), Rehabilitation in the Community (2000), Inclusion Amendment to Special Education Law (2002), UN CRPD ratification, Welfare Services for People with Disabilities Law (2012), and later accessibility/employment reforms.
- Around 20,000 people with disabilities reportedly still live in closed institutions; policy trend favors community-based models.
- Disparities are heightened for Arab citizens, people in poverty, and those facing administrative barriers.
- Multiple state bodies shape disability services and rights implementation (NII, Welfare, Health, Education, Labor, Defense, Justice, Transportation, local authorities, health funds).

## October 7 Impact Metrics (as cited)

- 20,000+ soldiers wounded since Oct 7 (as of Dec 2025)
- 80,000 total wounded veterans across conflicts
- 58% of wounded soldiers experienced emotional harm
- 38% dealing exclusively with PTSD
- ~1,000 new disability claims monthly
- ~$2.5B annual government budget for treatment of wounded soldiers

## Funding Landscape and Federation Allocations

- Ministry of Welfare Disabilities Administration figures cited: $671M over five years plus $135M annually.
- Jewish Federations designated allocations for disability programs:
  - 2021: $1.27M
  - 2022: $1.49M
  - 2023: $1.87M
  - 2024: $1.65M
- Additional breakdown (from one source):
  - 2021: 67 grants / 28 grantees
  - 2022: 70 / 31
  - 2023: 82 / 27
  - 2024: 81 / 28
- JDC-IU core allocation references:
  - 2023: 2.4% of $29.5M = $713,414
  - 2024: 3.4% of $32.5M = $1,098,277
- Philadelphia is explicitly noted among federations allocating disability-related support in 2023–2024.

## Strategic Recommendations

- Prioritize programs not funded by government (advocacy, accessibility, rights access, public awareness, research, technology).
- For government-funded organizations, prioritize innovation with a pathway to public adoption and targeted capacity investments.
- Fund additional services only where public funding is absent and need is clear.
- Consider underserved populations (including adults and minorities).

## Bizchut Entry (Report-Relevant Highlights)

- Mission: enable full exercise of rights to dignity, equality, autonomy, and freedom for people with disabilities.
- Operates across disability types and life domains.
- No government funding (independence rationale).
- Current challenge: maintaining attention and fundraising for disability rights during wartime, when donor focus may shift to immediate direct aid.
- Includes a case example (Tamar, deaf) tied to emergency alert accessibility litigation and subsequent state response.
