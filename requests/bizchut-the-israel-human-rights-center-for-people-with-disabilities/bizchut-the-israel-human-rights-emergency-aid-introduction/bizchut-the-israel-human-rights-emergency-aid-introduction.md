## The Ask

Bizchut requests (1) an introduction/meeting to present its current projects and (2) inquiry/coordination regarding emergency war-related funding for its disability-rights and wartime service work.

- **Funding Requested:** Not specified
- **Non-Financial Ask:** Meeting to present current projects; ask whether emergency war-related funds are available for Bizchut’s disability-rights work.

## Context & Background

Bizchut (בזכות) is Israel’s human rights center for people with disabilities, advancing dignity, equality, autonomy, and full inclusion. The request follows prior contact about support (about a year earlier) and reiterates that Bizchut receives no government funding in order to maintain independence in advocacy.

The emails attach and reference a North American Jewish Federations Task Force report (February 2026) on supporting Israelis with disabilities, in which Bizchut is one of 18 recommended nonprofits.

Following the war period described as “Lion’s Roar” / “Roar of the Lion,” Bizchut has temporarily redirected part of its work to urgent disability issues, including:

1. **Accessible emergency/missile alerts for deaf and hard-of-hearing people**
   - Alerts are described as currently inaccessible.
   - Bizchut filed a petition to the Supreme Court; one source notes a petition “~2 years ago,” with the state beginning to work on a solution.
   - An urgent motion was filed and media coverage is referenced (Ynet link referenced in one source).
   - The report cites a specific example involving Tamar (63, deaf) where Bizchut pursued accessible missile alerts; government committed to act and launched a pilot of accessible emergency alert devices.

2. **Priority return of foreign care workers stranded abroad**
   - Bizchut asks the Transportation Minister to prioritize return of foreign home-care workers who left for vacation and cannot return.
   - The stated impact: the absence leaves tens of thousands of people with disabilities and elderly at risk and severely burdens families.
   - Media coverage is referenced (Israel Hayom coverage is cited in one source).

3. **Accessible protected spaces for people with disabilities**
   - One source emphasizes that regulations under the Equal Rights for Persons with Disabilities Law have not been enacted for over 20 years.
   - Bizchut has petitioned (including with others), pressed the Prime Minister’s Office and the Defense Ministry, and requested an urgent Knesset debate.
   - One source notes that the discussion is scheduled for the following week (Ynet link referenced).

# Supporting Israelis with Disabilities: A Strategic Guide for Jewish Federations
February 2026

## Table of Contents
Executive Summary, Introduction, Background Information, Current Funding Landscape, Jewish Federations' Historic Partners, Task Force Methodology, Recommendations, Appendix (Task Force Members & Interviewees).

## Executive Summary
- The report presents the Jewish Federations of North America's Israel & Overseas Committee Designated Allocations Task Force's guide and recommendations for supporting people in Israel with disabilities.
- Up to 20% of Israel's population—around two million people—live with disabilities (psychosocial, physical, sensory, developmental, learning, intellectual, chronic illnesses).
- Israel has a strong legislative framework, but uneven implementation, regional disparities, bureaucratic complexity, and persistent gaps limit realization of rights.
- Urgency intensified since October 7, 2023: tens of thousands of Israelis have sustained permanent physical and psychosocial disabilities.
- The report offers guiding principles: prioritize programs not funded by government; support innovation with pathways to government adoption; invest in capital and human resources to maximize public funding; increase attention to underserved populations.
- It recommends eighteen vetted nonprofits plus continued support for JDC's Israel Unlimited (JDC-IU).

## Introduction
- The Task Force seeks to support Jewish Federations' overseas work with a more data-informed approach.
- It chose to examine support for Israelis with disabilities after first exploring Ethiopian Israeli integration (2023).
- Goal: develop a comprehensive resource introducing leading nonprofits, outlining strategic funding priorities, and providing allocation recommendations.

## Background Information
- Approximately 20% of Israel's 10 million residents live with disabilities.
- Legislative foundations include:
  - Equal Rights for Persons with Disabilities Law (1998)
  - Rehabilitation in the Community of Persons with Mental Disabilities Law (2000)
  - Inclusion Amendment to Special Education Law (2002)
  - Ratification of UN CRPD
  - Welfare Services for People with Disabilities Law (2012)
  - Employment integration and accessibility legislation
- Nearly 20,000 Israelis with disabilities still live in closed institutions; government committed to advancing community-based frameworks.
- Implementation uneven across regions and subpopulations; disparities exacerbated for Arab citizens, people in poverty, and those struggling with bureaucracy.
- Many government authorities involved: National Insurance Institute, Ministry of Welfare and Social Affairs, Ministry of Health, Kupot Holim, Ministry of Education, Ministry of Labor, Ministry of Defense, Ministry of Justice (Commission for Equal Rights), Ministry of Transportation, Local Authorities.

## October 7th: Impact on Israelis with Disabilities
- As of December 2025:
  - 20,000+ soldiers wounded since Oct. 7
  - 80,000 total wounded veterans from all conflicts
  - 58% of wounded soldiers experienced emotional harm
  - 38% dealing exclusively with PTSD
  - 1,000 new disability claims filed monthly
  - $2.5B annual government budget to treat wounded soldiers

## Current Funding Landscape
- Israeli government is the primary funder (billions per year).
- Ministry of Welfare and Social Affairs Disabilities Administration:
  - $671M over five years
  - plus $135M annually (within the 2022 Welfare Services for People with Disabilities Law framework)
- Philanthropy fills gaps, supports innovation and pilots, capital development, advocacy.

## Recommendations
- Support disability programming not funded by government:
  - accessibility
  - advocacy
  - public awareness
  - technology
  - accessing rights
  - research
- For government-funded nonprofits:
  - fund new/innovative approaches with a pathway to government adoption
  - invest in physical infrastructure or human resources to maximize government funding
  - provide additional services only where government does not fund and clear need exists
  - consider prioritizing underserved populations (adults, minorities)

## Recommended Organizations (18 + JDC-IU)

### Bizchut (relevant to this request)
- **Mission:** ensure people with disabilities can fully exercise rights to dignity, equality, autonomy, and freedom.
- **Scope:** advances rights for all types of disabilities in every aspect of life.
- **Five focus areas:**
  - independent living in the community
  - fair employment
  - participation in policymaking (“nothing about us without us”)
  - elimination of use of restraints in institutional settings
  - access to justice
- **Independence & funding:** Receives **no government funding** to maintain independence.
- **Main challenges:** directing public attention to people with disabilities during national crisis and war when attention is elsewhere—precisely when risks are heightened; raising philanthropic funds when donors prioritize direct aid over systemic change.
- **Capacity & budget:** eight staff; annual budget around **$600,000**.
- **Example cited in report:** petition to the Supreme Court on behalf of Tamar (deaf) demanding accessible means for missile alerts; government committed to act and launched a pilot of accessible emergency alert devices.
