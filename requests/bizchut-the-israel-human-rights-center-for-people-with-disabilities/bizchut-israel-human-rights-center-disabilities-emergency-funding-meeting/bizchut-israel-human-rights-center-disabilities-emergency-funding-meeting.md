## The Ask

Bizchut is requesting:
1. **An introduction meeting** to present current projects.
2. **Emergency war-related funding support** (amount not specified), asking whether emergency funds are available.
3. Consideration of **any support, including small amounts**, to sustain urgent disability-rights work during the current war period (referred to as "Lion's Roar" / "Roar of the Lion").

**Funding Requested:** Not specified  
**Non-Financial Ask:** Meeting to present active projects and discuss support options.

## Context & Background

Bizchut is a leading Israeli disability rights organization focused on dignity, equality, autonomy, and freedom for people with disabilities. It has over 30 years of experience, eight staff, and an annual budget of approximately $600,000. Bizchut receives **no government funding** in order to preserve independence in advocacy.

The March 5, 2026 outreach follows prior contact from about a year earlier regarding support. The message references and attaches the Jewish Federations of North America Israel & Overseas Committee Designated Allocations Task Force report (February 2026), in which Bizchut is one of **18 recommended organizations** for support.

Bizchut reports that war conditions increased urgency and prompted temporary/pivoted focus toward emergency disability-rights issues. They frame this as a period when people with disabilities face heightened risk while public attention and donor behavior may shift toward direct relief over systemic rights protections.

## Current Wartime Advocacy Priorities

1. **Accessible emergency alerts for deaf and hard-of-hearing people**  
   - Bizchut petitioned the Israeli Supreme Court regarding inaccessible Home Front Command alerts.  
   - A recent urgent motion was filed.  
   - The state reportedly began work on a solution; reporting references include Ynet.  
   - Report case example: Tamar (deaf) and pilot accessible alert devices.

2. **Return priority for foreign care workers**  
   - Bizchut wrote to the Transportation Minister to prioritize return of foreign/home-care workers stranded abroad after vacation.  
   - Rationale: tens of thousands of people with disabilities and elderly depend on these workers; absence creates severe risk and family burden.  
   - Coverage cited in one source: Israel Hayom.

3. **Accessible protected spaces**  
   - Bizchut argues accessibility regulations under the Equal Rights for Persons with Disabilities Law have not been enacted for over 20 years.  
   - Actions include advocacy to the Prime Minister’s Office and Ministry of Defense, legal/policy pressure with partners, and request for urgent Knesset discussion.  
   - One source notes a Knesset discussion was scheduled for the following week; another describes continued advocacy demands.

# Supporting Israelis with Disabilities: A Strategic Guide for Jewish Federations (February 2026)

## Executive Summary

- The Designated Allocations Task Force provides recommendations for supporting Israelis with disabilities.
- Up to **20% of Israel’s population** (about two million people) live with disabilities (psychosocial, physical, sensory, developmental, learning, intellectual, chronic illness).
- Israel has strong legislation but uneven implementation, regional disparities, bureaucratic complexity, and persistent service gaps.
- Since October 7, 2023, urgency increased due to growth in permanent physical and psychosocial disabilities.
- Government remains the primary funder; philanthropy is positioned to fill gaps, support innovation, advocacy, infrastructure, and organizational capacity.
- Recommends **18 vetted nonprofits** plus continued support for **JDC-Israel Unlimited (JDC-IU)**.

## Background Information

- Population context: ~20% of ~10M residents live with disabilities.
- Legislative framework includes:
  - Equal Rights for Persons with Disabilities Law (1998)
  - Rehabilitation in the Community of Persons with Mental Disabilities Law (2000)
  - Inclusion Amendment to Special Education Law (2002)
  - UN CRPD ratification
  - Welfare Services for People with Disabilities Law (2012)
  - Employment/accessibility-related legislation (including 2022 reforms)
- ~20,000 people with disabilities still live in closed institutions; policy direction favors community-based alternatives.
- Disparities are worse for Arab citizens, people in poverty, and people facing bureaucratic barriers.
- Multi-agency system includes National Insurance Institute, Welfare, Health, Kupot Holim, Education, Labor, Defense, Justice (Commission for Equal Rights), Transportation, and local authorities.

## October 7th Impact Metrics (as cited)

- 20,000+ soldiers wounded since Oct. 7 (as of Dec 2025)
- 80,000 total wounded veterans from all conflicts
- 58% of wounded soldiers experienced emotional harm
- 38% dealing exclusively with PTSD
- ~1,000 new disability claims monthly
- ~$2.5B annual government budget for treatment of wounded soldiers

## Current Funding Landscape & Federation Allocations

- Ministry of Welfare Disabilities Administration: $671M over five years + $135M annually (person-centered framework under disability services law context).
- Jewish Federations designated allocations (2021–2024):
  - 2021: $1.27M
  - 2022: $1.49M
  - 2023: $1.87M
  - 2024: $1.65M
- One extracted version also reports corresponding grant/grantee counts by year.
- Philadelphia is listed among federations allocating to disability-serving organizations in 2023–2024.
- JDC-IU core allocation examples:
  - 2023: 2.4% of $29.5M = $713,414
  - 2024: 3.4% of $32.5M = $1,098,277

## Recommendations

- Prioritize disability programming not funded by government (advocacy, accessibility, rights access, public awareness, technology, research).
- For government-funded entities, support:
  - innovation with pathway to government adoption,
  - capital/human resources to maximize public funds,
  - additional services only where government support is absent and need is clear.
- Increase focus on underserved populations (including adults and minorities).

## Bizchut Profile in Report (Relevant to Request)

- Mission: ensure rights to dignity, equality, autonomy, and freedom for people with disabilities.
- Focus areas: independent living, fair employment, participation in policymaking ("nothing about us without us"), ending restraints in institutions, access to justice.
- Over 30 years of work and strong credibility across sectors.
- Receives **no government funding** to preserve advocacy independence.
- Key challenge: maintaining attention to disability rights during crisis/war while fundraising conditions favor direct aid.
- Organization size: **8 staff**, annual budget around **$600,000**.
- Included case: Supreme Court petition for accessible missile alerts on behalf of a deaf woman (Tamar), followed by government commitment and pilot implementation efforts.
