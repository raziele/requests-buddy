## The Ask

Bizchut is requesting:
1. An introduction/working meeting to present its current projects.
2. Emergency war-related funding support for ongoing disability-rights work during the current conflict (referred to as "Lion's Roar"/"Roar of the Lion").

Bizchut notes prior contact with Federation stakeholders approximately one year earlier and references the Jewish Federations of North America (JFNA) Designated Allocations Task Force report (February 2026), where Bizchut is listed among 18 recommended organizations.

**Funding Requested:** Not specified (any amount would help).  
**Non-Financial Ask:** Meeting/introduction to discuss projects and potential support.

## Context & Background

Bizchut is a leading Israeli disability-rights organization focused on dignity, equality, autonomy, and freedom for people with disabilities across all life domains. It reports receiving no government funding in order to preserve independent advocacy.

In wartime, Bizchut redirected substantial activity to urgent disability-related issues:

1. **Accessible emergency alerts (Home Front Command)**
   - Alerts were inaccessible for deaf and hard-of-hearing people.
   - Bizchut petitioned the Supreme Court (initial petition about two years earlier), later filed an urgent motion, and advanced public/media attention (including Ynet coverage).
   - The state began working on a solution; reporting references a pilot of accessible emergency alert devices.

2. **Return of foreign care workers**
   - Bizchut urged the Transportation Minister to prioritize return of foreign home-care workers stranded abroad.
   - Rationale: tens of thousands of people with disabilities and elderly people depend on these workers; prolonged absence creates severe risk and family burden.
   - Israel Hayom coverage was cited.

3. **Accessible protected spaces**
   - Bizchut highlighted longstanding regulatory gaps under the Equal Rights for Persons with Disabilities Law (described as not enacted for over 20 years).
   - Actions included advocacy to the Prime Minister’s Office and Ministry of Defense, legal/policy pressure with partners, and a request for urgent Knesset deliberation.
   - A Knesset discussion was reported as scheduled for the following week.

Additional organizational context from the attached report:
- Over 30 years of activity.
- 8 staff.
- Approximate annual budget: **$600,000**.
- Core challenge: keeping disability rights visible during national crisis while donors prioritize direct relief over systemic rights-based change.

### 2025-RESOURCES-FOR-SUPPORTING-ISRAELIS-WITH-DISABILITIES-REPORT (1).pdf

# Supporting Israelis with Disabilities: A Strategic Guide for Jewish Federations (February 2026)

## Executive Summary

- Produced by JFNA’s Israel & Overseas Committee Designated Allocations Task Force.
- Estimates up to **20% of Israel’s population** (about **2 million people**) live with disabilities.
- Finds strong legal framework but uneven implementation, regional disparities, bureaucracy, and persistent service gaps.
- Notes post–Oct 7 escalation in disability needs (physical and psychosocial), and emphasizes philanthropy’s role in gaps government does not fund.
- Recommends guiding principles:
  - prioritize non-government-funded domains;
  - support innovations with pathway to government adoption;
  - invest in capital/human resources to maximize public funding leverage;
  - increase focus on underserved populations.
- Recommends **18 vetted nonprofits** plus continued support for **JDC-Israel Unlimited (JDC-IU)**.

## Background Information

- Key legal/policy framework includes:
  - Equal Rights for Persons with Disabilities Law (1998)
  - Rehabilitation in the Community of Persons with Mental Disabilities Law (2000)
  - Inclusion Amendment to Special Education Law (2002)
  - UN CRPD ratification
  - Welfare Services for People with Disabilities Law (2012)
  - Employment/accessibility legislation (including 2022 references)
- Approx. **20,000** people with disabilities still in closed institutions.
- Disparities are greater for Arab citizens, people in poverty, and people facing bureaucratic barriers.
- Multi-agency system includes National Insurance Institute; Welfare, Health, Education, Labor, Defense, Justice (Commission for Equal Rights), Transportation; Kupot Holim; and local authorities.

## October 7 Impact (report figures)

- As of Dec 2025:
  - **20,000+** soldiers wounded since Oct 7
  - **80,000** total wounded veterans from all conflicts
  - **58%** of wounded soldiers experienced emotional harm
  - **38%** dealing exclusively with PTSD
  - ~**1,000** new disability claims monthly
  - ~**$2.5B** annual government budget to treat wounded soldiers

## Current Funding Landscape

- Government is the primary funder (billions annually).
- Ministry of Welfare Disabilities Administration figures cited: **$671M over five years + $135M annually**.
- Jewish Federations designated allocations for disability programs:
  - 2021: **$1.27M**
  - 2022: **$1.49M**
  - 2023: **$1.87M**
  - 2024: **$1.65M**
- One extraction lists 2021–2024 grant activity as:
  - 2021: 67 grants / 28 grantees
  - 2022: 70 / 31
  - 2023: 82 / 27
  - 2024: 81 / 28
- Another extraction summarizes grantee count as **28–31 nonprofits/year**.
- JDC-IU core allocation references:
  - 2023: 2.4% of $29.5M = **$713,414**
  - 2024: 3.4% of $32.5M = **$1,098,277**
- Philadelphia is noted among federations allocating disability-focused funds (2023–2024).

## Recommendations

- Fund disability programming not funded by government:
  - accessibility
  - advocacy
  - public awareness
  - technology
  - access-to-rights support
  - research
- For government-funded nonprofits, prioritize:
  - innovation with adoption pathways
  - infrastructure and human capacity investments
  - additional services only where unmet need is clear
  - underserved populations (including adults and minorities)
