## The Ask

Bizchut is requesting:
1. **An introduction/working meeting** to present current projects.
2. **Emergency war-related funding support** (amount not specified) for disability-rights work during the current conflict (referred to as "Lion's Roar" / "Roar of the Lion").

They note prior contact about support approximately a year earlier and reference the Jewish Federations of North America (JFNA) Task Force report (February 2026), where Bizchut is one of 18 recommended organizations.

**Funding Requested:** Not specified  
**Non-Financial Ask:** Meeting to present current projects and discuss potential support.

## Context & Background

Bizchut is a leading Israeli disability-rights organization focused on dignity, equality, autonomy, freedom, and full social inclusion. It reports receiving **no government funding** to preserve advocacy independence.

The request follows renewed outreach to North American Federation contacts after previous discussions and alongside publication of the JFNA Designated Allocations Task Force report on supporting Israelis with disabilities. Bizchut was consulted for that report and is listed among recommended grantees.

The organization has redirected substantial attention to urgent wartime disability-rights needs and emphasizes that even small philanthropic support would help sustain advocacy and rights protection.

## Current War-Related Advocacy Priorities

1. **Accessible emergency alerts (Home Front Command)**
   - Alerts were inaccessible for deaf and hard-of-hearing people.
   - Bizchut petitioned the Supreme Court (original petition earlier; recent urgent motion filed).
   - State has begun implementing solutions, including pilot accessible alert devices.
   - Media coverage referenced (including Ynet).

2. **Return of foreign care workers**
   - Request to Transportation Minister to prioritize return of care workers stranded abroad.
   - Rationale: absence of workers puts tens of thousands of people with disabilities and elderly people at risk and increases family burden.
   - Media coverage referenced (including Israel Hayom).

3. **Accessible protected spaces**
   - Bizchut argues accessibility regulations under disability-rights law have not been enacted for over 20 years.
   - Advocacy directed to Prime Minister’s Office and Ministry of Defense; urgent Knesset discussion requested.
   - A Knesset discussion was reported as scheduled for the following week.

# Supporting Israelis with Disabilities: A Strategic Guide for Jewish Federations (February 2026)

## Executive Summary

- Produced by JFNA Israel & Overseas Committee Designated Allocations Task Force.
- Up to **20% of Israel’s population** (~2 million people) live with disabilities (psychosocial, physical, sensory, developmental, learning, intellectual, chronic illness).
- Israel has significant legal frameworks, but implementation gaps, regional disparities, bureaucratic barriers, and underserved populations persist.
- Since Oct 7, 2023, urgency has increased due to large-scale physical and psychosocial injury.
- Recommends philanthropy focus on areas government does not adequately fund: advocacy, accessibility, rights access, innovation, and capacity.
- Recommends **18 nonprofits + continued support for JDC Israel Unlimited (JDC-IU).**

## Background Highlights

- Key laws include: Equal Rights for Persons with Disabilities Law (1998), Rehabilitation in the Community (2000), Inclusion Amendment to Special Education (2002), UN CRPD ratification, Welfare Services for People with Disabilities Law (2012), and newer employment/accessibility measures.
- ~20,000 people with disabilities still live in closed institutions; transition to community-based frameworks remains a policy priority.
- Disparities are sharper for Arab citizens, people in poverty, and those with limited ability to navigate bureaucracy.
- Relevant authorities span National Insurance Institute, Welfare, Health, Education, Labor, Defense, Justice (Commissioner for Equal Rights), Transportation, Kupot Holim, and local authorities.

## October 7 Impact Metrics (as cited)

- 20,000+ soldiers wounded since Oct 7 (by Dec 2025).
- 80,000 total wounded veterans from all conflicts.
- 58% of wounded soldiers experienced emotional harm.
- 38% dealing exclusively with PTSD.
- ~1,000 new disability claims monthly.
- ~$2.5B annual government budget for treatment of wounded soldiers.

## Current Funding Landscape

- Government is the primary disability-services funder (billions/year overall).
- Ministry of Welfare Disabilities Administration: **$671M over five years + $135M annually**.
- Jewish Federations designated allocations for disability programming:
  - 2021: $1.27M
  - 2022: $1.49M
  - 2023: $1.87M
  - 2024: $1.65M
- JDC-IU core allocation examples:
  - 2023: 2.4% of $29.5M = $713,414
  - 2024: 3.4% of $32.5M = $1,098,277

## Recommendations (Task Force)

- Prioritize disability programming not funded by government.
- For government-funded nonprofits, support:
  - innovations likely to be adopted by government,
  - capital/human resources that unlock greater public funding,
  - additional services only where clear unmet need exists.
- Increase attention to underserved populations (including adults and minorities).

## Bizchut Profile in Report (Relevant to Request)

- Mission: ensure rights to dignity, equality, autonomy, freedom for all people with disabilities.
- Focus areas: independent living, fair employment, participation in policymaking (“nothing about us without us”), ending restraints in institutional settings, access to justice.
- 30+ years of activity; high credibility with institutions and policymakers.
- **No government funding** (to maintain advocacy independence).
- Reported operating scale: **8 staff**, **~$600,000 annual budget**.
- Wartime challenge: drawing attention and funding to disability rights amid crisis when donors may prioritize direct aid over systemic rights work.
- Included case example: petition regarding accessible missile alerts for Tamar (deaf), followed by government commitment and pilot rollout.
