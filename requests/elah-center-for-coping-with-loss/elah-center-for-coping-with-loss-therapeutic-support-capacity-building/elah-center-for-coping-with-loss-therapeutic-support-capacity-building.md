## The Asks

### 1. Therapeutic Support for War-Affected Individuals

Elah Center seeks philanthropic funding to provide therapeutic support for war-affected individuals who are not eligible for government assistance or for whom state frameworks are inadequate. The request covers five program areas:

1. **Support groups for grief and trauma** (two therapists per group, minimum 12 sessions, with option to extend up to 30–40 sessions).
2. **Individual therapy for grief and trauma** (20-session packages).
3. **Social-therapeutic groups for the elderly** (90-minute weekly sessions, typically 20 sessions).
4. **In-home individual therapy for homebound elderly**, including Holocaust survivors.
5. **Group and individual therapy for the Arab community** via the Kfar Kanna clinic and home-based services with Arabic-speaking therapists.

All programs use qualified therapists and are designed to strengthen resilience, reduce isolation, and support emotional recovery for populations that “fall between the cracks” of government support.

**Funding Requested:** Not specified

**Funding Breakdown (Unit Costs in NIS):**
- Support group (12 sessions, 2 therapists): NIS 36,000 (NIS 3,000 per group session with two therapists; option to extend up to 30–40 sessions).
- Individual therapy package (20 sessions): NIS 6,900 (NIS 345 per session, clinic or online).
- Elderly social-therapeutic group (20 sessions): NIS 30,000 (NIS 1,500 per 90-minute weekly session).
- In-home therapy for elderly (20 sessions): NIS 8,000 (NIS 400 per one-hour in-home session).
- Arab community group (12 sessions, ~12 participants, 90-minute weekly sessions): NIS 18,000 (NIS 1,500 per session).
- Arab community individual therapy (20 sessions): NIS 6,900 (NIS 345 per session).

### 2. National Welfare-Worker Supervision & Training Program

Elah Center also requests funding to expand a **national supervision and training program for welfare workers** in municipal social services. Following the escalation with Iran and Hezbollah and the prolonged Iron Swords War, the Center identified an urgent need to support frontline welfare staff who face secondary trauma, compassion fatigue, and overwhelming caseloads.

The program offers:
- **Ongoing group supervision** (הדרכות קבוצתיות מתמשכות) for welfare staff in municipal social services departments, regional councils, and local authorities.
- **Individual guidance and supervision** (ליווי אישי והדרכות פרטניות) for workers exposed to extreme cases and complex situations.
- **Short workshops and enrichment lectures** (סדנאות קצרות טווח ו/או הרצאות העשרה) on topics such as loss, trauma, self-care (שמירה עצמית), and related areas.

Elah recently held a Zoom support meeting for approximately 500 Jerusalem municipal welfare workers and seeks to continue and scale this service nationwide in partnership with local and government bodies.

**Funding Requested:** Not specified

## Context & Background

### Organizational Background

Elah Center has operated for decades (approximately 45 years) in trauma, bereavement, loss, and life-crisis care across Israel. The professional team includes some **600 experienced practitioners** from various fields of emotional care and support, working with deep commitment to social resilience and mental health in Israel.

The Center works both with first-circle victims and with those in broader circles affected by trauma and war, aiming to strengthen the resilience of individuals, families, communities, and the professional systems that support them.

### War Context and Unmet Needs

Since the outbreak of the Iron Swords War (October 2023), Israel has been in a prolonged state of conflict, leading to unprecedented emotional and social strain. The government has expanded psychological assistance to direct victims, and philanthropy has added substantial support, reflecting a growing recognition of psychosocial needs. Nevertheless:

- Many individuals **do not meet eligibility criteria** for government-funded assistance.
- Existing frameworks are often **insufficiently tailored** to specific circumstances and populations.
- Prolonged exposure to trauma, loss, uncertainty, and security threats intensifies vulnerabilities among civilians and professionals alike.

For these populations, Elah provides **tailored therapeutic support**, largely dependent on philanthropic funding.

### Target Populations and Gaps Addressed

1. **War-Affected Individuals Outside State Frameworks**

   Populations with urgent psychosocial needs that continue to fall between the cracks include:
   - Mothers and partners of combat soldiers.
   - Female lone soldiers.
   - Bereaved commanders.
   - Close friends of fallen soldiers and civilians.
   - Second-degree family members of fallen soldiers and civilians (Jewish and Arab).
   - Family members of wounded or traumatized soldiers.
   - First-line emergency personnel.

   Elah regularly receives requests to establish support groups and individual therapy for these populations. Philanthropic support enables timely intervention to prevent deterioration and long-term harm.

2. **Elderly and Homebound Elderly (Including Holocaust Survivors)**

   Older adults are among the most deeply impacted by the ongoing war:
   - Advanced age, declining health, and shrinking social networks increase risks of anxiety, depression, and loneliness.
   - The security situation significantly intensifies these vulnerabilities, especially for those who are homebound.

   Elah has provided trauma-informed mental health care for elderly individuals and Holocaust survivors since 1979, including:
   - Social-therapeutic groups using music, photographs, games, art, movement, animals, and gardening to foster connection, strengthen cognitive and social skills, and reduce stress and isolation.
   - In-home individual therapy, which addresses both mobility limitations and the need for stable, relationship-based care.

3. **Arab Community in Israel**

   The Arab community faces a unique mental-health burden shaped by:
   - Prolonged exposure to social stressors, violence, discrimination, and poverty.
   - Limited access to culturally sensitive mental-health services.
   - Persistent stigma around seeking help.

   Since 2010, Elah has operated a **clinic in Kfar Kanna** with a team of more than **50 Arabic-speaking therapists**, providing culturally adapted individual and group therapy in clinics and in clients' homes. The recent war has further increased psychological distress and demand for services.

4. **Welfare Workers in Social Services Departments**

   Welfare workers in municipal social services, regional councils, and local authorities face:
   - Heavy caseloads and responsibility for diverse and vulnerable populations.
   - Ongoing exposure to extreme cases and distressing situations.
   - The need to provide creative responses in the face of limited budgets and a challenging social, economic, and security reality.

   Since October 2023 and especially with the escalation and war with Iran, more and more people are affected, and the pressure on welfare workers is intensifying. Prolonged exposure puts them at real risk of:
   - Vicarious trauma.
   - Secondary traumatization.
   - Compassion fatigue and emotional exhaustion.

   Strengthening welfare workers through supervision, personal support, and training is essential for maintaining Israel’s social resilience and ensuring continuity of care for citizens.

## Program Structure & Objectives

### Therapeutic Programs for Individuals and Groups

Across the five therapeutic program types, the objectives are to:
- Support emotional processing of participants’ unique experiences of grief, trauma, and war-related stress.
- Strengthen internal resilience, coping skills, and personal and social identity.
- Reduce social isolation and build supportive peer networks.
- Prevent deterioration into severe depression, anxiety, and suicidality.
- Enable participants to maintain personal, family, and professional functioning despite ongoing challenges.

Regular group engagement provides a sense of belonging and shared understanding, while individual therapy offers personalized, deep work for those who require it. For the elderly and Holocaust survivors, the emphasis is on continuity of care, dignity, and maintaining quality of life. For the Arab community, the focus is on culturally adapted, language-accessible services delivered by trusted professionals.

### National Welfare-Worker Supervision & Training

The welfare-worker program is designed as a **dedicated and comprehensive support system** for social-services staff, with the following components:

- **Group Supervision (הדרכות קבוצתיות מתמשכות):**
  - Ongoing supervision groups enabling workers to process difficult cases, share burdens, and receive professional guidance.
  - Focus on vicarious trauma, boundaries, ethical dilemmas, and self-care strategies.

- **Individual Guidance and Supervision (ליווי אישי והדרכות פרטניות):**
  - Personalized support for workers dealing with particularly complex or emotionally challenging situations.
  - Emphasis on preventing burnout, compassion fatigue, and functional collapse.

- **Short-Term Workshops and Enrichment Lectures (סדנאות קצרות טווח / הרצאות העשרה):**
  - Thematic sessions on loss, trauma, self-care, resilience-building, and related topics.
  - May combine experiential elements with psychoeducation and practical tools.

Elah has already delivered such services over the past year **with great success**, including a Zoom meeting for ~500 welfare workers in Jerusalem. The organization seeks to continue and expand this program nationwide, in collaboration with local authorities and relevant government ministries.

### Rationale and Expected Impact

- **For Individuals and Families:** Timely, professional therapeutic support will mitigate the long-term psychological impact of war, reduce isolation, and promote recovery, especially for those outside formal entitlement systems.
- **For the Elderly and Holocaust Survivors:** Social-therapeutic groups and in-home therapy help preserve functioning, reduce loneliness, and maintain mental health in a highly vulnerable population.
- **For the Arab Community:** Culturally adapted services strengthen trust, reduce stigma, and improve access to quality mental-health care.
- **For Welfare Workers:** Strengthened welfare staff are better able to continue providing vital services to the public, ensuring continuity of care and maintaining the social safety net during an extended crisis.

Overall, Elah emphasizes that it already possesses the **professional knowledge, therapists, and infrastructure** needed to implement these programs nationwide. **The only missing component is funding.** The Center invites philanthropic partners to join in raising resources to provide these responses and build a more just and resilient society.

# Project Proposals – Elah Center for Coping with Loss

Since the outbreak of the Iron Swords War, the Israeli government has significantly expanded the provision of psychological assistance to direct victims, alongside substantial support from philanthropic organizations. This reflects a growing and encouraging recognition of the profound psychosocial needs created by the war.

Nevertheless, experience from the field indicates that many individuals continue to fall between the cracks—either because they are not eligible for government funding or because existing frameworks do not adequately address their specific circumstances. For these individuals, the Elah Center seeks to provide appropriate, tailored therapeutic support, made possible through philanthropic donations.

## 1. Support Groups for Individuals Coping with Grief or Trauma

Within the population affected by the war, there are individuals with urgent psychosocial needs who do not meet the criteria for government-funded assistance. These include, among others: mothers and partners of combat soldiers, female lone soldiers, bereaved commanders, close friends of fallen soldiers and civilians, second-degree family members of fallen soldiers or civilians (both Jewish and Arab), family members of wounded or traumatized soldiers, and first-line emergency personnel. Requests for support groups for these populations are received regularly by the Elah Center.

Given the emotional intensity and complexity of the issues addressed, each group is facilitated by two qualified therapists. Groups meet for a minimum of 12 sessions, with the option to extend to 30 or even 40 sessions, depending on participants' needs and the availability of funding.

- **Cost per session (2 therapists):** NIS 3,000  
- **Total cost for 12-session group:** NIS 36,000

## 2. Individual Therapy for People Coping with Grief or Trauma

Many individuals affected by the war do not meet the criteria for government-funded psychological care and require individualized support that cannot be provided in group settings. Philanthropic support enables timely intervention for people experiencing significant grief or trauma, preventing further deterioration and long-term harm.

- **Cost per one-hour session (clinic or online):** NIS 345  
- **Basic package (20 sessions):** NIS 6,900

## 3. Social-Therapeutic Support Groups for the Elderly

Older adults in Israel are among the populations most deeply affected by the prolonged state of war. Advanced age, declining health, and increasing social isolation already place many elderly individuals at heightened risk for anxiety, depression, and loneliness; the ongoing security situation has significantly intensified these vulnerabilities.

Demand for the Elah Center's social-therapeutic groups for the elderly continues to grow. Drawing on 45 years of experience, Elah has developed therapeutic methods specifically adapted to this population. Facilitated by professional therapists, the groups employ music, photographs, games, art, movement, animals, and gardening to foster meaningful social connections, strengthen cognitive and social skills, and reduce war-related stress, anxiety, and isolation.

- **Participants per group:** ~12  
- **Session length:** 90 minutes weekly  
- **Cost per session:** NIS 1,500  
- **Total cost for 20-session group:** NIS 30,000

## 4. Individual Therapy for Homebound Elderly

Many elderly individuals, including a significant number of Holocaust survivors, are not eligible for state-funded assistance. As mobility decreases and social networks shrink, access to appropriate mental health care becomes both more urgent and more difficult. The Elah Center has provided specialized, trauma-informed mental health care for elderly individuals and Holocaust survivors since 1979.

- **Cost per one-hour in-home session:** NIS 400  
- **Basic package (20 sessions):** NIS 8,000

## 5. Group and Individual Therapy for the Arab Community in Israel

The Arab community in Israel faces a unique mental-health burden shaped by prolonged exposure to social stressors, violence, discrimination, and poverty. The recent war has further intensified this reality. Access to culturally sensitive mental-health services remains limited, and stigma continues to prevent many from seeking help.

Since 2010, the Elah Center has operated a clinic in Kfar Kanna, providing culturally adapted therapeutic services through a professional team of more than 50 Arabic-speaking therapists. Individual and group therapy are offered in clinics or in clients' homes.

- **Group:** ~12 participants, 90-minute weekly sessions. Cost per session NIS 1,500; 12-session group NIS 18,000.  
- **Individual:** NIS 345/session; 20-session package NIS 6,900.

## Objectives

All programs support the emotional processing of participants' unique experiences while strengthening internal resilience, coping skills, and personal and social identity. Regular group engagement fosters a sense of belonging, reduces isolation, and helps build supportive peer networks. The programs contribute to the prevention of isolation and suicide, enhance emotional recovery, and enable participants to maintain personal and professional functioning.

## Conclusion

Elah Center possesses the professional knowledge, therapists, and infrastructure required to provide this support nationwide. The only missing component is funding. We hope you will be our partner in raising resources to provide this response and build a more just and resilient society.
