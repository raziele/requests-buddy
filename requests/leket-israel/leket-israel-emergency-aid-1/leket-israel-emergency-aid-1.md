## The Ask

Leket Israel, Israel's national food bank, is requesting emergency support to sustain food distribution during the current security crisis. Since the escalation with Iran, surplus food available for rescue has dropped sharply due to disruptions across the food supply chain: volunteer activity is paused, farmers have halted harvests, corporate offices are closed, and IDF bases (Leket's largest suppliers of cooked food) are not permitting entry.

Operational impacts described across the emails include:
- Daily meal distribution dropping from **8,000 to 3,000** (requiring purchase of additional meals to bridge the gap).
- A significant reduction in produce rescue (requiring temporary produce purchases).
- Continued effort to supply hundreds of nonprofit partners, with **supermarket vouchers** used where families cannot access distribution points.
- Temporary supplementation of rescued food with **purchased food** to maintain continuity.

## Context & Background

Since the outbreak of war with Iran, daily life across Israel has been marked by deep uncertainty: sirens interrupt routines, families move in and out of shelters, and widespread disruption touches nearly every part of daily life. Leket Israel emphasizes that food security is central to community resilience and trauma response; when parents know nourishing food will be available, they are better able to care for their children and cope with the period’s challenges.

The security situation has disrupted the networks that enable food rescue. Rocket fire and the broader instability forced a pause in volunteer activity and limited collection and distribution operations. Farmers halted harvests, corporate offices closed, and IDF bases did not permit entry—reducing access to cooked food and shrinking the surplus pipeline that typically supports Leket’s nonprofit partners.

Despite these challenges, Leket continues to work to ensure food reaches vulnerable families through a mix of strategies:
- Maintaining (and where possible increasing) regular distribution levels.
- Temporarily supplementing rescued food with purchased food.
- Providing supermarket vouchers for families who cannot access distribution points.

Maintaining these operations is described as requiring an additional **$508,210 per week**, primarily to cover the meals gap (distribution dropping from 8,000 to 3,000/day, with purchase needed for 5,000 additional meals daily) and the produce gap (farmers’ inability to harvest drastically reducing rescued produce, requiring significant temporary produce purchases). The sender(s) note that multiple Leket grant requests may already be under review by the receiving federation; this outreach specifically addresses the emergency needs created by the current situation.
