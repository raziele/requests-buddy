## The Ask

The Israel Women's Network requests funding to expand **The Alice Line** (קו אליס), Israel's national women's rights response center, during the current war with Iran. In the first three days of the escalation the hotline received nearly ten times its normal call volume (the equivalent of an entire month's calls in three days; one source describes "hundreds of inquiries" in three days). Funding would expand hotline capacity (multilingual counselors and legal staff), strengthen policy advocacy with government ministries as emergency regulations are drafted, and expand outreach and digital resources so women can access information on employment rights, reserve-spouse benefits, and compensation. ## Context & Background

Since the escalation of the war with Iran, Israel has returned to full emergency mode: schools are closed indefinitely, only essential workers may report to work, and tens of thousands (over 100,000 in some sources) of reservists have been called to military service. Women across Israel are experiencing immediate disruptions to employment, caregiving responsibilities, and economic stability. The Alice Line was established during the war that began in October 2023 as a national support and policy platform to help women navigate emergency regulations, access their rights, and address systemic gaps in government responses. It functions as both a direct assistance center and a policy intelligence hub, allowing IWN to identify emerging problems in real time and advocate for policy changes. In times of war, women's needs are often not integrated into national decision-making; policy is drafted quickly without gender analysis, and the absence of women in leadership roles means their perspectives are missing from decision-making.

In the first three days of the current escalation, the Alice Line received the equivalent of an entire month's volume of calls—nearly ten times the normal rate (or hundreds of inquiries). Women are calling with urgent questions about employment rights, childcare obligations, unpaid leave (חל״ת / halat), small business collapse, benefits for families and partners of reservists, property damage, compensation claims, and basic legal protections. During the previous phase of the war, information gathered through the Alice Line led directly to significant policy changes, including expanding eligibility for childbirth allowances for women in reserve duty, ensuring employment protections for partners of reservists, securing economic benefits for workers from evacuated communities, and correcting discriminatory compensation regulations.

**Case example (Rachel):** Rachel is a 34-year-old mother of two. When the war with Iran began, schools and childcare in her area were closed. Her partner was called back to reserve duty after already serving more than 300 days during the war in Gaza. Overnight, Rachel was alone with two small children and no childcare. The Ministry of Finance announced that parents whose children cannot attend school may take unpaid leave (halat). She was unsure of her rights: compensation, rights vis-à-vis her employer, and how to ensure family income while her partner served. The legal team explained her rights under emergency regulations and advised on steps with her employer and on financial grants for partners of reservists.

**Systemic challenges reflected in calls:** Women pushed out of work when schools close; self-employed women and small business owners losing income; partners of reservists managing households alone; women in precarious employment losing shifts; emergency regulations drafted without a gender lens; women facing violence locked in at home; women without safe rooms in greatest danger.

IWN seeks to expand hotline capacity (multilingual counselors and legal staff), recruit and train additional legal staff, increase policy advocacy with government ministries as emergency regulations are drafted, expand multilingual outreach and advertising, develop and update accessible webinars and digital content on women's rights, and create guidelines for employers. The detailed request covers a six-month period (April–September 2026) at a total cost of $199,922 (639,750 ILS, at 3.2 ILS per USD).

### The Alice Line during the War with Iran (1).pdf

**The Alice Line: Emergency Expansion During the Iran War**

**Overview**

The Israel Women's Network (IWN) is launching an emergency expansion of The Alice Line, Israel's national women's rights response center, to address the urgent needs of women during the current war with Iran. Since the escalation began, Israel has returned to full emergency mode: schools are closed indefinitely, only essential workers may report to work, and tens of thousands of reservists have been called to military service. In this environment, women across Israel are experiencing immediate disruptions to employment, caregiving responsibilities, and economic stability.

The Alice Line was established during the war that began in October 2023 as a national support and policy platform to help women navigate emergency regulations, access their rights, and address systemic gaps in government responses. The Alice Line is both a direct assistance center and a policy intelligence hub, allowing IWN to identify emerging problems in real time and advocate for policy changes before discriminatory or harmful systems become entrenched.

With the outbreak of the new war with Iran, the need has intensified dramatically. In the first three days of the current escalation, the Alice Line received the equivalent of an entire month's volume of calls—nearly ten times the normal rate. Women are calling with urgent questions about employment rights, childcare obligations, unpaid leave, small business collapse, and benefits for families of reservists.

**Rachel's story**

Rachel is a 34-year-old mother of two young children. When the war with Iran began, schools and childcare in her area were closed. Her partner was called back to reserve duty after already serving more than 300 days during the war in Gaza. Overnight, Rachel was alone at home with two small children and no childcare. The Ministry of Finance announced that parents whose children cannot attend school may take unpaid leave (halat). Rachel was unsure of her rights: compensation, rights vis-à-vis her employer, and how to ensure family income while her partner served. The legal team explained her rights under emergency regulations and advised on steps with her employer and on financial grants for partners of reservists.

Systemic challenges reflected in calls: women pushed out of work when schools close; self-employed women and small business owners losing income; partners of reservists managing households alone; women in precarious employment losing shifts; emergency regulations drafted without gender lens; women facing violence locked in at home; women without safe rooms in greatest danger.

**The Solution**

The Alice Line operates at two levels:

(1) **Support:** Direct legal and bureaucratic assistance in multiple languages on employment rights during emergency shutdowns, reserve-spouse benefits, compensation eligibility, and other urgent issues.

(2) **Advocacy:** Collecting real-time information to enable legal and policy teams to intervene quickly with government ministries, the Civil Service Commission, and the Knesset to correct discriminatory policies and close legal gaps.

(3) **Protecting women in the workplace:** Creating written and visual guidelines to educate employers and ensure they protect employees' rights during the war emergency.

The IWN track record: During the previous phase of the war, information from the Alice Line led to policy changes including: expanding eligibility for childbirth allowances for women in reserve duty; ensuring employment protections for partners and co-parents of reservists; securing economic benefits for workers from evacuated communities; correcting discriminatory regulations that excluded women from key compensation.

**Request**

To meet urgent needs created by the current escalation, IWN seeks support to expand the Alice Line's emergency response capacity. Funding will allow IWN to: expand hotline capacity; increase legal staffing; strengthen policy advocacy with government ministries; expand multilingual outreach; develop accessible webinars and digital resources.
