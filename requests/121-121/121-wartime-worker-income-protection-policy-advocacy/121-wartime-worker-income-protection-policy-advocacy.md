## The Ask

121 requests support for its wartime project to protect workers whose income was reduced or interrupted during the "Roar of the Lion" war period. The organization is advancing urgent policy and legislative changes to close compensation gaps (especially for hourly workers), promote a "flexible employment" model that allows partial work with state income completion, and sustain coordinated advocacy, research, coalition-building, and public communications to secure a broader economic safety net.

**Funding Requested:** NIS 120,000 (total project budget; donor asked to contribute any amount)

**Funding Breakdown:**
- Management and strategy: NIS 11,000
- Policy advancement: NIS 25,000
- Coalition/partnership management: NIS 18,000
- Field work: NIS 11,000
- Research and policy development: NIS 20,000
- Communications: NIS 10,000
- Digital and new media: NIS 10,000
- Overhead: NIS 15,000

**Non-Financial Ask:** Partnership and backing for policy advocacy efforts with government decision-makers and public communications to advance legislative reform.

## Context & Background

The attached request describes a recurring wartime labor-market failure in Israel: while parts of the economy reopen, education and childcare frameworks often remain only partially active, leaving many workers (especially parents) unable to return fully to work. According to the document, existing unpaid-leave ("חל\"ת") mechanisms do not adequately cover large groups whose earnings are materially reduced, particularly hourly workers who are about 31% of the workforce and disproportionately from weaker populations.

121 reports that since the October 2023 war period it has led multi-organization advocacy to expand economic protections for affected workers. It cites prior policy gains, including reduced eligibility periods and monthly grants for workers aged 67+, but states major gaps persist. In the current "Roar of the Lion" war context, it is pursuing two parallel tracks: (1) closing remaining compensation gaps for uncovered worker groups and (2) legislating a "flexible employment" model that would allow partial work plus state income supplementation instead of full unpaid leave.

The organization frames this as both a social and fiscal priority: preserving employment attachment, preventing poverty deterioration among vulnerable workers, and reducing state costs relative to full unemployment-style support. The request seeks resources to continue coalition operations, policy analysis, direct government engagement, and communications campaigns during the war period and after, as temporary government decisions evolve and longer-term legislative solutions are pursued.

**Date:** March 2026  
**Subject:** Request for support for the project "Protection for Workers in Emergencies" (following the "Roar of the Lion" war)

#### Main request
- 121 asks for financial support to continue a policy project focused on workers and employers harmed by wartime disruptions.
- The project emphasizes two parallel goals:
  1. Build and advance compensation mechanisms for workers left without adequate government response (especially hourly workers).
  2. Promote a "Flexible Employment" model that allows partial work with state income completion, instead of forcing full unpaid leave.

#### Problem statement and rationale
- Emergency security conditions repeatedly create a gap between formal reopening of the economy and workers' practical ability to work.
- Many education/child frameworks operate partially, forcing difficult tradeoffs for parents of young children.
- This harms workers, employers, and overall economic resilience.
- Existing unpaid-leave support does not adequately cover many workers whose income falls during emergencies.
- The document states that hourly workers are about **31% of Israel's workforce** and are largely from weaker populations.
- Additional groups cited as vulnerable under current rules include single parents, people with disabilities, newer labor-market entrants, and workers aged 67+.

#### Key policy gaps described
- Eligibility thresholds and training-period conditions are not adapted to unstable/partial employment realities.
- A 14-day absence threshold can leave many workers uncompensated when shutdown periods are shorter.
- Workers aged 67+ are not eligible for standard unemployment benefits and rely on separate grants.
- Current grants for older workers are described as too low (up to **NIS 4,000/month**).

#### Work done to date (as reported)
- Since October 7, 2023 ("Iron Swords" period), 121 says it identified major entitlement gaps and mobilized broad civil-society partnerships.
- It reports coalition activity with about **50 civil society organizations** in 2023.
- It conducted meetings with senior political/professional officials in Finance, Economy, and Labor spheres and led significant media activity.
- Reported outcomes in prior emergency frameworks include:
  - Reduction of required qualification period (e.g., from 12 to 6 months; and 3 months for people with disabilities).
  - Reduction of minimum unpaid-leave duration (e.g., from 30 to 14 days; later references to 10 days in a 2025 framework).
  - Cancellation of vacation-day offsets from unemployment support.
  - Monthly grants for people aged 67+ (up to 75% salary, capped around NIS 4,000).
- Despite progress, the request says major gaps remained, especially for hourly workers.

#### Current planned activity (project continuation)
- Continue through the "Roar of the Lion" war period and aftermath.
- Two central tracks:
  1. **Close remaining gaps** for uncovered groups (hourly workers, workers 67+, other sensitive labor groups), including adjustment of threshold rules.
  2. **Legislate Flexible Employment** so workers can keep partial employment while receiving state income supplementation.
- Operational channels listed:
  - Policy and research work.
  - Building/managing partnerships with civil-society and business actors.
  - Direct engagement with government and Knesset decision-makers.
  - Public communications campaign to build support for reforms.
