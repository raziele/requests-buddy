## The Ask

The Haifa Rape Crisis Center (HRCC) is requesting $200,000 for emergency capacity expansion due to the 'Shaagat HaAri' operation. This funding will support an additional volunteer training course, expansion of WhatsApp support hours, and strengthening immediate response capacity. The organization anticipates a 30% increase in requests for assistance following the de-escalation of the conflict.

**Funding Requested:** $200,000

**Funding Breakdown:**
- New Volunteer Training Course (Emergency Cohort): $95,000
- WhatsApp Expansion (extended hours, additional responders, supervision): $60,000
- Surge Capacity Reinforcement (intake, coordination, flexible staffing): $45,000

## Context & Background

Operation 'Shaagat HaAri' has created a climate of sirens, security alerts, and uncertainty in Israel, exacerbating the trauma of sexual violence survivors. HRCC, with a history of responding to national crises, anticipates a significant surge in demand for their services once the immediate emergency subsides. This is a pattern observed in previous conflicts, where survivors often delay seeking help during acute periods and then reach out in large numbers during calmer times. In 2025 alone, HRCC received over 13,000 requests for assistance. The current operation comes after nearly two years of sustained war and stress, potentially making survivors more vulnerable.

The organization's existing services include a 24/7 hotline and WhatsApp support. They have seen an increase in late-night contacts from survivors experiencing heightened anxiety, fear, and suicidal thoughts due to the ongoing conflict. To prepare for the anticipated post-operation surge, HRCC plans to expand their volunteer training, extend WhatsApp operating hours, and strengthen their immediate response capacity. This includes crisis response during peak hours, accompaniment to police stations, support through criminal proceedings, assistance with National Insurance hearings, and utilizing their Safe Social Space for resilience building.

**Haifa Rape Crisis Center**

**Preparing for the Post-Operation Surge: $200,000 Emergency Capacity Expansion During Operation “Shaagat HaAri”**

Dear Friends and Partners,

As Operation "Shaagat HaAri” continues and communities across Israel once again live under sirens, security alerts, and prolonged uncertainty, we are preparing for a dynamic we know well from previous periods of war.

Research and decades of field experience show that survivors of sexual violence often experience collective crises as a double trauma. During times of war, many survivors instinctively withdraw, minimize their own pain, and hesitate to seek help, feeling that their suffering is less legitimate in the face of a national emergency.

One woman recently wrote to us:

“I'm angry at myself for feeling so broken right now when other people have shrapnel falling on their homes."

This tendency to shrink inward fueled by shame and self-silencing, often leads to a worsening of symptoms: increased flashbacks, avoidance of emotional support, neglect of physical and emotional needs, and declining functioning. In many cases, it also correlates with heightened despair and increased suicide risk.

Based on what we have seen in previous conflicts, we know that when the immediate emergency subsides, and people finally have space to process what they have endured, many survivors reach out simultaneously for support.

We are preparing now for that moment.

At the Haifa Rape Crisis Center (HRCC), we have responded to every major escalation, war, and national crisis over the past four decades. One pattern remains consistent:

During acute military operations, communities function in survival mode. Emotional processing is postponed. Trauma responses are contained.

But when relative calm returns, when there is finally a quiet moment, survivors begin reaching out.

By the end of 2025, our helplines received over 13,000 requests for assistance. These calls reflected intensified emotional distress – including urgent requests for mental health support, referrals for hospitalization, worsening health and economic hardship, and a profound need to reduce isolation and rebuild anchors for daily life.

This time, the risk is even greater.

Operation "Shaagat HaAri” comes after nearly two years of sustained war, displacement, uncertainty, and cumulative national stress.

Over the past year, many survivors of sexual violence had only just begun to stabilize: returning to therapy, rebuilding routines, cautiously reclaiming work and social life.

This new operation interrupts that fragile progress.

Repeated exposure to national emergencies compounds trauma. Sirens, confinement, and renewed insecurity reactivate hypervigilance, panic responses, and loss of control, especially among women whose nervous systems have already been under prolonged strain.

Recovery is not linear. Repeated disruption can undo months of therapeutic progress.

We believe the post-operation surge may be both concentrated and intense, and we are preparing now to ensure we can respond without delay.

**1. New Volunteer Training Course (Emergency Cohort)**

**2. Expansion of WhatsApp Support Hours**

In recent years, and especially during periods of security escalation, our WhatsApp helpline has become a critical lifeline for survivors.

Many women:

* cannot speak aloud while confined to shelters or safe rooms during sirens
* live in environments where privacy is limited
* feel safer initiating contact through written messages rather than phone calls

Even now, we are already seeing how this channel becomes essential under emergency conditions. While overall call volume has not yet reached its expected peak, a growing number of contacts are occurring late at night, when survivors wake from alerts, sirens, or are unable to sleep due to anxiety.

Some messages reflect extremely difficult circumstances, including women writing that they are sheltering in a safe room together with the person who harmed them, or reaching out because escalating fear and isolation are intensifying suicidal thoughts.

To ensure we can respond to these situations in real time, we plan to:

* Extend WhatsApp operating hours
* Increase the number of trained responders available per shift

**3. Expanding Immediate Response Capacity**

To ensure we can provide timely and responsible support during the anticipated post-operation surge, additional funding will allow us to strengthen and expand the critical emergency responses we are already providing every day.

This includes:

* Expanding response coverage during peak hours, ensuring that survivors who reach out in crisis are met by a trained responder without delay.
* Maintaining and strengthening accompaniment services, including urgent accompaniment to police stations, where survivors must often gather the strength to report abuse and provide critical evidence needed to open a case.
* Continuing long-term accompaniment through criminal proceedings, supporting women who have waited years for justice and must now face investigations, testimonies, and court processes that can reopen deep trauma.
* Providing support during National Insurance (Bituach Leumi) committee hearings, many of which still take place remotely and require survivors to recount their trauma and daily struggles to secure or extend the benefits that allow them to survive.
* Utilizing our protected Safe Social Space as a resilience-building emergency response, offering survivors a physically and emotionally safe environment where they can reduce isolation, stabilize, and reconnect with a supportive community during periods of national stress.
* Strengthening coordination between the hotline, accompaniment teams, and follow-up services, ensuring continuity of care for women navigating multiple systems simultaneously during crisis.
