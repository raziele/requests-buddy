## The Ask

Ezrat Achim requests **$450,000** for a **90-day** emergency response after a missile strike in a residential neighborhood in Beit Shemesh during Operation "Shaagat HaAri." Lives were lost, homes destroyed, and hundreds of families displaced. The organization responded immediately alongside emergency and security forces and is already providing replacement medical equipment, mobility aids, hospital transportation, essential household items, daily follow-up with displaced families, and trauma care. Funds would scale food distribution (kitchen normally prepares **127,000+ hot meals/year**, now open beyond regular recipient lists), expand emergency hotline capacity (normally **~300 requests/day**), and stabilize families over the next three months.

Ezrat Achim describes itself as Beit Shemesh’s embedded medical and social safety net—not an outside relief agency—with **1,400 volunteers**, **700 neighborhood representatives**, and **27 equipment branches** (per one intake).

**Funding Requested:** $450,000

**Funding Breakdown:**
- Medical equipment & emergency supplies: $120,000
- Trauma and mental health services: $110,000
- Transportation & ambulance operations: $85,000
- Food distribution & essential family assistance: $95,000
- Volunteer coordination & emergency logistics: $40,000

**Non-Financial Ask:** Partnership and a brief call this week to share immediate plans and coordination during this window.

## Context & Background

On **Sunday** (March 2026 per one source), a missile struck a residential neighborhood in Beit Shemesh. Families fled with almost nothing; some lost loved ones; many remain in shock. Rabbi Avraham Kop and Ezrat Achim teams were on scene within minutes with volunteers and medical teams, coordinating rescue efforts and providing immediate medical and logistical support.

Today the organization supports hundreds of families whose homes were damaged or unlivable—some with relatives, others in temporary housing. Elderly and chronically ill residents lost access to medical equipment, electricity-dependent devices, or safe mobility. Current operations include replacement equipment and mobility aids, transport to hospitals and ongoing treatments, essential household items and appliances, daily follow-up, and emotional support and trauma care for children and parents. Meal distribution is expanded; the hotline runs in emergency mode around the clock **six days a week (24/6)** in one description, while another describes scaling toward **24/7** demand—treat both as urgent extended coverage with sharply higher volume (urgent medical transport, families without functioning kitchens, parents seeking psychological support for children).

Over the next 90 days, priorities align on five areas: (1) replacing emergency medical equipment and supplies, (2) expanding trauma and counseling services, (3) increasing hospital transportation and ambulance capacity, (4) scaling food distribution and essential aid, and (5) volunteer coordination and case management for displaced families. The $450,000 is framed as essential so no one in Beit Shemesh navigates the crisis alone after headlines fade.
