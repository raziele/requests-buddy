## The Ask

Ezrat Achim, Beit Shemesh's embedded medical and social safety net with 1,400 volunteers, 700 neighborhood representatives, and 27 equipment branches, requests **$450,000** for a 90-day emergency response following a missile strike that hit a residential neighborhood in Beit Shemesh on Sunday (Operation "Shaagat HaAri"). The organization was on scene within minutes under Rabbi Avraham Kop (Founder & Chairman; also ZAKA Commander of Beit Shemesh). Funds would: replace destroyed medical equipment and mobility aids; scale food distribution (normally over 127,000 hot meals/year, now open to anyone in need) and 24/6 emergency hotline capacity (normally ~300 requests/day); expand trauma and mental health services for children and families; increase hospital transportation and ambulance operations; and support volunteer coordination and case management for displaced families over the next three months.

## Context & Background

On Sunday, March 2026, a missile struck a residential neighborhood in Beit Shemesh during Operation "Shaagat HaAri." Lives were lost, homes were destroyed, and hundreds of families—including the elderly and young children—were displaced; some lost loved ones, many remain in shock. Ezrat Achim is not an outside relief agency but the city's permanent infrastructure: Rabbi Avraham Kop serves as ZAKA Commander for Beit Shemesh, and the organization's teams were on scene within minutes alongside emergency and security forces, coordinating rescue efforts and providing immediate medical and logistical support.

Today the organization supports hundreds of families whose homes were damaged or made unlivable; many are with relatives or in temporary housing. Elderly and chronically ill residents lost access to basic medical equipment, electricity-dependent devices, or safe mobility. Ezrat Achim is providing replacement medical equipment and mobility aids, transportation to hospitals and ongoing treatments, essential household items and appliances, daily follow-up with displaced families, and emotional support and trauma care for children and parents. The kitchen, which normally prepares over 127,000 hot meals a year, has opened distribution beyond regular recipient lists. The hotline, which usually handles about 300 requests a day, is now operating 24 hours a day, six days a week in emergency mode—covering urgent medical transport, families without functioning kitchens, and parents seeking psychological support for children.

The crisis will not end when headlines fade. The requested $450,000 covers the next 90 days across five areas: replacing emergency medical equipment and supplies, expanding trauma and counseling services, increasing hospital transportation and ambulance capacity, scaling food distribution and essential aid, and supporting volunteer coordination and case management for displaced families. Ezrat Achim operates as a local actor with 1,400 volunteers and 700 neighborhood representatives in the city; when something happens, they step outside their doors rather than mobilizing from afar.
