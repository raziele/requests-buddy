# Emergency Update: Supporting People with Disabilities During the Current Security Crisis – March 2026

Dear Friends,

As Israel once again comes under attack, fear and uncertainty grip the entire country. For the children and adults with disabilities we serve and for their families, these moments are not only terrifying; they are deeply destabilizing.

When sirens wail, most people can instinctively process what is happening, decide how to respond, and seek safety. For many people with disabilities, this is far more complex. Some cannot move quickly or independently. Others struggle to understand what is happening, to interpret the urgency of the situation, or to read and respond to the reactions of those around them. Even when the sirens fall silent, the impact does not end. Routines collapse, familiar anchors disappear, and anxiety, confusion, and trauma often intensify long after the immediate danger has passed.

## What we are doing right now

### 1. Emergency Hotline

We have launched a national Emergency Hotline for professionals, caregivers and families of people with disabilities. Initial contact is made via WhatsApp, with follow-up by phone or video, providing free, real-time emotional support and professional consultation. Since its launch, the hotline has supported parents, adults with disabilities and staff in residential and community frameworks. Responses have included immediate emotional guidance, practical coping strategies, referrals for continued therapy and, where needed, extended consultations and group Zoom guidance sessions. The hotline serves as a central national support channel during this period of heightened stress and uncertainty.

### 2. Virtual Support Sessions for Families

Twice weekly, for as long as the conflict continues, we are holding virtual support sessions for Beit Issie families at two different times of day to maximize participation. Each session includes guidance from a leading mental health professional, together with staff from our school, early intervention center, or other relevant departments. Sessions are offered in both Hebrew and Arabic, providing families with practical tools, emotional support, and a sense of connection during an extremely isolating time.

On a national level, Beit Issie Shapiro is also partnering with the Shalem Foundation, together with the Ministry of Welfare and Social Affairs and local municipalities, to deliver a series of four open online guidance sessions for parents and caregivers of children and adults with disabilities. Offered in both Hebrew and Arabic, these sessions focus on practical strategies for coping with stress, supporting children and adults with disabilities during emergency situations, and strengthening family resilience. The webinars are led by experienced professionals from welfare and community services across the country and are open to families nationwide seeking support during this challenging time.

### 3. Emotional and Practical Support for Staff

While supporting thousands of families, our own staff are facing the same dangers and uncertainties: spouses called up for reserve duty, damaged homes, loved ones stranded abroad, and children at home for extended periods. Our social workers at our campuses in Ra'anana and Qalanswa are providing emotional support and professional guidance to staff coping with trauma, injury, or loss. In parallel, we are offering practical assistance, including meals, children's activity and game packages, virtual entertainment for children, wellness sessions such as yoga, and small but meaningful care packages for all staff, as an expression of solidarity, appreciation, and care.

### 4. Educational and Therapeutic Support for Children

Whenever possible, we are providing virtual classes and activities for students in our special education school, alongside home visits by teachers and therapists in accordance with safety regulations. Staff are also preparing emergency virtual activities and take-home kits to help families maintain therapeutic and educational continuity during prolonged disruptions.

## Financial need

The situation is evolving daily, and needs are growing by the hour. We currently estimate that Beit Issie Shapiro's emergency response will require **$US150,000–$US200,000** for every 10–14 days the conflict continues, in order to sustain these critical services.

In addition to these emergency efforts, Beit Issie Shapiro is experiencing a significant loss of income due to the closure of services, outpatient clinics, and the postponement or cancellation of professional courses and training programs, amounting to **at least $US100,000 per week** in planned income that cannot currently be realized. At the same time, as part of our commitment to our staff and their families, Beit Issie Shapiro has chosen not to reduce salaries, even for employees who are unable to work under the current circumstances, at a cost of **approximately $US230,000 per week**.

## Impact Stories: Emotional Support Through the Emergency Hotline

### Easing Severe Anxiety

A 43-year-old man with borderline intellectual disability who uses a wheelchair has been experiencing severe anxiety since the outbreak of the war. His mother reached out to Beit Issie Shapiro's support hotline seeking guidance. Shafi Mashiach, Director of our Dual Diagnosis Unit, spoke with him directly. Although he has verbal communication abilities, his language is limited. During the conversation, Shafi gently guided him through calming breathing techniques designed to help regulate anxiety (e.g. "breathe in as if smelling a flower"). By the end of the call, he had learned practical tools to help manage his stress, and a follow-up conversation was scheduled.

### Helping a Child Feel Safe Again

A single mother of three children reached out for guidance after a frightening experience during a recent siren. One of her children, who lives with autism and cerebral palsy, had fallen while rushing to the shelter. Since then, he had refused to return there. Dafna, an Occupational Therapist from our Dual Diagnosis Unit, provided the mother with practical strategies to help rebuild the child's sense of safety: gradually reintroducing the shelter environment at calm times, with activities the child enjoys (e.g. blowing soap bubbles), so the space becomes associated with positive experiences rather than fear. These simple but concrete tools gave the mother a way to gently support her child in overcoming the trauma of the fall.
