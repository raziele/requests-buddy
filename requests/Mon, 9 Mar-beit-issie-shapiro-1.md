## Context & Background

The ongoing conflict in Israel has created a state of fear and uncertainty, which is particularly destabilizing for individuals with disabilities. Beit Issie Shapiro is addressing this by providing immediate and critical support through various channels. Their Emergency Hotline offers real-time emotional support and professional consultation. They also conduct virtual support sessions for families, educational and therapeutic activities for children, and provide support for their own staff who are also facing the crisis.

Beit Issie Shapiro is experiencing significant income loss due to program cancellations, amounting to at least $100,000 per week. Simultaneously, they are committed to maintaining staff salaries, costing approximately $230,000 per week. The organization emphasizes that their support provides immeasurable strength and a measure of calm during this turbulent time.

### Beit Issie Emergency Support - War with Iran - March 2026.pdf

Beit Issie Shapiro
Changing the lives of people with disabilities
On the Willie & Celia Trump Campus

Emergency Update: Supporting People with Disabilities
During the Current Security Crisis – March 2026

Dear Friends,

As Israel once again comes under attack, fear and uncertainty grip the entire country. For the children
and adults with disabilities we serve and for their families, these moments are not only terrifying; they
are deeply destabilizing.

When sirens wail, most people can instinctively process what is happening, decide how to respond, and
seek safety. For many people with disabilities, this is far more complex. Some cannot move quickly or
independently. Others struggle to understand what is happening, to interpret the urgency of the
situation, or to read and respond to the reactions of those around them. Even when the sirens fall silent,
the impact does not end. Routines collapse, familiar anchors disappear, and anxiety, confusion, and
trauma often intensify long after the immediate danger has passed.

What we are doing right now:

1. Emergency Hotline
We have launched a national Emergency Hotline for professionals, caregivers and families of people with
disabilities. Initial contact is made via WhatsApp, with follow-up by phone or video, providing free, real-
time emotional support and professional consultation. Since its launch, the hotline has supported
parents, adults with disabilities and staff in residential and community frameworks. Responses have
included immediate emotional guidance, practical coping strategies, referrals for continued therapy and,
where needed, extended consultations and group Zoom guidance sessions. The hotline serves as a
central national support channel during this period of heightened stress and uncertainty.

2. Virtual Support Sessions for Families
Twice weekly, for as long as the conflict continues, we are holding virtual support sessions for Beit Issie
families at two different times of day to maximize participation. Each session includes guidance from a
leading mental health professional, together with staff from our school, early intervention center, or
other relevant departments. Sessions are offered in both Hebrew and Arabic, providing families with
practical tools, emotional support, and a sense of connection during an extremely isolating time.

On a national level, Beit Issie Shapiro is also partnering with the Shalem Foundation, together with the
Ministry of Welfare and Social Affairs and local municipalities, to deliver a series of four open online
guidance sessions for parents and caregivers of children and adults with disabilities. Offered in both
Hebrew and Arabic, these sessions focus on practical strategies for coping with stress, supporting
children and adults with disabilities during emergency situations, and strengthening family resilience.
The webinars are led by experienced professionals from welfare and community services across the
country and are open to families nationwide seeking support during this challenging time.

3. Emotional and Practical Support for Staff
While supporting thousands of families, our own staff are facing the same dangers and uncertainties:
spouses called up for reserve duty, damaged homes, loved ones stranded abroad, and children at home
for extended periods. Our social workers at our campuses in Ra'anana and Qalanswa are providing
emotional support and professional guidance to staff coping with trauma, injury, or loss. In parallel, we
are offering practical assistance, including meals, children's activity and game packages, virtual
entertainment for children, wellness sessions such as yoga, and small but meaningful care packages for
all staff, as an expression of solidarity, appreciation, and care.

4. Educational and Therapeutic Support for Children
Whenever possible, we are providing virtual classes and activities for students in our special education
school, alongside home visits by teachers and therapists in accordance with safety regulations. Staff are
also preparing emergency virtual activities and take-home kits to help families maintain therapeutic and
educational continuity during prolonged disruptions.

The situation is evolving daily, and needs are growing by the hour. We currently estimate that Beit Issie
Shapiro's emergency response will require $US150,000–$US200,000 for every 10–14 days the conflict
continues, in order to sustain these critical services. Your support enables us to respond immediately,
flexibly, and with compassion, so that people with disabilities, their families, and our dedicated staff are
not left to face this crisis alone.

In addition to these emergency efforts, Beit Issie Shapiro is experiencing a significant loss of income due
to the closure of services, outpatient clinics, and the postponement or cancellation of professional
courses and training programs, amounting to at least $US100,000 per week in planned income that
cannot currently be realized. At the same time, as part of our commitment to our staff and their families,
Beit Issie Shapiro has chosen not to reduce salaries, even for employees who are unable to work under
the current circumstances, at a cost of approximately $US230,000 per week. From past crises, we know
that this decision provides immeasurable strength and a measure of calm in the storm for our colleagues
and their families.

Thank you for standing with us when it matters most, and for your consideration of the Beit Issie Shapiro
community's needs at this critical time.

Impact Stories: Emotional Support Through the Emergency Hotline
To illustrate how these efforts are making a difference in real time, we would like to share a few examples
from the many calls and consultations our teams are currently handling through the Emergency Hotline.

Easing Severe Anxiety
A 43-year-old man with borderline intellectual disability who uses a wheelchair has been experiencing
severe anxiety since the outbreak of the war. His mother reached out to Beit Issie Shapiro's support
hotline seeking guidance.

Shafi Mashiach, Director of our Dual Diagnosis Unit, spoke with him directly. Although he has verbal
communication abilities, his language is limited, making it difficult for him to express the intensity of his
fears.

During the conversation, Shafi gently guided him through calming breathing techniques designed to help
regulate anxiety. When meeting clients in person, she often uses simple imagery to make the technique
accessible, for example, encouraging them to "breathe in as if smelling a flower.” While explaining this
concept over the phone was more challenging, she carefully adapted the guidance to ensure he could
understand and practice the technique. By the end of the call, he had learned practical tools to help
manage his stress, and a follow-up conversation was scheduled to ensure continued support.

Helping a Child Feel Safe Again
A single mother of three children reached out for guidance after a frightening experience during a recent
siren. One of her children, who lives with autism and cerebral palsy, had fallen while rushing to the
shelter. Since then, he has refused to return there, leaving his mother deeply worried about how to keep
him safe during future alarms.

Dafna, an Occupational Therapist from our Dual Diagnosis Unit, provided the mother with practical
strategies to help rebuild the child's sense of safety. She suggested gradually reintroducing the shelter
environment at calm times, when there is no siren, so the space can become associated with positive
experiences rather than fear.

For example, she recommended bringing activities the child enjoys, such as blowing soap bubbles, to
create a relaxed and comforting atmosphere. These simple but concrete tools gave the mother a way to
gently support her child in overcoming the trauma of the fall.

For families already coping with the daily realities of disability, the additional pressures of war, especially
for those without protected rooms or shelters at home, can feel overwhelming. In these moments,
timely professional guidance and compassionate support can make a profound difference.
