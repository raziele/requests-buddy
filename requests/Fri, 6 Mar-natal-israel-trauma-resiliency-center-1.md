## The Ask

NATAL is providing critical mental health support to individuals and families impacted by the ongoing conflict with Iran. They have experienced a 620% increase in calls to their helpline and are operating in emergency mode to address the profound psychological needs arising from relentless sirens, missile fire, and the resulting trauma. Support extends to veterans, security personnel, and their families dealing with post-trauma and anxiety.

**Funding Requested:** Not specified

## Context & Background

Following a joint preemptive operation by Israel and the United States against Iran, Israel has experienced intense anxiety and traumatic incidents. NATAL's Helpline saw a 620% increase in calls, primarily related to anxiety and fear. The intensity of the conflict has placed a heavy burden on children, the elderly, people with disabilities, and those with pre-existing mental health conditions. Simultaneously, the 'Nefesh Achat' Helpline, operated by NATAL, is also in emergency mode, supporting disabled IDF veterans and former security personnel dealing with post-trauma and anxiety. NATAL is also providing therapeutic intervention in hotels for civilians displaced by missile strikes.

Preparing for the Post-Operation Surge: $200,000 Emergency Capacity Expansion
During Operation “Shaagat HaAri”

Dear Friends and Partners,

As Operation "Shaagat HaAri” continues and communities across Israel once again live under sirens,
security alerts, and prolonged uncertainty, we are preparing for a dynamic we know well from previous
periods of war.

Research and decades of field experience show that survivors of sexual violence often experience
collective crises as a double trauma. During times of war, many survivors instinctively withdraw, minimize
their own pain, and hesitate to seek help, feeling that their suffering is less legitimate in the face of a
national emergency.

One woman recently wrote to us:

“I'm angry at myself for feeling so broken right now when other people have shrapnel falling on their
homes."

This tendency to shrink inward fueled by shame and self-silencing, often leads to a worsening of
symptoms: increased flashbacks, avoidance of emotional support, neglect of physical and emotional
needs, and declining functioning. In many cases, it also correlates with heightened despair and increased
suicide risk.

Based on what we have seen in previous conflicts, we know that when the immediate emergency
subsides, and people finally have space to process what they have endured, many survivors reach out
simultaneously for support.

We are preparing now for that moment.

At the Haifa Rape Crisis Center (HRCC), we have responded to every major escalation, war, and national
crisis over the past four decades. One pattern remains consistent:

During acute military operations, communities function in survival mode. Emotional processing is
postponed. Trauma responses are contained.

But when relative calm returns, when there is finally a quiet moment, survivors begin reaching out.
