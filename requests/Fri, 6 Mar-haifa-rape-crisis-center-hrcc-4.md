## The Ask

The Haifa Rape Crisis Center (HRCC) is requesting $200,000 for emergency capacity expansion to prepare for a post-operation surge in demand for services from survivors of sexual violence. This funding will support a new volunteer training course, expansion of WhatsApp support hours, and increased immediate response capacity.

**Funding Requested:** $200,000

**Funding Breakdown:**
- New Volunteer Training Course (recruitment, training, supervision): $95,000
- WhatsApp Expansion (extended hours, additional responders, supervision): $60,000
- Surge Capacity Reinforcement (intake, coordination, flexible staffing): $45,000

## Context & Background

The Haifa Rape Crisis Center (HRCC) has consistently responded to major escalations, wars, and national crises over the past four decades. They observe a pattern where during acute military operations, emotional processing is postponed, but a surge in requests for support follows when calm returns. Following previous conflicts, requests typically rise by up to 30% in the weeks after de-escalation. In 2025, HRCC received over 13,000 requests. The current operation, 'Shaagat HaAri,' follows nearly two years of sustained war and cumulative national stress, making the risk of a post-operation surge even greater. This operation interrupts the fragile progress of survivors who were beginning to stabilize. HRCC is preparing for a concentrated and intense post-operation surge by expanding their volunteer training, WhatsApp support hours, and immediate response capacity.

Dear Friends and Partners,

As Operation "Shaagat HaAri” continues and communities across Israel once again live under sirens,
security alerts, and prolonged uncertainty, we are preparing for a dynamic we know well from previous
periods of war.

Research and decades of field experience show that survivors of sexual violence often experience
collective crises as a double trauma. During times of war, many survivors instinctively withdraw, minimize
their own pain, and hesitate to seek help, feeling that their suffering is less legitimate in the face of a
national emergency.

One woman recently wrote to us:

“I'm angry at myself for feeling so broken right now when other people have shrapnel falling on their
homes."

This tendency to shrink inward fueled by shame and self-silencing, often leads to a worsening of
symptoms: increased flashbacks, avoidance of emotional support, neglect of physical and emotional
needs, and declining functioning. In many cases, it also correlates with heightened despair and increased
suicide risk.

Based on what we have seen in previous conflicts, we know that when the immediate emergency
subsides, and people finally have space to process what they have endured, many survivors reach out
simultaneously for support.

We are preparing now for that moment.

At the Haifa Rape Crisis Center (HRCC), we have responded to every major escalation, war, and national
crisis over the past four decades. One pattern remains consistent:

During acute military operations, communities function in survival mode. Emotional processing is
postponed. Trauma responses are contained.

But when relative calm returns, when there is finally a quiet moment, survivors begin reaching out.

Over the past year, many survivors of sexual violence had only just begun to stabilize: returning to

This new operation interrupts that fragile progress.

Repeated exposure to national emergencies compounds trauma. Sirens, confinement, and renewed
insecurity reactivate hypervigilance, panic responses, and loss of control, especially among women
whose nervous systems have already been under prolonged strain.

Recovery is not linear. Repeated disruption can undo months of therapeutic progress.

We believe the post-operation surge may be both concentrated and intense, and we are preparing now
to ensure we can respond without delay.

2. Expansion of WhatsApp Support Hours

In recent years, and especially during periods of security escalation, our WhatsApp helpline has become
a critical lifeline for survivors.

Many women:

• cannot speak aloud while confined to shelters or safe rooms during sirens

• live in environments where privacy is limited

• feel safer initiating contact through written messages rather than phone calls

Even now, we are already seeing how this channel becomes essential under emergency conditions.
While overall call volume has not yet reached its expected peak, a growing number of contacts are
occurring late at night, when survivors wake from alerts, sirens, or are unable to sleep due to anxiety.

Some messages reflect extremely difficult circumstances, including women writing that they are
sheltering in a safe room together with the person who harmed them, or reaching out because
escalating fear and isolation are intensifying suicidal thoughts.

To ensure we can respond to these situations in real time, we plan to:

• Extend WhatsApp operating hours

• Increase the number of trained responders available per shift

3. Expanding Immediate Response Capacity

To ensure we can provide timely and responsible support during the anticipated post-operation surge,
additional funding will allow us to strengthen and expand the critical emergency responses we are
already providing every day.

This includes:

• Expanding response coverage during peak hours, ensuring that survivors who reach out in crisis

are met by a trained responder without delay.

• Maintaining and strengthening accompaniment services, including urgent accompaniment to
police stations, where survivors must often gather the strength to report abuse and provide
critical evidence needed to open a case.

• Continuing long-term accompaniment through criminal proceedings, supporting women who
have waited years for justice and must now face investigations, testimonies, and court processes
that can reopen deep trauma.

• Providing support during National Insurance (Bituach Leumi) committee hearings, many of
which still take place remotely and require survivors to recount their trauma and daily struggles
to secure or extend the benefits that allow them to survive.

• Utilizing our protected Safe Social Space as a resilience-building emergency response, offering
survivors a physically and emotionally safe environment where they can reduce isolation,
stabilize, and reconnect with a supportive community during periods of national stress.

• Strengthening coordination between the hotline, accompaniment teams, and follow-up
services, ensuring continuity of care for women navigating multiple systems simultaneously
during crisis.
