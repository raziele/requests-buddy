## The Ask

Mashiv HaRuach is requesting urgent support to continue and scale its on-site "Charging Stations" for frontline teams operating under prolonged wartime emergency conditions in Israel, and to launch longer-term resilience processes for high-strain populations such as medical and municipal teams and reservists' families. They report severe cumulative fatigue and limited ability for workers to leave duty stations, so the program delivers short, structured stress-reduction and coping sessions directly in the workplace.

**Funding Requested:** 7,600 NIS per day for Charging Stations and 86,000 NIS per group for longer-term processes (approx. $2,300/day and $26,000/group).

**Funding Breakdown:**
- Charging Stations for frontline teams (approx. 80 participants): 7,600 NIS per day (approx. $2,300 USD)
- Longer-term processes for key populations (approx. 25 participants): 86,000 NIS per group (approx. $26,000 USD)

## Context & Background

Mashiv HaRuach states it was established after the outbreak of the Iron Swords war to support "those who support others" during recurring national emergencies. In the current war with Iran, they describe Israel's civilian front as being in prolonged and complex crisis conditions with no clear end, and identify frontline responders as under sustained pressure.

According to the request and attached document, hospital teams are working continuously under emergency conditions, often in underground facilities, while hospitals remain critical 24/7 infrastructure. Staff face layered strain, including physical, emotional, and family burden, after extended periods of intense work. They also report that Home Front Command restrictions and ongoing emergency conditions prevent teams from leaving workplaces for recovery.

To address this, the organization designed "Charging Stations," short on-site group sessions embedded in the workday. The sessions combine movement, facilitated discussion, practical coping tools, grounding exercises, and psychoeducation on secondary trauma, compassion fatigue, disenfranchised grief, second-victim effects, and team functioning under prolonged emergency conditions. The model uses small groups (up to 13) with two facilitators in parallel to provide both group containment and individual support when distress is triggered.

They are seeking immediate funding to continue daily Charging Stations and additional support for deeper longer-term intervention tracks for populations under sustained strain, including medical teams, municipal leaders, and spouses of reservists.

## "Charging Stations" - Immediate On-Site Support for Frontline Teams

The document describes operation during the ongoing war with Iran. It characterizes the Israeli civilian front as deeply immersed in a prolonged, complex, and demanding reality with no clear end in sight.

### Situation described
- Across hospitals nationwide, most teams are working under emergency conditions, often in underground facilities.
- Teams outside protected underground spaces face different but similarly complex challenges while hospitals continue functioning 24/7.
- The cumulative toll is described as physical, emotional, and familial fatigue.
- Many teams are operating beyond normal capacity after a long period of intense work.
- Home Front Command restrictions and ongoing emergency conditions prevent hospital teams from leaving work for rest/recovery.

### Program response
- "Charging Stations" are short, structured pauses delivered within the workday.
- Sessions aim to release accumulated strain and enable meaningful recharge.
- Components include:
  - Movement
  - Facilitated discussion
  - Practical coping tools for individuals and teams
  - Brief psychoeducation

### Psychological topics named
- Ripple effects of trauma
- Secondary trauma
- Disenfranchised grief
- "Second victim" phenomenon
- Compassion fatigue
- Team functioning under prolonged emergency conditions

### Delivery model
- Small groups of up to 13 participants.
- Two facilitators work in parallel.
- Intended outcome: strong group holding plus individual attention, including immediate/tailored support in distress-trigger situations without disrupting the group process.
