## The Ask

JDC is requesting $6.876 million in urgent emergency funding to respond to the March 2026 missile attacks from Iran and Lebanon. The request covers humanitarian relief in five directly hit Israeli cities, welfare support for 10 northern frontline municipalities, housing transition support for up to 300 displaced households (All the Way Home), accessibility measures and shelter kits for people with disabilities and children, emergency replacement of 1,442 medical oxygen cylinders for municipal response teams, and heightened security and mental health support for Jewish communities in Europe. JDC has already deployed rapid response in Beit Shemesh and the North using unrestricted funds and is seeking additional funding to scale and sustain this lifesaving work.

**Funding Requested:** $6,876,000

**Funding Breakdown:**
- Humanitarian Relief for Directly Hit Cities ($200,000 per city x 5 cities): $1,000,000
- Welfare Support for Northern Frontline Municipalities ($150,000 per city x 10 cities): $1,500,000
- All the Way Home (Ad HaBayit) for Families Whose Apartments Were Severely Damaged: $3,300,000
- Increasing Accessibility in Shelters for People with Disabilities and Accessible Shelter Kits for Children: $200,000
- Emergency Replacement of Medical Gas Cylinders: $576,000
- Increased Needs of Global Jewish Communities: $300,000

## Context & Background

Israel was again thrown into war in early March 2026. Over roughly 100 hours, hundreds of missiles and drones were launched from Iran and Lebanon toward Israel, sending millions into safe rooms and bomb shelters. As of March 3, direct hits in Tel Aviv, Beit Shemesh, Jerusalem, and Be'er Sheva caused 12 fatalities, hundreds of injuries, and extensive damage, forcing over 1,500 citizens to evacuate to hotels. JDC has been responding using established government and municipal partnerships and unrestricted funds (including from JUF): in Beit Shemesh it delivered 1,700 pounds of essential supplies to evacuees, provided equipment to municipal emergency teams, set up a flexible humanitarian fund, and deployed community caseworkers; in the North it delivered 160 respite kits to shelters in Nahariya; nationally it is embedded in government coordination and deploying digital mental health and emergency tools.

JDC's funding menu is designed to address the most urgent priorities as the conflict continues. Humanitarian relief and welfare support build on models used since October 7 and during the 2025 missile attacks. The All the Way Home program targets up to 300 vulnerable displaced households (about 100 already identified) who need help navigating bureaucracy and finding housing. Accessibility and shelter kits address gaps for people with disabilities and children in public shelters. A Ministry of Health directive in mid-February 2026 raised safety concerns about existing medical gas cylinders; JDC seeks to replace 1,442 cylinders (300 immediately, the rest within one to three weeks) to maintain lifesaving capacity. A portion of the request also supports Jewish communities in Europe and elsewhere facing heightened security threats and increased demand for mental health and crisis response.

# Responding to Emergency in Israel
## Urgent Funding Needs, March 4, 2026

## Israel Again in the Shadow of War

Over the past few days, Israel was once again thrown into war. In the last 100 hours, hundreds of missiles and drones were launched from Iran – and more recently, Lebanon – to Israel, sending millions of Israelis to safe rooms and bomb shelters at all hours of the day and night. While most were intercepted, as of March 3rd, direct hits in Tel Aviv, Beit Shemesh, Jerusalem, and Be'er Sheva caused 12 fatalities, hundreds of injuries, and extensive damage to homes and infrastructure, forcing over 1,500 citizens to leave their homes and evacuate to hotels.

As this crisis unfolds, JDC is there. Since the first news of the attacks, JDC drew on its established networks of government ministries and local partners to assess emerging needs and explore possible solutions.

JDC has built strong foundations using unrestricted and emergency funding (including before October 7th) that allow rapid, effective response:

- **Digital Tools:** JDC-developed platforms for education and mental health professionals, AI-powered support apps for reservists' spouses, and data dashboards for multi-ministerial round tables.
- **Municipal Partnership:** Work with municipalities (Mashiv Haruach, Northern Initiative, etc.) strengthening community resilience and local capacity.
- **Leveling the Playing Field in Critical Aid:** ALS/BLS/CPR kits and Emergency Medical Units in low-income municipalities.

The following funding menu outlines the most urgent priorities.

## Highlighted Urgent Funding Needs

### 1. Humanitarian Relief for Directly Hit Cities
Support for cities absorbing massive, direct missile damage (e.g., Beit Shemesh, Be'er Sheva).

**$200,000 per city x 5 cities = $1,000,000**

- Immediate humanitarian relief: clothing, essential supplies, toiletries, items for children (with Shinua Hevrati).
- Emergency readiness and medical equipment for municipal teams (tourniquets, bandages, stretchers, lifesaving and rescue kits).
- Flexible humanitarian assistance fund for the municipality.
- Two full-time community caseworkers to reinforce municipal welfare and increase access to rights and services.

### 2. Welfare Support for Northern Frontline Municipalities
Support for low-socio-economic authorities in the North.

**$150,000 per city x 10 cities = $1,500,000**

JDC has received urgent appeals from seven municipalities: Ma'ale Yosef, Shlomi, Kiryat Shmona, Nahariya, Hurfeish, Deir al-Asad, Mevo'ot Hermon. Requested support includes food, household supplies, mattresses, biodegradable and accessible medical equipment, community support personnel, resilience and training packages, activity kits, and emergency relief. JDC is active in 24 locations along the northern confrontation line.

### 3. All the Way Home (Ad HaBayit) Program for Families Whose Apartments Were Severely Damaged

**$3,300,000**

Personalized housing transition support for up to 300 vulnerable households in hard-hit cities. Helps displaced residents access rights, navigate bureaucracy, and find viable housing in cooperation with municipalities and NGOs. 100 families already identified; demand expected to grow. (Context: June war with Iran displaced ~13,000 Israelis, including 4,000 particularly vulnerable.)

### 4. Increasing Accessibility in Shelters for People with Disabilities and Accessible Shelter Kits for Children

**$200,000**

- Municipalities: portable ramps, marked stairs/thresholds, guiding strips, signage; training for staff and volunteers.
- Accessible shelter kits for children: pop-up tents, weighted blankets, floor mats, noise-canceling headphones to reduce distress and support emotional regulation.

### 5. Emergency Replacement of Medical Gas Cylinders

**$576,000**

Ministry of Health (mid-February 2026) directive identified safety concerns with existing cylinders in some kits. JDC will procure new, certified oxygen cylinders: 300 immediately, remaining 1,142 within one to three weeks. Includes centralized collection, phased replacement, and coordination with MOH and national authorities.

### 6. Increased Needs of Global Jewish Communities

**$300,000**

- Netherlands: expanded social work and mental health for vulnerable Jewish students and families; crisis-response for stranded foreigners and local members.
- Turkey, France, Brussels, Tunisia: acute increases in security threats around schools, synagogues, youth movements, and gatherings; aim to absorb rising security costs and reinforce protective infrastructure.
