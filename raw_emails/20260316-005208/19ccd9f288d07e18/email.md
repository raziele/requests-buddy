Dear friends,
I hope you and your loved ones are safe.

In these tense days, when everyone is searching for some sense of stability,
I wanted to share a little of what the past days have looked like for our children and young adults.

S. is a single mother raising three five-year-old girls. They live on the fourth floor of a building with no elevator and no shelter. When the sirens sound, they have to run to the shelter at the nearby school. Getting down and back up the stairs with three small children in the short time available is nearly impossible, and in recent days they have simply moved into the shelter.
S. is also dealing with serious financial strain and a great deal of loneliness. When she needs to prepare something for the girls to eat, she has to ask people in the shelter to watch them for a few minutes. When we reached her, we realized there was almost no food left at home, and we made sure to bring food and basic supplies. The hardest part for her is asking for help. As a child she was removed from her home, so turning to municipal welfare services is something she deeply fears.

Y., another young adult we support, owns a small business and is currently serving in the reserves. Each time he is called up, his business is directly affected. There is no staff and no one who can run it in his absence. The result is a sharp drop in income and real concern about keeping up with basic expenses.

R., another young woman we work with, shared that in recent days she has almost stopped eating. She lives with a chronic illness, and the security situation has made things even harder for her.

L., another young woman, is dealing with a very complex situation at home. Her mother struggles to function and barely works, and she finds herself carrying the responsibility for both the household and herself. Recently she told us that she has had to decide what to buy with the little money she has left - medication or food.

N., a 16-year-old girl for whom we serve as legal guardians, lost her father at a young age. Her mother is seriously ill and lives in a residential facility, unable to care for her children. Her older brother is also facing significant challenges. On regular days she eats at school, but this week we discovered that her refrigerator was completely empty. We were able to provide immediate support so she could buy basic groceries.

Unfortunately, these are not unusual stories. This is the reality for children and young people growing up without a family safety net.

We stay in close contact with them, checking in with them, understanding what they need, and stepping in where help is most urgent. In moments like these, the importance of steady, reliable support becomes even clearer.

May peace return soon for all of us.
Warmly,
Adi
